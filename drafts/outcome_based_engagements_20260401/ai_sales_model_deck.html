The Problem

The AI Efficiency Trap

AI makes teams faster. T&M billing punishes teams for being faster.

The math, simply

A team takes a 12-week engagement at $150,000. Midway through, they deploy AI tooling. They finish in 6 weeks.

Under T&M, they've earned $75,000 for delivering identical scope.

AI efficiency = revenue penalty. That is a perverse incentive.

⚠

The risk isn't one project billing short. It's clients using AI efficiency arguments to systematically reduce headcount across ongoing contracts — the same exposure, just slower.

The Bridge

Output-Based Engagements: The Practical Bridge

Not all value models are created equal. Know the difference before proposing.

Model Unit of Value Who Absorbs Risk When to Use
Fixed Price Defined scope, defined price Vendor absorbs overrun risk Simple, bounded scope — not an evolution of T&M
Output-Based Specific deliverables — APIs deployed, suites maintained, features shipped Vendor absorbs production risk; client absorbs business risk The right starting point. Measurable, attributable, builds trust
Outcome-Based Measurable business results — churn reduction, cost savings, revenue lift Vendor absorbs share of business risk Requires instrumentation + track record. Graduate to this — don't start here
💡

Output-based is the pragmatic on-ramp. Use each engagement to build measurement infrastructure and trust. Graduate to outcome-based when the foundation is in place. Most practitioners recommend this path — do not conflate the two models.

"Most contracts now comprise hybrid pricing structures rather than 100% outcome-based pricing."

— Wipro, on the practical state of transition
Pre-Conditions

What You Need to Commit to Output Levels

Output-based means committing to a production rate — not a scope list. The pre-conditions are about controlling what determines that rate.

What you're actually committing to

Output-based is not "fixed scope, fixed price" — that's fixed fee. Output-based means: we will sustain X level of output, measured by an agreed metric, for a given price. Scope can flex. The throughput commitment doesn't.

Examples: N features per sprint meeting acceptance criteria · M APIs deployed per quarter · a maintained regression suite at a defined coverage level

Non-negotiable pre-conditions
🎯
Full control over team composition — who is on the team and at what level directly determines sustainable throughput. If the client controls staffing decisions, the throughput commitment is not yours to make.
⚙️
Major control over ways of working — AI toolset, sprint cadence, review process, deployment pipeline. These are the levers that set your production rate. A client-dictated process is a client-controlled throughput ceiling.
📋
Named client obligations that affect throughput — timely decisions, environment access, stakeholder availability. If client delays block output, the throughput commitment must be paused, not absorbed.
How scope flex works inside output-based

Scope can change — the rate cannot

The client can reprioritize what the team builds within a period. What they cannot do is change how much the team produces without a commercial adjustment. The throughput commitment is the contract. Scope direction is the client's right within that commitment.

If you can't control these inputs, don't commit to the output
  • Client directs individual tasks and assignments → They control throughput, not you
  • Client mandates team members or roles → Staffing risk is theirs, pricing risk is yours
  • Client dictates tooling or process → Your AI efficiency gains may be blocked entirely
⚠

Control over inputs is the prerequisite. Committing to a throughput level you don't control is not output-based pricing — it's T&M risk with a fixed-fee label.

Critical Infrastructure

Measurement: You Must Agree Before Work Starts

You cannot retroactively define success. If you can't measure it, you can't contract on it.

Story points are the wrong answer
  • They measure effort, not value. A 1-point change unblocking $5M is not a 1-point contract line item.
  • Team-relative by design. A "5" for one team is a "3" for another. No stable exchange rate exists.
  • Gameable under commercial pressure. Teams inflate estimates to demonstrate velocity. Clients have noticed.
  • Untranslatable. No CFO has ever reported "847 story points delivered" to a board.
What must be agreed upfront
📏
Specific, measurable baseline — current state with data to prove it, not an aspirational target
🔭
Attribution methodology — how both parties determine that the outcome shifted because of the work, not external factors
👤
Named business owner with authority over the decisions the outcome depends on
Better measurement units for software delivery

Functional Decomposition

Break into working capabilities with acceptance criteria. "Authentication module in production." Either works or it doesn't. Payment triggers on acceptance.

DORA Metrics (Retainer Anchors)

Deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR. Objective, automatable, impossible to game without changing actual delivery behavior.

Value Stream Milestones

Milestones tied to business capabilities the client can act on — not arbitrary technical checkpoints. Payment triggers on milestone verification.

Outcome Proxies

When direct attribution is hard, agree leading indicators: feature adoption rate, API call volume. Measurable and attributable without requiring full business causation.

New Cost Variable

Tokenomics: The Cost Dimension We're Still Learning

Token economics is becoming a critical part of pricing — and we don't yet have reliable estimation models.

Why tokenomics matters now
  • AI-native delivery consumes tokens at scale. Every LLM call — code gen, review, documentation, testing, planning — has a direct dollar cost attached.
  • Token costs vary wildly by use case. A reasoning-heavy task can cost 10x more than a simple completion on the same model.
  • Model choice dramatically affects cost. GPT-4o vs. Claude Sonnet vs. open-source models can differ by orders of magnitude for equivalent tasks.
  • Token costs must appear in the engagement P&L. If we don't price them, we absorb them — silently — as margin erosion.
⚠

We do not yet have reliable estimation models for token usage. Build contingency into pricing until we do. Track actuals on every AI-native engagement so we can calibrate.

What must go into pricing

Tool license costs

Copilot, Cursor, Claude API, OpenAI, and others. Per-seat or per-token — model carefully based on actual team size and usage patterns.

Inference / API token costs

Estimate by use case category: code generation, code review, documentation, test generation, planning. Apply contingency factor until actuals are available.

Infrastructure overhead

Vector databases, embedding runs, agent orchestration compute. Often overlooked but can be material on agentic workstreams.

📊

Every AI-native engagement should track token costs as a delivery metric — not just as a billing line item. This data becomes our estimation model for future pricing.

Engineering Discipline

Efficient Token Use Is a Competitive Advantage

Token efficiency is not just a cost control measure — it's a delivery discipline that separates AI-native teams from AI-augmented ones.

Why it matters commercially
  • Under output-based pricing, compressed token cost = margin. Every unnecessary call erodes it.
  • Token waste compounds on agentic workstreams — an inefficient prompt architecture can multiply costs 3–5x.
  • Clients will increasingly ask about AI cost structures in enterprise procurement. The team that can explain and demonstrate efficiency wins trust.
Efficiency principles
✂️
Right-size the model to the task. Don't send reasoning-heavy requests to a frontier model when a smaller, faster model solves it adequately.
🔁
Use caching aggressively. Prompt caching on static system prompts, code context, and documentation can reduce costs 60–80% on repeat tasks.
🎯
Scope the context window deliberately. Bloated context = expensive inference. Teach teams to provide focused, relevant context, not full codebases.
Operational practices

Prompt architecture reviews

Treat prompt design as an engineering artifact — review for efficiency the same way you review code for performance. One good prompt template saves thousands of inefficient calls.

Model routing by task type

Define task categories (generation, review, planning, research) and route to the lowest-cost capable model. Document the routing logic — this becomes your team's cost playbook.

Token usage telemetry

Track tokens consumed per output unit from day one. This data informs future pricing and identifies waste patterns. You cannot optimize what you don't measure.

Team Requirements

Don't Do This Without the Right People

An output-based AI-native engagement is a fundamentally different delivery model. It requires different leadership, not just different tools.

Delivery Manager (AI-Native)

Not a traditional PM who manages timesheets and standups. This role must:

  • Understand AI tooling well enough to make staffing and architecture decisions
  • Track output-level commitments, not burn rates — fundamentally different reporting
  • Manage client expectations on delivery model: no hours visibility, output accountability instead
  • Identify when token costs are spiking and why
  • Negotiate scope with clients from a position of domain knowledge

AI Champion (Embedded)

The team's internal AI expert and quality bar. This role must:

  • Drive adoption of AI tooling across all team members — not just power users
  • Design and maintain the team's prompt architecture and AI workflow standards
  • Identify use cases where AI can increase throughput and compress delivery time
  • Monitor output quality from AI-generated artifacts — catch regressions
  • Report on efficiency gains and provide data for future pricing calibration
🚫

Do not commit to output-based delivery without both roles filled. A capable DM without an AI Champion is managing a team that isn't actually AI-native. An AI Champion without a capable DM has no one to translate delivery intelligence into commercial accountability. Both are required — not optional nice-to-haves.

The Method

A New Approach to Pricing: Five Steps

Start with what we know. Adjust for what AI changes. Price the full cost picture. Then choose the commercial structure.

1
Start with standard resource-based pricing
Build the T&M baseline: team size, roles, rate cards, weeks. This is your cost floor and your reference point for all adjustments that follow. Never skip this — you need the anchor.
2
Determine achievable maturity level and constraints
Assess: What AI maturity level is achievable? Which specific AI-assisted or AI-native use cases apply? What tools are available? What are the client/environment constraints (security, data access, process, culture)? Be honest — optimistic maturity assumptions become delivery risk.
3
Adjust resource-based pricing for AI gains, bottlenecks, and constraints
Reduce team size, duration, or role mix based on what AI genuinely enables at the assessed maturity level. Account for bottlenecks (handoffs, review cycles, client dependencies) that AI cannot compress. Don't apply a blanket efficiency multiplier — model it by workstream.
4
Estimate cost of tool licenses and tokens
Add AI tool licenses (per-seat or consumption-based), API token costs by use case category, and infrastructure overhead. Apply a contingency buffer — token estimation is still maturing. Track actuals against estimates to calibrate for future deals.
5
Decide on the commercial structure
Choose the structure that matches measurement maturity, relationship stage, and delivery confidence: milestone payments, subscription retainer, gain-sharing, SLA-linked, or hybrid T&M + outcome bonus. The hybrid is usually the right starting point for new output-based relationships.

Appendix

Choosing the Right Commercial Structure

Match the mechanism to what the engagement actually is — not to what sounds most sophisticated.

Structure Best For Measurement Required Relationship Stage
Hybrid T&M + Outcome Bonus Start Here Most new output-based relationships — protects cash flow, introduces accountability Outcome metrics agreed upfront; base fee covers costs New or transitioning relationships
Milestone Payments Project-shaped work with defined deliverables; accessible entry point Acceptance criteria per milestone; payment on verification New clients; early trust-building
Subscription Retainer Long-running platform or product teams; continuous development DORA metrics or throughput SLAs; quarterly re-scoping Established accounts with delivery track record
SLA-Linked Pricing Managed services, platform operations, high-availability systems Uptime, response time, quality metrics — automated tracking Any stage; requires operational instrumentation
