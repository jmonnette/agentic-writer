Rethinking What We're Selling

EPAM's strength has always been the brilliance and deep expertise of our engineers. That was the product. AI commoditizes that. When any team with the right tools can produce working code at speed, raw engineering talent stops being the differentiator.

The pivot: stop selling expertise and start selling outcomes and accountability. Clients don't actually want engineers — they want results they can report upward. We need to be the vendor who owns getting there, not the one who shows up and executes what they're told.

Once the Conversation Starts

Client Objections You'll Hear — and What to Say

Commercial, stakeholder, and AI-specific objections

⚖️ Legal & Procurement
"Our legal team will never sign a non-standard contract structure."
Response: They will flag it — that's their job. The answer is standard outcome-contract templates: pre-negotiated positions on IP allocation, measurement audit rights, attribution methodology, and outcome definition schedules. EPAM needs outcome-contract templates the way it has T&M templates. If those don't exist yet, that's the business case to bring to practice leadership.
📉 Risk-Conscious Client
"What happens when EPAM misses the outcome target?"
Response: In a well-structured hybrid, EPAM does not receive the outcome bonus — and the client has paid base fees at market rates for work delivered. No clawbacks on base fees. The vendor's downside is "we don't earn the premium." If base fees are at risk on an outcome miss, that is a structuring error, not a feature. Every AM should be able to give this answer before the contract is signed.
💰 CFO / Procurement (Rate Squeeze)
"We can get this done for less if we just negotiate your rates down."
Response: Rate optimization saves unit cost — it does nothing about total cost of ownership: the EM hours spent directing our backlog, rework from insufficient accountability, knowledge loss when engineers rotate. A managed capability model may have a higher rate card and a lower total cost. We'd be happy to model that comparison. Take this conversation to the CTO or CFO — not procurement.
🏢 Engineering Manager (Staff Aug Accounts)
"I need to stay in control of what my team works on."
Response: You keep strategic control — you still define priorities and outcomes. What changes is that you no longer direct the day-to-day execution of our work. We manage ourselves to the outcomes you set. That frees you from 30% of your week that's currently spent directing our backlog.
🔍 Skeptical CTO
"Is EPAM actually set up to manage outcome risk?"
Response: Partially, honestly. We have the delivery tooling (AI/Run), engineering capability, and measurement infrastructure to support outcome commitments. What's still developing: internal governance for gain-sharing contracts and outcome tracking. The DMs already running outcome-oriented sprints — defining success in business result terms, using telemetry to demonstrate progress — are ahead of the curve. That's the operating model we're building toward.
🤖 Procurement (AI Seniority Argument)
"If AI is doing most of the work now, why are we paying senior engineer rates? Surely junior people with AI tools are equivalent."
Response: This gets the causality backwards. AI doesn't reduce the need for seniority — it amplifies it. A junior engineer with AI tools produces faster junior-quality output. A senior engineer with AI tools produces faster senior-quality output, makes better architectural decisions, catches failure modes earlier, and directs the AI toward the right problem. The value of senior engineers was never their typing speed. It was always their judgment. AI hasn't automated judgment. Escalate this conversation to engineering leadership, who live with the consequences of seniority decisions. Procurement does not.

The recommended path: Start with output-based or capacity-based models. Graduate to outcome-based as trust and measurement infrastructure develop. Capacity-based is often the natural evolution from staff augmentation — same team shape, different commercial logic. A client is ready for outcome-based when they can state a measurable baseline, track it, and agree on attribution methodology before work begins.

Tokenomics

Token Economics: The Question Every Deal Will Raise

No universal answer — but leaving it unaddressed is a deal risk.

💳

Who Pays?

This is a commercial question and there is no single right answer. Token costs can be client-absorbed (pass-through), EPAM-absorbed (built into pricing), or shared. The model matters under outcome-based contracts — absorbing token costs on a gain-sharing deal is a margin bet that needs to be modeled explicitly.

Document it in the contract. Do not leave it implicit.
📈

Who's Watching?

Token spend is not static — it scales with usage, model choice, prompt design, and task volume. Without monitoring, costs erode margin silently. Someone needs to own the usage dashboard: tracking consumption against budget, flagging anomalies, and escalating before cost becomes a problem.

Treat token budgets like infrastructure costs — not a surprise at invoice time.
⚙️

EPAM's Governance Responsibility

Regardless of who pays, EPAM needs internal standards for efficient, value-delivering token use. Model selection, prompt engineering, workflow design, and caching strategies can vary costs by 10x for equivalent outputs. Efficiency is a delivery discipline. We need practices for it — and clients will ask to see them.

This is an EPAM maturity gap we need to close.
⚠️

Clients paying for AI tooling — or who suspect they are — will ask. AMs who can't answer the tokenomics question will lose credibility at a critical commercial moment. Have a position before the conversation starts. Bring it up first if the engagement uses AI at scale.

Measurement Problem

Story Points Are Not the Answer

They were designed for internal planning. They fail as a commercial unit.

Four Reasons Story Points Fail Commercially
01
Measure effort, not value
A 1-pt config change unblocking $5M revenue ≠ an 8-pt feature nobody uses.
02
Team-relative by design
A "5" in one team is a "3" in another. No exchange rate. No stable cross-contract answer.
03
Gameable under commercial pressure
Teams inflate estimates to show velocity. Clients have noticed. It erodes trust.
04
No business translation
No CFO has ever reported to a board that their initiative delivered 847 story points.
The Key Insight

There is no perfect commercial unit for complex software delivery.

Precise imperfect units beat vague perfect ones.

The goal is not to find perfection. It is to find something better than hours and points — and several options exist.

🎯

If you propose a commercial engagement structured around story point throughput, you're building on sand. The next slide shows what to use instead.

The Underlying Problem With Point 01

Story points were designed as a measure of relative effort and complexity — not hours. That's the textbook definition. In practice, most teams use them as a disguised hour estimate. But even when used correctly, story points only tell you how hard something is to build — not whether it should be built, or what it's worth. Business value is what clients are paying for. Story points were never designed to bridge that gap — and they can't. We need to sell value, not effort.

Measurement Problem

Five Better Measurement Approaches

Each works for different engagement types. Know when to use which.

Approach How It Works Best For
Functional Decomposition Recommended Define working software capabilities with acceptance criteria. Payment triggers on acceptance — not hours, not points. "Auth module in production." "Payment integration processing transactions." Project delivery; milestone-based contracts; any work with definable "done" states
DORA Metrics For retainers Deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, MTTR — objective, automatable, impossible to game without changing real behavior. Ties retainer value to engineering health. Subscription retainers; team-as-a-service; long-running platform teams
Value Stream Milestones Define milestones that represent genuine business capabilities the client can act on — not technical checkpoints. "API enabling X integration." "BI layer adopted by Y teams." Platform builds; modernization programs; multi-phase delivery
Acceptance-Criteria Delivery Each output defined by what it must do to be accepted, not how long it took to build. The criteria become the contract. Shifts from "how much effort?" to "does this work as agreed?" Managed services; AI-assisted delivery; any repeatable output type
Outcome Proxies When true business outcomes are distal or hard to attribute, agree on leading indicators. "Feature adoption >40% within 30 days." "API volume reaches X by week 8." Measurable and attributable. Transition to outcome-based; early gain-sharing engagements; pre-instrumentation phase

"Invest at the front end defining what success looks like — or spend the same time at the back end arguing about whether you got there. Most clients choose the front end when it's framed that way."

Before the Client Conversation

The Objections That Stop AMs
Before They Say a Word

These aren't client objections. They're the internal fears that prevent the conversation from starting at all.

😟 "Raising this will unsettle a relationship I've spent years building."
This is the most common reason the conversation never happens — and it's usually wrong. Clients who are already asking "what are we getting for this?" are more unsettled by the current model than by a conversation about evolving it. The AM who raises it first is seen as a strategic partner. The AM who waits for the client to raise it is seen as a vendor defending the status quo. The relationship risk runs in both directions. Inaction is not neutral.
😬 "If delivery misses the outcome, I'll be the one who gets blamed."
In a well-structured hybrid deal, EPAM's downside is not earning the outcome bonus — not losing base fees, not losing the account, and not a finger pointed at the AM. The AM's job is to structure the deal correctly and loop in the right support. The accountability for outcome delivery sits with the DM and the delivery team, with the AM as the commercial architect — not the scapegoat. This requires clarity on internal roles, which is a leadership decision (see slide 16).
🤔 "I don't know how to have this conversation without sounding like I'm pitching something."
The conversation doesn't start with a proposal. It starts with a diagnostic question: "We've been working together for X years. As you think about what you're getting from that investment — what would 'this is working really well' actually look like for you?" That question surfaces the client's outcome language, which becomes the basis for the commercial conversation. You're not pitching a model. You're listening for what the client already wants — and offering a structure that delivers it.
💭 "I don't know enough about these models to answer the client's questions."
You don't need to be a commercial architect to open the conversation. You need to know: (1) the difference between output, capacity, and outcome models, (2) which structure fits this engagement, and (3) who to bring in when the client says yes. The first two this deck covers. The third requires deal support infrastructure — which is a leadership decision. Your job is to open the door. You don't have to build the room alone.
