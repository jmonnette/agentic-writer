# Persona Review: EPAM Account Manager
**Deck**: The Hours Are Over — EPAM East BU QBR 2026
**Persona**: Account Manager, 5–12 years IT services, 1–4 existing accounts
**Date**: 2026-04-01

---

## In-Character Review

*Written as the Account Manager giving honest feedback after the QBR.*

---

Okay, so I sat through the whole thing. Here's what I actually think.

The first few slides — fine. I already knew T&M was under pressure. My clients have been asking questions about AI for a year. I don't need a graph to tell me there's margin compression. I'm living it. What I was hoping to get out of this session was something I could actually use. That part is where it got complicated.

The slide that lost me — and I mean the moment I started thinking about my inbox instead of the deck — was slide 3, "The Death of T&M is Inevitable." That title. That is not a conversation I can bring back to my client. It sounds like someone is already writing off the model that literally pays for everything I do every day. I understand the argument. I even agree with parts of it. But when you lead with "inevitable," you are telling me that my current accounts are a liability, and you haven't yet told me how to fix that. I spent the next three slides a little checked out, waiting to see if the support was coming.

The competitive urgency slide did not help. McKinsey, Accenture, Wipro. Great. Those are not my peer AMs. I don't know a single other AM at EPAM who has successfully closed a gain-sharing deal. Where is that person? I want to buy them a coffee. That case study with the e-commerce retailer and the $180K retainer plus 20% gain-share — that one slide told me more than the previous four. I want ten more of those. I want one where the account looks like mine.

The levels model on slide 11 is the best thing in the deck. That one I could actually use. When I look at most of my accounts, they are at Level 1 or Level 2. And the slide literally tells me the goal is not to jump to Level 5 — just move one level. That felt honest. That felt achievable. I wrote that down. That's the one thing I will actually remember from this QBR.

But then here is what the deck never answered for me: *what does "EPAM owns a defined domain" look like in practice on a Monday morning?* I have a client where we staff a data platform team. Six engineers. We effectively run that team. But the client still assigns sprint work. Is that Level 2 or Level 3? How do I have that conversation with my client without them thinking I'm trying to renegotiate them out of control? The deck says "name it" — but naming it is not the same as knowing how to say it.

The objections slide was actually useful, but it addressed the wrong objections. The legal/procurement one and the skeptical CTO one — those are the objections I hear *after* the client is already considering the change. The objection I never see addressed is the one that happens *before* I ever raise the topic: my own internal fear that this conversation will unsettle a relationship I have spent three years building. That is not a client objection. That is a me problem. And nobody on that stage acknowledged it exists.

The deck also never once told me who I call when I actually want to try this. I have a renewal coming up in Q3 with a fintech client. They have started asking the "what are we getting for this" question. That is a real opportunity. But if I go back to my desk today and want to explore a hybrid structure, I don't know who to email. I don't know if there are contract templates ready. I don't know if a practice lead can join my next call. The deck spent a lot of time telling me why I should change and almost no time telling me what the first call I make should be.

The AI/Run slide at the end felt like a product pitch grafted onto a commercial strategy presentation. I understand what it does. But "have it ready as the answer when the measurement question comes" — okay, but I have never demoed AI/Run to a client. Is there a sales motion for that? Who supports me?

When the session ended, someone behind me said "great deck." I nodded. But walking out, I was thinking: *I could actually try the Level 2 to Level 3 move on my fintech account at the Q3 renewal — if I knew who to involve and what the contract actually looks like.*

That's it. That's where I am.

## Structured Gaps Analysis

*Outside the persona voice.*

---

### 1. Disengagement Point

**Slide 3 ("The Death of T&M Is Inevitable")** is the primary friction trigger. The absolute framing — "inevitable," "indefensible" — reads as a verdict on the AM's current accounts before the deck has provided any rescue path. An AM who manages two stable T&M accounts hears this as existential critique, not strategic context. They disengage or go defensive before the practical slides arrive.

**Secondary friction: Slide 4** (competitive urgency, McKinsey/Accenture/Wipro). The AM has no reference point for how those commitments translate to their individual account motion. Competitive urgency arguments land with BU Heads; they read as pressure to the AM.

---

### 2. Practical Usability

**What lands:**
- The five deal examples on slide 8 (especially the hybrid T&M + outcome bonus on the regional bank) are the highest-utility content in the deck. Concrete numbers, real scenarios, named client types.
- The levels model (slide 11) with the explicit "move one level" instruction is actionable. AMs can self-locate and identify one concrete next step.
- The AM Checklist on slide 13 (five diagnostic questions for existing aug accounts) is directly usable on the next client call.

**What stays too abstract:**
- "Start the conversation at QBR level, not staffing level" — repeated twice with no script. What does the AM actually say at the next QBR?
- "Name what EPAM de facto owns" — the concept is correct; the mechanics of the naming conversation are absent.
- Three diagnostic questions on slide 5 are good, but framed as self-assessment. They need a client-facing version.

---

### 3. The Objections Slide (Slide 14)

The five objections addressed are **client-side objections after a conversation has started**. They do not address:

- The AM's pre-conversation fear: *"Raising this will signal I'm unhappy with current terms and unsettle the client."*
- The accountability gap question: *"If delivery misses the outcome, who gets blamed?"* The deck addresses this for the hybrid model in the objection card, but never explicitly names the AM's personal risk as distinct from the contract risk.
- The quota/comp question: *"What happens to my quota credit if the outcome bonus doesn't pay out?"* This is a real internal objection that affects whether AMs will try this at all. It is not addressed anywhere in the deck.

---

### 4. Asymmetric Framing (Upside Without Loss Acknowledgment)

The deck is almost entirely framed around upside: earn more, differentiate, capture gain-share, become a strategic partner. It does not acknowledge:

- Why T&M made sense and was actively chosen by clients — and still makes sense in many situations (slide 4 gestures at this but buries it)
- What the AM legitimately risks during a transition: relationship disruption, client confusion, a failed pilot that damages trust
- The real operational cost of running a hybrid engagement vs. a staff aug account that "runs itself"

The absence of loss acknowledgment reads as leadership being sold on the strategy and expecting the AM to get on board. That increases defensive resistance rather than reducing it.

---

### 5. What Would Drive Action vs. "This Is for Someone Else"

**Toward action:**
- A named internal contact — deal desk, practice lead, or commercial architect — the AM can call when an opportunity surfaces
- One slide confirming contract templates exist (even if in development) and where to get them
- A peer AM success story, ideally from inside the BU, not a generic case study
- Explicit protection language: "Your base revenue is not at risk during a hybrid pilot. The T&M floor is the floor."

**Toward "this is for someone else":**
- No peer social proof from inside EPAM
- No support structure named — who do I call?
- Objections slide addresses client concerns but not AM internal concerns
- The comp/quota question is completely absent
- The gap between "name the domain" and "here is a script for the naming conversation" is never bridged

---

### 6. What They'd Say to Their Manager

> "The levels model was actually useful — I think I can move my fintech account from Level 2 to Level 3 at the Q3 renewal. But I need to know who to bring in from our side before I raise it with the client. Who's the deal desk contact? And what does the contract actually look like?"

The willingness to act is there. The organizational scaffolding to enable the action is not in the deck.

---

### Priority Recommendations

| Gap | Severity | Fix |
|-----|----------|-----|
| No internal support contact named | High | Add one slide or call-out: "Your deal desk / practice lead contact is ___. Here's when to loop them in." |
| Comp/quota question unaddressed | High | One sentence in the objections slide or a dedicated FAQ addendum |
| No peer AM success story from inside EPAM | High | Replace one external case study with an internal BU example, even anonymized |
| AM's personal pre-conversation fear not acknowledged | Medium | Add to objections slide: "I don't want to unsettle a relationship I've spent years building" |
| "Name the domain" has no accompanying conversation script | Medium | A 3-sentence talking track would close this gap |
| Loss acknowledgment absent | Medium | One paragraph recognizing why T&M was the right model and what the AM is legitimately protecting |
| Slide 3 title framing | Low | Soften "inevitable" to reduce pre-emptive defensiveness before the practical slides land |
