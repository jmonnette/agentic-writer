# The Hours Are Over: How to Sell and Deliver Outcomes in an AI-Accelerated World

Your team delivers a 12-week project in 6. Do you celebrate — or do you quietly panic about the revenue shortfall? A client asks you to prove the ROI of their investment. You open a timesheet. Is that what they're looking for? A competitor just proposed a gain-sharing model on the same opportunity you pitched as T&M. Who wins the deal?

These aren't hypothetical provocations. They're the real conversations happening in client meetings right now — and most of us aren't ready for them.

The time-and-materials model has served this industry well for decades. It is familiar, simple to administer, and requires almost no courage to sell. But AI has exposed its structural flaw in a way that can no longer be papered over: **T&M rewards inefficiency and punishes the teams that actually invest in getting better.** The faster our engineers become — and AI is making them meaningfully faster — the less revenue we earn under the model we've built our business around. That is a perverse incentive, and every account manager and delivery manager at EPAM needs to understand it clearly.

This is a practical guide to doing something about it.

---

## When Efficiency Becomes Your Enemy

Here is the math in its simplest form. A delivery team takes on a 12-week engagement at $150,000. Midway through the project, they deploy AI-assisted development tooling. They finish in 6 weeks. Under T&M, they've just earned $75,000 for delivering identical scope. The client got what they wanted. The team got half the budget.

That is not a hypothetical. That is the structural problem that AI has introduced into every project-scoped T&M contract in our portfolio.

The billable hour was always a flawed mechanism — it discouraged the very efficiency clients claimed to want. A consultant who takes 10 hours bills more than one who takes 2 hours to produce the same output. When human effort was the primary input, clients accepted this as the cost of doing business. When AI can compress delivery cycles by 60–80% on specific workstreams, that tolerance evaporates.

The perverse incentive is clearest in project-scoped engagements. But it does not stop there. In staff augmentation and ongoing platform work — where scope is not capped and teams simply redirect freed capacity to new tasks — the immediate revenue math looks safer. A team that finishes features faster adds more features. Hours stay flat. Revenue appears stable. The problem is that clients have noticed this dynamic and are using it against us. TCS, Infosys, and Wipro are facing selective renegotiations as enterprise clients use AI-driven efficiency arguments to demand lower rates or fewer full-time equivalents on existing augmentation contracts. The risk in staff augmentation T&M is not a single project billing short — it is clients systematically reducing headcount on ongoing contracts as AI makes each FTE more productive. The revenue compression is slower but the structural exposure is identical.

Clients are acting on this across the board. The *Wall Street Journal* noted in December 2025 that AI is rendering the traditional billable hour obsolete across professional services — not just in law and consulting, but in IT engineering delivery. CIOs entered 2026 with what analysts are calling "budget exhaustion" after years of AI pilots with limited results. They are no longer willing to fund open-ended T&M spend without ROI accountability attached.

EPAM generates 70–80% of its revenue through T&M billing. That is the baseline we are defending — and it is not a defensible position for the long term. Our 2026 strategic imperative is converting AI-related demand into tangible, higher-margin engagements. That ambition is not possible at scale under an hourly billing model.

The answer is not to panic. The answer is to learn a different commercial language.

## Output, Outcome, and Fixed-Price Are Not the Same Thing

Before we discuss how to sell value-based engagements, we need to be precise about what we're selling. Most practitioners conflate three distinct models, and that confusion produces bad contracts.

**Fixed-price** is familiar: defined scope, defined price, vendor absorbs overrun risk. The trap is well-known — lowball to win, then fight on change orders when scope drifts. Fixed-price is not an evolution of T&M; it's just a different way to bill for inputs.

**Output-based** is different. Payment is tied to production of specific deliverables — not hours worked, not business results achieved. Payment per artifact: per API deployed, per automated regression suite maintained, per support ticket resolved, per microservice shipped to production. As Infosys BPM frames it: "charging based on number of tickets fixed rather than hours invested." The vendor absorbs production risk — how efficiently they build — but the client still absorbs whether the outputs actually work.

**Outcome-based** goes further. Payment is tied to measurable business results that the client achieves because of the work. A fee linked to percentage reduction in application downtime. Gain-sharing on cost savings from process automation. A revenue-linked fee on a commerce platform that actually converts better. The vendor accepts some share of business risk — not just delivery risk.

The distinction matters enormously in practice:

| | Output-Based | Outcome-Based |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit of value** | Deliverable produced | Business result achieved |
| **Example** | Data pipeline deployed | 15% reduction in customer churn |
| **Vendor controls** | Directly | Partially |
| **Measurement** | Relatively simple | Requires instrumentation |

Consider two ways to scope the same engagement. *Output*: We will build a real-time personalization engine. Deliverable: a working system. Payment: upon deployment. *Outcome*: We will help you reduce customer churn by 15% through a personalization engine. Payment: a base fee upon deployment, plus a gain-sharing bonus for each point of churn reduction below baseline.

The personalization engine in scenario two could work perfectly — and churn might still not drop, because the client's pricing is uncompetitive or a market shock intervened. That is the vendor absorbing business risk beyond their control. It is why most experienced practitioners recommend starting with output-based models and graduating to outcome-based as trust and measurement infrastructure develop.

**A client is ready to graduate from output-based to outcome-based contracting when they can demonstrate:**

1. They can state a specific, measurable baseline — current churn rate, deployment frequency, processing cost per transaction — not an aspirational target.
2. They have a data system that already tracks that baseline consistently, without requiring the vendor to build the instrumentation first.
3. They have agreed in principle on an attribution methodology — how will both parties determine that the outcome shifted because of the work, not because of external factors?
4. There is a named business owner with authority over the decisions (pricing changes, process adoption, organizational changes) the outcome depends on.
5. There is an established working relationship — at minimum one prior engagement — that has given both parties confidence in the other's execution and good faith.

If a client cannot meet the first three criteria, output-based is the right model. Use the engagement to build the instrumentation and the trust. Graduate when the foundation is in place.

Not output-based and outcome-based interchangeably. They require different contracts, different governance, and different risk models. Treat them as distinct.

## Five Ways to Price a Project Without Billing the Hour

There is no single outcome-based contract structure. There are five proven mechanisms — and the right choice depends on the engagement type, the client's measurement maturity, and how much delivery risk you're prepared to absorb. Here is a practical guide to each.

**Milestone payments** are the most accessible entry point. Break the project into phases; release payments upon verification of defined deliverables at each checkpoint. A four-phase structure might look like: 20% on signed architecture, 30% on working prototype, 30% on UAT sign-off, 20% on production deployment. This aligns payment with delivery risk and eliminates the "paying for effort that doesn't produce anything" problem that plagues T&M in discovery-heavy projects. It is output-based, not outcome-based — but it is a meaningful improvement over hourly billing.

**Gain-sharing** is the purest outcome-based structure. The vendor receives a base fee plus a percentage of quantified savings or revenue gains attributable to their work. The Wipro–BT engagement is the archetype: a predominantly T&M base contract with progressive gain-share introduction triggered by headcount reduction, process efficiency improvement, and lead cycle time reduction. Typical splits run 20–40% of documented gains to the vendor for a defined period — usually 12–24 months post-deployment. The critical requirement: a contractually agreed baseline measurement methodology defined before work begins. Without that, attribution disputes are inevitable.

**Subscription retainer** (Capacity-as-a-Service) works well for long-running platform teams and continuous product development. A fixed monthly fee for a defined delivery capability — squad composition, throughput SLAs, quality metrics — with flexible scope within the retainer boundary. No per-hour policing. No change order theater. Predictable cash flow for the vendor; flexible scope for the client.

**SLA-linked pricing** is the natural model for managed services and operations. Base fee tied to availability, performance, or quality metrics. Deductions for SLA breach; bonuses for sustained outperformance. Example: a 99.9% uptime SLA with a 2% fee reduction per 0.1% below target and a 5% bonus for sustained 99.95% over six months. The key, as CIO Magazine frames it, is ensuring that the delivered outcome creates "incremental business value for the customer" — not just maintaining the status quo.

**Per-unit output pricing** works for high-volume, repeatable workstreams where individual outputs are discrete and automatable. Price per production-grade microservice deployed, per automated test suite maintained, per claim processed, per code review completed. Accenture's model in insurance processing — "$10 per claim processed" rather than per consulting hour — is the canonical example at scale. This model benefits most directly from AI automation: if AI compresses the per-unit production cost, the margin on each unit expands. It is worth noting that per-unit pricing is most mature at large BPO and managed services firms; for EPAM, it is an aspirational direction most applicable to high-volume managed services and AI operations work, not a default for project delivery.

The hybrid structure — T&M-equivalent base covering core delivery costs, with outcome-linked bonuses layered on top — is the pragmatic on-ramp for most existing client relationships. It protects cash flow, limits vendor risk exposure during the trust-building phase, and introduces clients to outcome accountability without asking them to abandon a commercial model they know. Wipro is direct about this reality: "Most contracts now comprise hybrid pricing structures rather than 100% gain share/outcome-based pricing." Start there. Graduate clients to purer outcome models as measurement infrastructure matures.

**Choosing the right structure.** The decision is not arbitrary — it follows from three engagement characteristics. First, measurement maturity: if the client can already state a baseline and track it, gain-sharing or SLA-linked structures are viable; if they can't, milestone or subscription models are the appropriate starting point. Second, relationship stage: new clients and first engagements belong in milestone structures where trust is built incrementally; established accounts with a track record of delivery and data quality are candidates for gain-sharing. Third, delivery confidence: per-unit and outcome-based structures require high confidence in delivery predictability; if scope is exploratory or requirements are actively shifting, T&M or a subscription retainer is more honest about the actual risk profile. Match the pricing mechanism to what the engagement actually is — not to what sounds most sophisticated.

## AI Isn't Just Compressing Delivery — It's Enabling the Commercial Model That Should Replace T&M

Here is the virtuous circle that makes outcome-based models more viable in 2026 than they were in 2019 — and more viable next year than they are today.

AI-assisted scoping tools analyze historical delivery data to produce more accurate effort and timeline estimates. Better estimation means lower proposal risk — the "unknown unknowns" that historically made fixed-price and outcome-based contracts feel dangerous become more manageable. Delivery managers can make outcome commitments with higher confidence because they have empirical data, not intuition, behind their estimates.

AI automation compresses the cost of producing an output. Under T&M, that efficiency is transferred to the client automatically — they pay less because the team works fewer hours. Under output-based pricing, compressed delivery cost becomes margin. This is the mechanism by which outcome-based models benefit from AI investment: the efficiency the team builds into delivery shows up as profit, not as discounted invoices.

Faster delivery cycles mean outcomes land earlier in the engagement. The period over which outcome risk is carried shrinks. A personalization engine that would have taken six months to deploy now deploys in ten weeks — which means the gain-sharing measurement period starts sooner and the vendor's outcome exposure is time-compressed.

The measurement problem — which has historically been the hardest obstacle to outcome-based contracts — is now directly addressable. This is not a generic analytics platform; it is EPAM's proprietary answer to the question every client asks before signing an outcome-based contract: "How will we know?" When a client raises that question — and they will — AI/Run is the answer. Have it ready before the commercial conversation begins; it is not the hook, but it is the proof point that makes the commitment credible.

This virtuous circle — AI efficiency lowering delivery cost, measurement infrastructure enabling outcome attribution, compressed cycles reducing exposure — is what makes 2026 the right moment to push this transition, not a future moment when the model is more mature. ## The Objections Are Real. So Are the Mitigations.

The risks of outcome-based engagements are genuine. Dismissing them does not help account managers or delivery managers have better conversations. Here is an honest accounting of the real risks — and the contract mechanics that address each one.

**Attribution disputes.** Business outcomes are influenced by factors beyond the vendor's work. A churn reduction could reflect a competitor's exit, a pricing adjustment, or a macroeconomic shift — not the personalization engine. Mitigation: define a contractually agreed baseline measurement period before work begins; use control groups or A/B architectures where possible; include "external event" clauses that neutralize attribution if market conditions shift dramatically. Federal IT contracting guidance — from an unlikely source for commercial lessons — frames it well: "There's transparency and data to help identify where things went sideways, because everyone is aligned on the same deliverable."

**Scope creep.** Because the vendor is accountable for outcomes, clients sometimes interpret this as an unlimited obligation to add requirements until the outcome is achieved. The contract language that prevents this is specific: input scope is what the vendor commits to build; outcome target is the business result both parties are working toward. Those are two separate schedules in the contract, not one. A concrete example: Input scope — we will build and deploy three ML models to specification. Outcome target — 15% reduction in claim processing time. If the models are deployed and perform to spec but the client has not updated their intake workflow, the outcome target condition has not been met — and additional scope is a change order, not an obligation. The outcome target is the destination; the input scope is the agreed road. Defining them separately prevents the client from treating the destination as a blank check on the road.

**Measurement infrastructure gaps.** Many clients lack the telemetry, dashboards, or data systems needed to measure the outcomes they've contracted for. Mitigation: build measurement infrastructure as a contractual deliverable — an output-based phase — before the outcome-linked phase begins. EPAM AI/Run addresses this directly for engineering and AI delivery work. For engagements where client-side business metrics are the outcomes, scope the instrumentation work explicitly and price it separately.

**Client co-responsibility failures.** Outcomes often require client behavior: data quality, stakeholder availability, process adoption, organizational change. If the client doesn't perform, the vendor misses outcomes they don't fully control. Mitigation: define explicit client obligations in the contract — not as a liability clause, but as a shared accountability framework. Include condition precedents: outcome payments are only due if named client obligations were met. This is not adversarial; it is honest about how outcomes actually get produced.

**Cash flow timing.** Outcome payments arrive later than T&M invoices. Back-loaded gain-sharing creates cash flow stress, particularly on longer engagements. Structure base fees at T&M-equivalent levels to cover costs and provide a modest working margin; treat outcome bonuses as incremental upside, not as the primary revenue stream until the model is proven within an account. The risk/reward framing matters here: in a well-structured hybrid deal, the base fee is the floor and the outcome bonus is the premium. That means the downside is "we earn market rates" and the upside is "we earn meaningfully above market." That framing is not frightening to an AM — it is a value proposition.

**Three counterarguments your clients and legal teams will raise — and what to say back.**

*"Our legal and procurement teams will never sign this."* They will flag it. That is their job. Non-standard contract structures require legal partnership — IP allocation, measurement audit rights, attribution methodology, outcome definition schedules. The answer is not to avoid outcome-based contracts; it is to build standard contract templates that anticipate these questions. EPAM's legal and commercial teams need outcome-contract templates the way they have T&M templates: pre-negotiated positions on the common disputes, so AMs are not improvising in front of a procurement team. If your account doesn't have those templates yet, that is the business case to bring to your practice leadership.

*"What happens when EPAM misses the outcome target?"* The honest answer: in a well-structured hybrid deal, EPAM does not receive the outcome bonus — and the client has paid base fees at market rates for the work delivered. There is no clawback of base fees on work that was scoped, delivered, and accepted. That is the risk structure. The vendor's downside is "we don't earn the premium." The client's protection is "we paid for work, not promises." This is the answer every AM should be able to give before the contract is signed. If the contract has been structured to put base fees at risk on an outcome miss, that is a structuring error — not a feature.

*"Is EPAM actually set up internally to manage outcome risk?"* This is the most important question — and the most honest answer today is: partially. EPAM has the delivery tooling (AI/Run), the engineering capability, and the measurement infrastructure to support outcome commitments. What is still developing is the internal governance: who owns outcome tracking on a gain-sharing contract, who decides when an external event clause is triggered, how delivery managers report against outcome targets rather than burn rates. Delivery managers who are already running outcome-oriented sprints — defining success in business result terms, using telemetry to demonstrate progress — are ahead of the curve. Those who aren't need to start there before the commercial model can be credibly proposed externally. The internal operating model must match the external commercial model. That is not a future state; it is the prerequisite.

These are design problems, not reasons to retreat to T&M. Every one of them is solvable with careful contract architecture. The firms that have figured this out — McKinsey, Accenture, Wipro — are not taking uncalculated risks. They are taking deliberate, instrumented risks. That is what we need to start doing.

## The Language Shift: From "How Many People?" to "What Does Success Look Like?"

Everything above is context. This section is what you do on your next client call.

The opening question that changes the conversation is not "How many engineers do you need?" It is: "What business problem are you actually trying to solve — and how will you know when it's solved?"

That question sounds simple. It is actually a diagnostic that separates clients who are ready for outcome-based engagements from clients who need more runway. A client who can answer it precisely — "We need to reduce our software deployment lead time from three weeks to three days, and we'll know we've done it when our change failure rate stays below 2%" — is a client ready to contract on outcomes. A client who responds with "We just need some developers" is telling you they haven't connected the work to the results yet. Your job is to help them make that connection before the contract is signed, not after.

Move from inputs to baselines. Before proposing any outcome-linked structure, establish: what does the current state look like? What is the baseline measurement? Who controls the data? This is not diligence theater — it is the foundation on which outcome-based contracts are built. A client who cannot state their current churn rate cannot buy a churn-reduction engagement. Baseline definition is where the outcome conversation becomes real.

When proposing the commercial structure, lead with the hybrid model. "Here is our base engagement — it covers delivery costs and is structured around milestone deliverables. Separately, here is a gain-sharing layer tied to the business outcomes we're both trying to achieve. If we hit the outcomes, we both win. If we don't, you've paid for the work at market rates." That framing is not risky. It is credible. It signals that we believe in our delivery capability — and it directly addresses the trust deficit that many clients carry after years of AI investments that did not move the business.

One organizational reality deserves direct acknowledgment: most AMs are currently compensated on hours sold or revenue recognized per billing period. That structure is not neutral — it actively works against the behavior this article is asking for. An AM who proposes a gain-sharing model on an opportunity that would have closed as T&M is taking a real personal risk if their quota is measured in T&M revenue. This is not a behavior problem. It is a structural problem. Leadership needs to align incentives — quota structures, compensation plans, recognition mechanisms — before asking individual AMs to absorb that risk on their own. If you are an AM who sees the opportunity and wants to move in this direction, document the win, show the margin, and make the case upward. The organizational change has to be led from the top.

For delivery managers: the internal operating model must match the external commercial model. A delivery manager who manages their team to hours and timesheets internally will struggle to credibly propose outcome accountability externally. If you are already running outcome-based sprints, defining success in terms of business results rather than story points, and using delivery telemetry to demonstrate progress — you have already built the practice that makes outcome-based selling authentic. If you haven't, that is where to start.

This is not a pricing experiment. It is a structural realignment of how we define value — and the clients who want partners willing to bet on their own delivery will find us or they will find someone else.

## The Question Isn't Whether This Model Works — It's Whether You'll Master It Before Your Competition Does

The T&M model is not disappearing. For exploratory discovery, for rapidly shifting requirements, for early-stage proof-of-concept work — hourly billing remains appropriate. The argument isn't that T&M is always wrong. It's that it should be the exception in an AI-augmented delivery world, not the default we reach for because it requires no courage to propose.

McKinsey has accepted this. Accenture is building its GenAI revenue on it. Wipro has declared a public target. And EPAM's own 2026 strategic priorities require converting AI delivery capability into higher-margin engagements — which cannot happen as long as our commercial model systematically gives away the value AI creates.

The account managers and delivery managers who learn this model — who can define baselines, structure outcome contracts, propose hybrid gain-sharing, and instrument delivery with AI/Run — are the ones who will win the deals that the rest of the market is still pitching at hourly rates. The clients looking for that level of partnership exist. They are not a small segment. **43% of enterprise buyers actively prefer vendors willing to share risk through pricing** (PwC). They are waiting for someone to walk in and propose it credibly.

Ask yourself, on your next qualified opportunity: Have you asked the client what success looks like in business terms? Have you defined the baseline? Have you explored a milestone or gain-sharing structure alongside the T&M proposal?

If the answer is no to all three — you've left value on the table. Both yours, and theirs.

The hours are over. The question is what we replace them with.
