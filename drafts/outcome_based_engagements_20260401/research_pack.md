## Executive Summary

The time-and-materials model is structurally broken in the AI era — not because clients have always hated it, but because AI removes the economic justification for it. When AI compresses a 10-hour task to 2 hours, a T&M model punishes the firm that invested in AI efficiency: it earns less. This perverse incentive is forcing a reckoning across all of professional services, and IT services firms are at the epicenter.

The transition to output- and outcome-based models is no longer theoretical. McKinsey now generates ~25% of its global fees from outcome-based contracts. Accenture is booking billions in AI-linked engagements explicitly tied to client results. EPAM Systems itself is actively transitioning from T&M toward outcome-based and fixed-price engagements as its AI-driven delivery model matures — yet the majority of revenue still flows through hourly billing.

The core challenge isn't willingness — it's capability. Account managers and delivery managers trained in T&M thinking lack the vocabulary, tooling, and confidence to define, measure, price, and defend outcome-based engagements. This article addresses that gap: what the models are, how leading firms are selling and structuring them, what the real risks are, and how AI itself becomes the enabler of more precise outcome delivery.

## Key Findings

### 1. Why T&M Is Structurally Untenable in the AI Era

#### The Core Paradox
The billable hour model contains a fatal flaw that AI has finally made visible: it rewards inefficiency and penalizes productivity. A consultant who takes 10 hours bills more than one who takes 2 hours to produce the same output. When human effort was the primary input, this was a tolerable friction. When AI compresses delivery cycles by 60–80%, it becomes a structural misalignment that clients are no longer willing to accept.

#### Evidence
- **AI compresses delivery cycles, but T&M firms earn less for it.** As one analysis notes: "As AI compresses delivery cycles, time no longer justifies price, accelerating margin pressure and commoditization." (h-in-q.com, 2025)
- **The billable hour discouraged efficiency.** "Consulting professionals might inflate hours worked to increase billables or dwell on lengthy processes rather than finding innovative and quicker solutions." (galton.ai)
- **Clients are repricing existing T&M deals.** TCS, Infosys, and Wipro are facing "selective renegotiations" as clients use AI-driven efficiency arguments to demand lower rates or fewer FTEs on T&M contracts. *Financial Express*, 2025.
- **CIOs entered a "budget exhaustion" phase in late 2025**, reducing appetite for open-ended T&M spend and demanding ROI accountability on AI projects.
- **The Wall Street Journal noted in December 2025** that AI is rendering the traditional billable hour obsolete across professional services.
- **TSIA's framing**: "If you try to modernize AI offerings without modernized pricing, every other transformation effort stalls." (tsia.com)
- **Futurice** (2026): "Most consultancies still track performance using the same core metrics: utilization, average billing rate, project margin. These have worked well in a model built around hours and headcount." But they are now actively misaligned with value delivery.

#### The Revenue Paradox for IT Services Firms
EPAM Systems, which generates 70–80% of revenue from T&M billing, faces a direct threat: the faster AI makes its engineers, the less revenue it earns under its current model. The only escape is to reprice based on value delivered, not hours spent.

### 2. The Engagement Model Spectrum

#### Time-and-Materials (T&M)
- **Definition**: Client pays for hours worked at agreed rates (blended or role-based). Scope can flex.
- **Risk profile**: Low delivery risk for vendor; high spend risk for client.
- **When it makes sense**: Exploratory work, early-stage discovery, rapidly-changing requirements.
- **AI-era problem**: Client absorbs all efficiency gains; vendor has no incentive to invest in productivity tooling.

#### Fixed-Price
- **Definition**: Defined scope, defined price. Vendor absorbs overrun risk.
- **Risk profile**: High delivery risk for vendor; budget certainty for client.
- **When it makes sense**: Well-defined, stable scope with known effort.
- **AI-era problem**: Cuts corners risk; disputes when scope drifts; vendor may lowball to win, then fight on change orders.
- Wednesday.is: "Fixed Price contracts shift the risk predominantly to the service provider. They must deliver the agreed scope within the fixed budget, which can incentivize efficiency but also lead to cutting corners."

#### Output-Based
- **Definition**: Payment tied to production of specific deliverables — not hours, not results. Payment per artifact: per API built, per module delivered, per ticket resolved, per story point completed.
- **Risk profile**: Vendor absorbs effort-per-unit risk; client absorbs "does it work?" risk.
- **IT examples**:
  - Price per resolved support ticket
  - Price per deployed microservice
  - Price per test case automated
  - Price per code review completed
- **Key distinction from outcome-based**: Output is what you made; outcome is what changed because you made it.
- From Infosys BPM: "In a technology outsourcing engagement, charging based on number of tickets fixed rather than hours invested is a good example of output-based pricing."

#### Outcome-Based
- **Definition**: Payment tied to measurable business results achieved by the client. The vendor's fee is linked to whether the client actually got better.
- **Risk profile**: Highest delivery risk for vendor; highest value alignment for client.
- **IT examples**:
  - Fee tied to % reduction in application downtime
  - Gain-sharing on cost savings from process automation
  - Revenue-linked fee on e-commerce platform improvements
  - Fee tied to lead conversion rate improvement from marketing tech build
  - Subscription fee + bonus tied to SLA achievement
- From DigitalRoute: "An outcome-based service shifts the focus towards results. Instead of measuring success based on effort or outputs, it places emphasis on the value delivered."

#### Hybrid Models (Most Common in Practice)
Most real-world advanced engagements use a hybrid structure: a T&M or fixed-price base covering core delivery, plus outcome-linked bonuses or gain-sharing layered on top. Wipro: "Most contracts now comprise hybrid pricing structures rather than 100% gain share/outcome-based pricing."

### 3. Output vs. Outcome: The Practical Distinction for IT Services

This is the most important conceptual distinction in the article — and the one most often confused.

| Dimension | Output-Based | Outcome-Based |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Unit of value** | Deliverable produced | Business result achieved |
| **Example metric** | # of features shipped | % increase in conversion rate |
| **Vendor controls** | Directly | Partially (shared with client behavior) |
| **Measurement** | Relatively simple | Requires instrumentation + attribution |
| **Risk for vendor** | Production risk | Market/adoption/business risk |
| **Client motivation** | Cleaner than T&M | Fully aligned with business goals |

**Practical IT examples to make the distinction concrete:**

*Output*: We will build a data pipeline that ingests customer behavior events in real-time. Deliverable: a working pipeline. Payment: upon deployment.

*Outcome*: We will help you reduce customer churn by 15% through a personalization engine. Payment: a base fee upon deployment + a gain-sharing bonus for every point of churn reduction below baseline.

The critical nuance: outcome-based contracts require the vendor to accept some business risk that is not entirely within their control. A personalization engine may work perfectly and churn may still not drop — because of pricing, competitor moves, or macroeconomic factors. This is why most mature practitioners recommend starting with output-based models and graduating to outcome-based as trust and instrumentation develop.

### 5. Proven Pricing Mechanisms

#### Milestone-Based Payments
- **How it works**: Project broken into phases; payments released upon completion of verified milestones.
- **Best for**: Output-based engagements with clear deliverable checkpoints.
- **Example**: 20% upon signed architecture; 30% upon working prototype; 30% upon UAT sign-off; 20% upon production deployment.
- **Advantage over T&M**: Aligns payment with delivery risk; client only pays for completed work.
- **Advantage over fixed-price**: Less exposure to scope disputes if milestones are defined around outputs, not features.

#### Gain-Sharing
- **How it works**: Vendor receives a base fee + a percentage of quantified savings or revenue gains attributable to their work.
- **Best for**: Automation, cost-reduction, and efficiency programs where baseline is measurable.
- **Example**: Wipro-BT: gain-sharing triggered by headcount reduction and lead cycle time improvement.
- **Typical split**: 20–40% of documented gains to vendor, for a defined period (typically 12–24 months post-deployment).
- **Risk**: Attribution disputes ("would savings have happened anyway?"). Requires contractually agreed baseline measurement methodology.

#### Subscription / Retainer (Capacity-as-a-Service)
- **How it works**: Fixed monthly fee for access to a defined team capacity or service capability. Scope is flexible within the retainer.
- **Best for**: Ongoing platform engineering, managed AI operations, continuous product development.
- **Example**: $X/month for a squad of 6 engineers + 1 architect + 1 product owner — with defined SLAs on throughput and quality metrics.
- **Advantage**: Predictable cash flow for vendor; flexible scope for client; no per-hour policing.

#### SLA-Linked Pricing (with Penalties and Bonuses)
- **How it works**: Base fee tied to availability, performance, or quality metrics. Deductions for SLA breach; bonuses for exceeding targets.
- **Best for**: Managed services, operations, support engagements.
- **Example**: 99.9% uptime SLA; 2% fee reduction per 0.1% downtime below SLA; 5% bonus for sustained 99.95%+ over 6 months.
- **From CIO**: "Bonus payments are made to the vendor for achieving specific performance levels above the contract's SLAs... the key is to ensure that the delivered outcome creates incremental business value for the customer."

#### Per-Unit / Per-Transaction Pricing
- **How it works**: Price per discrete output unit — claim processed, ticket resolved, test automated, code review completed.
- **Best for**: High-volume, repeatable workstreams with automatable outputs.
- **Example**: $X per production-grade microservice deployed; $Y per automated regression test suite maintained.
- **Accenture example**: "$10 per claim processed" rather than per hour.

#### Progressive / Hybrid Models
- **How it works**: Start with T&M or fixed-price base; introduce gain-sharing or outcome bonuses progressively as trust and measurement infrastructure develop.
- **Best for**: Long-term partnerships where measurement systems need to be built before pure outcome pricing is viable.
- **From Wipro**: "Most contracts now comprise hybrid pricing structures rather than 100% gain share/outcome-based pricing."
- **From SoftwareOne**: "A viable approach is to structure your agreements in a hybrid model. Base part of the scope on standard pricing, and some of it on the success/outcome model."

### 6. Real Risks and How Leading Firms Mitigate Them

#### Risk 1: Outcome Attribution ("Was it us?")
- **Problem**: Business outcomes (churn rate, conversion rate, revenue) are influenced by many factors beyond the vendor's work. Disputes arise over what portion of an improvement is attributable to the vendor.
- **Mitigation**:
  - Define a contractually agreed baseline measurement period before work begins
  - Use control groups or A/B testing architectures where possible
  - Limit gain-sharing to outcomes directly instrumented through the vendor's systems
  - Include "external event" clauses that neutralize attribution if market conditions shift dramatically
  - Federal News Network (2025): "There's transparency and there's data to help identify where things went sideways because everyone is aligned on the same deliverable."

#### Risk 2: Scope Creep in Outcome Contracts
- **Problem**: Because the vendor is accountable for outcomes, clients may keep adding requirements, believing the vendor "owes" more work until the outcome is achieved.
- **Mitigation**:
  - Define "input scope" separately from "outcome target" in the contract
  - Use a formal change control process for work additions
  - Cap total input investment while preserving outcome targets as a shared goal
  - Future of Sourcing: "The contract should afford sufficient flexibility but also protect the vendor from unlimited scope expansion."

#### Risk 3: Measurement Infrastructure
- **Problem**: Many clients lack the telemetry, dashboards, or data systems needed to measure the outcomes they've contracted for.
- **Mitigation**:
  - Build measurement infrastructure as a contractual deliverable (output) before the outcome-linked phase begins
  - Rezoomex (2025): "Start with a controlled pilot, ideally with a trusted client or a low-risk internal initiative. Choose a project where outcomes are easy to define and measure."
  - EPAM's AI/Run is specifically designed to provide attribution models and executive dashboards — this is a competitive asset for EPAM in this space.

#### Risk 4: Client Co-Responsibility
- **Problem**: Outcomes often require client behavior changes — process adoption, data quality, stakeholder engagement. If the client doesn't perform, the vendor misses outcomes they don't control.
- **Mitigation**:
  - Define explicit "client obligations" in the contract (data availability, business owner availability, adoption commitments)
  - Build in "condition precedents" — outcome payments are only due if client obligations were met
  - Everest Group: "The challenge today lies in mindset and governance, building the trust, collaboration, and risk-sharing discipline needed to make these partnerships sustainable."

#### Risk 5: Cash Flow and Revenue Timing
- **Problem**: Outcome payments arrive later than T&M invoices; hybrid models with back-loaded gain-sharing create cash flow stress.
- **Mitigation**:
  - Structure base fees at T&M-equivalent levels to cover cost; outcome bonuses are incremental
  - Ensure milestone payments cover costs at each phase
  - Original Pricing (2025): "Small companies should start with hybrid models and limit outcome-based contracts to a percentage of total revenue."

### 7. How AI Specifically Enables Outcome-Based Delivery

This is the virtuous circle that makes outcome-based models more viable in 2026 than they were in 2019:

#### Better Estimation → Lower Proposal Risk
- AI-assisted scoping tools analyze historical delivery data to produce more accurate effort and timeline estimates
- Reduces the "unknown unknowns" that make fixed-price and outcome-based contracts feel dangerous
- EPAM AI/Run: compares AI-assisted delivery uplift against historical baselines, giving delivery managers a real empirical basis for outcome commitments

#### Automation → Higher Margin on Outcome Contracts
- AI automation compresses delivery time, reducing the cost of producing an output
- Under T&M, this efficiency is given away to the client; under output-based pricing, it becomes margin
- Bessemer VP (2026): "New entrants combining AI automation with domain expertise are already proving they can deliver outcomes better, faster, and cheaper."

#### Faster Delivery → Sooner Outcome Realization
- AI-accelerated development means outcomes land earlier in the engagement, reducing the period over which outcome risk is carried
- This is especially true in code generation, test automation, and documentation — areas where AI is most mature

#### Value Tracking Infrastructure
- AI-native delivery platforms (like EPAM AI/Run) produce real-time metrics on delivery velocity, task completion rates, and adoption gains
- This telemetry is the measurement infrastructure that outcome-based contracts require
- Microsoft (2025): "Within four weeks of implementation, [AI tools] saved 989 hours on routine tasks, boosting productivity valued at INR 989K" — this kind of quantification is exactly what outcome-based billing requires

#### Predictive Maintenance / Managed Services
- AI-powered operations (AIOps) can guarantee SLAs with higher confidence than human-only ops teams
- This makes SLA-linked pricing safer to commit to
- CustomGPT (2025): MSPs are shifting to "predictive insights instead of reactive fixes, automation instead of manual processes, and measurable business outcomes instead of uptime guarantees"

## Counterarguments and Tensions

### Counterargument 1: "Outcome-based models are too risky for vendors"
**The tension is real.** Outcome-based contracts expose vendors to business risk outside their control. A perfectly built personalization engine won't save a churn rate if the client's pricing is uncompetitive. The mitigation is not to refuse outcome-based models but to design them carefully: define client obligations, use hybrid structures, and instrument baselines before go-live. The firms that figured this out (McKinsey, Accenture, Wipro) are growing; the firms that haven't (traditional body-shop IT providers) are facing rate pressure and headcount reductions.

### Counterargument 2: "Clients don't trust outcome measurement"
**Also real.** Clients in T&M relationships have often learned to distrust vendor-reported metrics. The answer is third-party instrumentation, shared dashboards, and contractual audit rights — not abandoning the model. This is exactly what EPAM AI/Run is designed to address.

### Counterargument 3: "T&M will never fully die"
**Probably true.** For exploratory, research-heavy, or rapidly-changing-requirement work, T&M remains appropriate. The argument isn't that T&M is always wrong — it's that it should be the exception, not the default, in an AI-augmented delivery world. The billable hour "won't disappear entirely, but AI is set to disrupt the relationship" (Thomson Reuters, 2025 ILTACON analysis). The question isn't whether T&M survives; it's whether an account manager defaults to it without thinking.

### Counterargument 4: "Our clients won't buy it"
**The data contradicts this.** 43% of enterprise buyers actively prefer vendors willing to share risk through pricing (PwC). CIOs in 2025–2026 are demanding ROI accountability on every engagement. The client who "won't buy" outcome-based models is often a client whose account manager hasn't learned to propose it credibly.
