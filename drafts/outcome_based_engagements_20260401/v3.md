# The Hours Are Over: How to Sell and Deliver Outcomes in an AI-Accelerated World

Your team delivers a 12-week project in 6. Do you celebrate — or quietly panic about the revenue shortfall? A client asks you to prove the ROI of their investment. You open a timesheet. Is that what they're looking for? A competitor just proposed a gain-sharing model on the same opportunity you pitched as T&M. Who wins the deal?

These aren't hypothetical provocations. They're the real conversations happening in client meetings right now — and most of us aren't ready for them.

The time-and-materials model has served this industry well for decades. It is familiar, simple to administer, and requires almost no courage to sell. But AI has exposed its structural flaw: **T&M rewards inefficiency and punishes the teams that actually invest in getting better.** The faster our engineers become — and AI is making them meaningfully faster — the less revenue we earn under the model we've built our business around. That is a perverse incentive, and every account manager and delivery manager at EPAM needs to understand it clearly.

This is a practical guide to doing something about it.

---

## When Efficiency Becomes Your Enemy

Here is the math in its simplest form. A delivery team takes on a 12-week engagement at $150,000. Midway through, they deploy AI-assisted development tooling. They finish in 6 weeks. Under T&M, they've earned $75,000 for delivering identical scope. The client got what they wanted. The team got half the budget.

That is not a hypothetical. That is the structural problem AI has introduced into every project-scoped T&M contract in our portfolio.

The billable hour was always flawed — it discouraged the very efficiency clients claimed to want. A consultant who takes 10 hours bills more than one who takes 2 for the same output. When AI can compress delivery cycles by 60–80% on specific workstreams, that tolerance evaporates.

The perverse incentive is clearest in project-scoped engagements. But it doesn't stop there. In staff augmentation and ongoing platform work, the immediate revenue math looks safer — a faster team adds more features, hours stay flat, revenue appears stable. The problem is that clients have noticed this dynamic. TCS, Infosys, and Wipro are facing selective renegotiations as enterprise clients use AI-driven efficiency arguments to demand lower rates or fewer FTEs. The risk isn't a single project billing short — it is clients systematically reducing headcount on ongoing contracts as AI makes each FTE more productive. The revenue compression is slower, but the structural exposure is identical.

The *Wall Street Journal* noted in December 2025 that AI is rendering the traditional billable hour obsolete across professional services — not just in law and consulting, but in IT engineering delivery. CIOs entered 2026 with "budget exhaustion" after years of AI pilots with limited results. They are no longer willing to fund open-ended T&M spend without ROI accountability attached.

EPAM generates 70–80% of its revenue through T&M billing. That baseline is not a defensible position for the long term. Our 2026 strategic imperative is converting AI-related demand into higher-margin engagements — which is not possible at scale under an hourly billing model.

The answer is not to panic. The answer is to learn a different commercial language.

## Output, Outcome, and Fixed-Price Are Not the Same Thing

We need to be precise about what we're selling. Most practitioners conflate three distinct models, and that confusion produces bad contracts.

**Fixed-price** is familiar: defined scope, defined price, vendor absorbs overrun risk. The trap is well-known — lowball to win, then fight on change orders when scope drifts. Fixed-price is not an evolution of T&M; it's a different way to bill for inputs.

**Output-based** ties payment to specific deliverables — not hours worked, not business results achieved. Payment per artifact: per API deployed, per regression suite maintained, per support ticket resolved, per microservice shipped. As Infosys BPM frames it: "charging based on number of tickets fixed rather than hours invested." The vendor absorbs production risk; the client still absorbs whether the outputs actually work.

**Outcome-based** goes further. Payment is tied to measurable business results the client achieves because of the work: a fee linked to reduced application downtime, gain-sharing on cost savings from process automation, a revenue-linked fee on a commerce platform that converts better. The vendor accepts some share of business risk — not just delivery risk.

The distinction matters enormously in practice:

| | Output-Based | Outcome-Based |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit of value** | Deliverable produced | Business result achieved |
| **Example** | Data pipeline deployed | 15% reduction in customer churn |
| **Vendor controls** | Directly | Partially |
| **Measurement** | Relatively simple | Requires instrumentation |

Consider two ways to scope the same engagement. *Output*: Build a real-time personalization engine; payment upon deployment. *Outcome*: Reduce customer churn by 15% through a personalization engine; base fee upon deployment, plus gain-sharing for each point of churn reduction below baseline.

The personalization engine in scenario two could work perfectly — and churn might still not drop if the client's pricing is uncompetitive or a market shock intervenes. That is the vendor absorbing business risk beyond their control. It is why most experienced practitioners recommend starting with output-based models and graduating to outcome-based as trust and measurement infrastructure develop.

**A client is ready to graduate from output-based to outcome-based contracting when they can demonstrate:**

1. They can state a specific, measurable baseline — current churn rate, deployment frequency, processing cost per transaction — not an aspirational target.
2. They have a data system that already tracks that baseline consistently, without requiring the vendor to build the instrumentation first.
3. They have agreed in principle on an attribution methodology — how will both parties determine that the outcome shifted because of the work, not external factors?
4. There is a named business owner with authority over the decisions the outcome depends on.
5. There is an established working relationship — at minimum one prior engagement — that has given both parties confidence in each other's execution and good faith.

If a client cannot meet the first three criteria, output-based is the right model. Use the engagement to build instrumentation and trust. Graduate when the foundation is in place.

Do not use output-based and outcome-based interchangeably. They require different contracts, different governance, and different risk models.

## The Measurement Problem: When Units Aren't Discrete

Here is the question every AM and DM runs into the moment they try to move off T&M: *How do you actually measure the thing you're selling?*

For managed services and high-volume BPO work, the unit is obvious — tickets processed, uptime percentage, claims handled. The problem is that most of EPAM's delivery work isn't that clean. It's product engineering, platform builds, data infrastructure, modernization programs. Work where the unit of delivery resists easy discretization.

The default answer in software delivery has been story points. It is the wrong answer — and we need to name that honestly before proposing anything to a client.

### Story Points Fail as a Commercial Unit

Story points were designed as an internal planning tool for agile teams. They are team-relative by design — a 5-point story for one team may be a 2-point story for another, depending on experience, familiarity with the codebase, and tooling. That relativity is a feature for internal velocity tracking. It is a defect when you try to use story points as the basis for a commercial contract.

Four problems make story points commercially unworkable:

1. **They measure effort, not value.** A story point estimates how long something will take the team, not what it's worth to the business. A 1-point configuration change that unblocks a $5M revenue stream is not commercially equivalent to an 8-point feature that nobody uses.
2. **They're team-relative.** A "5" in one team is a "3" in another. There is no exchange rate. Clients asking "how many story points per sprint will we get?" are asking a question that has no stable, cross-contract answer.
3. **They're gameable.** Teams under commercial pressure to demonstrate velocity inflate estimates. The result is the appearance of productivity — higher story point counts, same actual delivery rate. Clients have noticed this.
4. **They don't translate to client value.** No CFO has ever reported to a board that their platform initiative delivered 847 story points. Story points are a delivery-side abstraction that never had a natural business-side counterpart.

If you propose a commercial engagement structured around story point throughput, you're building on sand. At best, the client accepts it without understanding it. At worst, they understand exactly what you've proposed — and find a vendor who can speak their language.

### Practical Alternatives for Non-Discrete Work

There is no perfect commercial unit for complex software delivery. That's an honest thing to say. But precise imperfect units beat vague perfect ones — and several approaches are significantly better than hours or story points.

**Functional decomposition.** Break the engagement into functional capabilities delivered — not hours, not points, but working software capabilities with acceptance criteria. "Authentication module complete and in production," "payment integration live and processing transactions," "data pipeline ingesting X event volume at Y latency." Each capability is a unit. It either works, or it doesn't. Payment triggers on acceptance. This approach forces the upfront definition work that most engagements skip — and that definition work is where scope risk lives. Investing three days to define acceptance criteria for a functional capability buys you weeks of scope argument prevention.

**Throughput metrics as capacity proxies.** For subscription and retainer structures, DORA metrics — deployment frequency, lead time for changes, change failure rate, mean time to restore — provide a team-level picture of delivery capacity that is objective, automatable, and impossible to game without also changing actual delivery behavior. A retainer priced around a defined DORA profile ("a team maintaining weekly deployment frequency with sub-2-hour MTTR") ties commercial value to real engineering health, not hour counts. These aren't output metrics in themselves, but they validate that the capacity the client is paying for is operating as described.

**Value stream milestones.** Define value milestones up front with the client: "API layer enabling X client integration," "data pipeline processing Y transaction volume," "self-service BI layer live and adopted by Z teams." Tie payment to milestone completion, not time spent. The milestones should represent genuine business value inflection points — not arbitrary technical checkpoints. If a milestone doesn't represent a meaningful capability the business can act on, it's the wrong milestone.

**Acceptance-criteria-based delivery.** Each unit of output is defined by what it has to do to be accepted, not how long it took to build. Payment triggers on acceptance. The acceptance criteria become the contract. This shifts the commercial conversation from "how much effort did you spend?" to "does this work as agreed?" — which is a question clients can actually evaluate and a standard delivery teams can objectively meet.

**Outcome proxies when true outcomes are hard to attribute.** Sometimes the real outcome — revenue growth, customer retention, cost reduction — is too distal or too influenced by factors outside the delivery team's control to tie directly to a contract. The answer is not to abandon outcome thinking; it's to agree on leading indicators. "Feature adoption rate exceeds 40% within 30 days of launch." "API call volume reaches X by week eight post-deployment." "Automated pipeline replaces manual process for Y transaction type." These proxies are measurable, attributable, and far more meaningful than hours worked — even if they're one step removed from the ultimate business outcome.

### The Investment in Clarity

Every one of these approaches requires upfront definition work that T&M contracts skip entirely. Under T&M, the work starts on Monday and scope gets clarified as you go. Under functional decomposition or acceptance-criteria-based delivery, someone has to define what "done" looks like before the first line of code is written.

That investment is not overhead. It is scope risk management. The engagements that blow up on T&M — the ones that run three times the original estimate, that end in disputes and damaged relationships — almost always trace back to insufficient definition at the start. The upfront clarity that alternative measurement approaches require is the same clarity that prevents the late-project explosions that everyone involved would prefer to avoid.

When a client pushes back on the definition work — "let's just get started" — the honest response is: "We can start without it. But we'll spend that time eventually. We'll spend it either at the front end defining what success looks like, or at the back end arguing about whether we got there." Most clients, when it's framed that way, invest in the front end.

The measurement problem is real. It has no perfect solution. But AMs and DMs who walk into a proposal conversation with functional decomposition, DORA-anchored retainers, and acceptance-criteria-based delivery in their toolkit are having a fundamentally different conversation than those who shrug and say "we work in story points."

## Five Ways to Price a Project Without Billing the Hour

There is no single outcome-based contract structure. Five proven mechanisms exist — and the right choice depends on engagement type, measurement maturity, and how much delivery risk you're prepared to absorb.

**Milestone payments** are the most accessible entry point. Break the project into phases; release payments upon verification of deliverables at each checkpoint. A four-phase structure: 20% on signed architecture, 30% on working prototype, 30% on UAT sign-off, 20% on production deployment. This aligns payment with delivery risk and eliminates the "paying for effort that produces nothing" problem that plagues T&M in discovery-heavy projects. Output-based, not outcome-based — but a meaningful improvement over hourly billing.

> **What it looks like in practice:** A global financial services firm is migrating a legacy data warehouse to a cloud-native data platform. EPAM structures payment in four tranches: 20% upon signed data architecture and platform design ($180K), 30% upon data warehouse decommissioned and historical data migrated and validated ($270K), 30% upon self-service BI layer live and accepted by three pilot business units ($270K), 20% upon 90-day SLA achieved at agreed query performance and availability targets ($180K). The client pays for verified progress, not time spent. EPAM absorbs delivery risk on each phase; the client absorbs scope changes only through formal change orders.

**Gain-sharing** is the purest outcome-based structure. The vendor receives a base fee plus a percentage of quantified savings or revenue gains attributable to their work. The Wipro–BT engagement is the archetype: a T&M base with progressive gain-share triggered by headcount reduction, process efficiency improvement, and lead cycle time reduction. Typical splits run 20–40% of documented gains for 12–24 months post-deployment. The critical requirement: a contractually agreed baseline measurement methodology before work begins. Without it, attribution disputes are inevitable.

> **What it looks like in practice:** A mid-market e-commerce retailer has a personalization and recommendation engine that's underperforming; their current recommendation-driven revenue is $4.2M annually against a $6M industry benchmark for their traffic volume. EPAM builds the new recommendation engine on a reduced retainer of $180K (versus $280K at full T&M). In exchange, EPAM earns 20% of documented revenue lift above the $4.2M baseline, measured monthly for 12 months post-launch. Attribution methodology: the gain-sharing metric is revenue from recommendation-influenced transactions as tracked by the client's existing analytics stack, with a contractually agreed measurement window and an "external event" clause that resets the baseline if a major market disruption occurs. If the engine performs as expected and drives $2M in incremental recommendation revenue, EPAM earns $400K in gain-sharing — a total engagement value of $580K against the $280K T&M alternative.

**Subscription retainer** (Capacity-as-a-Service) works well for long-running platform teams and continuous product development. A fixed monthly fee for a defined delivery capability — squad composition, throughput SLAs, quality metrics — with flexible scope within the retainer boundary. No per-hour policing. No change order theater. Predictable cash flow for the vendor; flexible scope for the client.

> **What it looks like in practice:** A Series C SaaS company needs a product engineering team as a continuous delivery partner for their core platform. EPAM provides a defined capacity subscription at $95K/month: four senior engineers, one staff engineer serving as technical lead, and access to EPAM's AI-assisted development tooling. The team is scoped around the client's product roadmap, with a quarterly re-scoping right — the client can reprioritize within the retainer but cannot unilaterally expand team size mid-quarter. Throughput is tracked via DORA metrics (deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate) against agreed baselines; sustained underperformance triggers a structured conversation and remediation window before any contract adjustment. The client gets a committed, stable team and predictable monthly burn. EPAM gets predictable revenue without per-sprint scope negotiations.

**SLA-linked pricing** is the natural model for managed services and operations. Base fee tied to availability, performance, or quality metrics. Deductions for SLA breach; bonuses for sustained outperformance. Example: a 99.9% uptime SLA with a 2% fee reduction per 0.1% below target and a 5% bonus for sustained 99.95% over six months. The key, as CIO Magazine frames it, is ensuring that the delivered outcome creates "incremental business value for the customer" — not just maintaining the status quo.

> **What it looks like in practice:** A healthcare technology company engages EPAM for platform operations and managed services on a patient-facing application that must meet HIPAA availability requirements. Base fee: $65K/month. SLA targets: 99.9% uptime, P1 incident response under 15 minutes, and bi-weekly deployment frequency. Penalty structure: 2% fee reduction per 0.1% below the uptime SLA in a given month; P1 response breaches above 30 minutes incur a $2,500 flat deduction per incident. Bonus structure: if EPAM sustains 99.95%+ uptime and monthly deployment frequency for six consecutive months, it earns a 5% quarterly bonus ($9,750). The structure puts meaningful but bounded performance risk on EPAM while creating real upside for genuine excellence — not just meeting the floor.

**Per-unit output pricing** works for high-volume, repeatable workstreams where outputs are discrete and automatable. Price per microservice deployed, per automated test suite maintained, per claim processed, per code review completed. Accenture's model in insurance processing — "$10 per claim processed" rather than per consulting hour — is the canonical example. AI automation is most valuable here: compressed per-unit production cost becomes expanded margin. Worth noting: per-unit pricing is most mature at large BPO and managed services firms. For EPAM, it is an aspirational direction in high-volume managed services and AI operations — not a default for project delivery.

The hybrid structure — T&M-equivalent base covering delivery costs, with outcome-linked bonuses layered on top — is the pragmatic on-ramp for most existing relationships. It protects cash flow, limits risk exposure during the trust-building phase, and introduces clients to outcome accountability without asking them to abandon a commercial model they know. Wipro is direct: "Most contracts now comprise hybrid pricing structures rather than 100% gain share/outcome-based pricing." Start there. Graduate clients as measurement infrastructure matures.

> **What it looks like in practice:** A regional bank is modernizing a core loan origination system built on 15-year-old Java infrastructure. EPAM structures a hybrid T&M + outcome bonus engagement. T&M floor: $420K covering 18 months of delivery at negotiated blended rates — this covers EPAM's delivery costs with a modest margin and protects cash flow. Outcome bonus: 15% of total contract value ($63K) paid if the modernized system achieves defined performance targets within 90 days of go-live — specifically: transaction processing time under 2 seconds at peak load, zero P1 incidents in the first 90 days, and successful cutover from the legacy system with no data integrity exceptions. If the team hits those targets, total engagement value is $483K. If they don't, the client paid market rates for delivered work. No clawback. The client gets commercial incentive alignment; EPAM's downside is "we earned standard rates."

**Choosing the right structure.** Three factors drive the decision. Measurement maturity: if the client can state and track a baseline, gain-sharing or SLA-linked structures are viable; if not, milestone or subscription is the right starting point. Relationship stage: new clients belong in milestone structures; established accounts with a delivery track record are candidates for gain-sharing. Delivery confidence: per-unit and outcome-based structures require high predictability; exploratory or shifting scope calls for T&M or a subscription retainer. Match the pricing mechanism to what the engagement actually is — not to what sounds most sophisticated.

## The Objections Are Real. So Are the Mitigations.

The risks of outcome-based engagements are genuine. Here is an honest accounting of each — and the contract mechanics that address them.

**Attribution disputes.** Business outcomes are influenced by factors beyond the vendor's work — a churn reduction could reflect a competitor's exit or a macroeconomic shift, not the personalization engine. Mitigation: define a contractually agreed baseline measurement period before work begins; use control groups or A/B architectures where possible; include "external event" clauses that neutralize attribution if market conditions shift dramatically. Federal IT contracting guidance frames it well: "There's transparency and data to help identify where things went sideways, because everyone is aligned on the same deliverable."

**Scope creep.** Clients accountable for outcomes sometimes treat vendor accountability as an unlimited obligation to add requirements until results arrive. The prevention is precise contract language: input scope and outcome target appear as two separate schedules, not one. Concrete example — Input scope: build and deploy three ML models to specification. Outcome target: 15% reduction in claim processing time. If the models are deployed and perform to spec but the client hasn't updated their intake workflow, the outcome target has not been met — and additional scope is a change order, not an obligation. The destination and the agreed road are not the same thing. Define them separately.

**Measurement infrastructure gaps.** Many clients lack the telemetry or data systems needed to measure contracted outcomes. Mitigation: build measurement infrastructure as a contractual deliverable — an output-based phase before the outcome-linked phase. EPAM AI/Run addresses this directly for engineering and AI delivery work. For engagements where client-side business metrics are the outcomes, scope and price the instrumentation work separately.

**Client co-responsibility failures.** Outcomes require client behavior: data quality, stakeholder availability, process adoption, organizational change. If the client doesn't perform, the vendor misses outcomes they don't fully control. Mitigation: define explicit client obligations in the contract as a shared accountability framework, not a liability clause. Include condition precedents: outcome payments are only due if named client obligations were met.

**Cash flow timing.** Outcome payments arrive later than T&M invoices. Structure base fees at T&M-equivalent levels to cover costs and provide a modest working margin; treat outcome bonuses as incremental upside, not the primary revenue stream until the model is proven within an account. In a well-structured hybrid deal, the base fee is the floor and the outcome bonus is the premium. The downside is "we earn market rates." The upside is "we earn meaningfully above market." That framing is not frightening to an AM — it is a value proposition.

**Three counterarguments your clients and legal teams will raise — and what to say back.**

*"Our legal and procurement teams will never sign this."* They will flag it. That is their job. Non-standard contract structures require legal partnership — IP allocation, measurement audit rights, attribution methodology, outcome definition schedules. The answer is not to avoid outcome-based contracts; it is to build standard templates that anticipate these questions. EPAM needs outcome-contract templates the way it has T&M templates: pre-negotiated positions on common disputes so AMs aren't improvising in front of a procurement team. If those templates don't exist yet, that is the business case to bring to practice leadership.

*"What happens when EPAM misses the outcome target?"* In a well-structured hybrid deal, EPAM does not receive the outcome bonus — and the client has paid base fees at market rates for work delivered. No clawback of base fees on work that was scoped, delivered, and accepted. The vendor's downside is "we don't earn the premium." The client's protection is "we paid for work, not promises." Every AM should be able to give that answer before the contract is signed. If base fees are at risk on an outcome miss, that is a structuring error — not a feature.

*"Is EPAM actually set up internally to manage outcome risk?"* The honest answer today is: partially. EPAM has the delivery tooling (AI/Run), the engineering capability, and the measurement infrastructure to support outcome commitments. What is still developing is the internal governance: who owns outcome tracking on a gain-sharing contract, who decides when an external event clause is triggered, how delivery managers report against outcome targets rather than burn rates. Delivery managers already running outcome-oriented sprints — defining success in business result terms, using telemetry to demonstrate progress — are ahead of the curve. Those who aren't need to start there. The internal operating model must match the external commercial model. That is not a future state; it is the prerequisite.

These are design problems, not reasons to retreat to T&M. Every one is solvable with careful contract architecture. The firms that have figured this out — McKinsey, Accenture, Wipro — are not taking uncalculated risks. They are taking deliberate, instrumented risks. That is what we need to start doing.

## The Language Shift: From "How Many People?" to "What Does Success Look Like?"

Everything above is context. This section is what you do on your next client call.

The opening question that changes the conversation is not "How many engineers do you need?" It is: "What business problem are you actually trying to solve — and how will you know when it's solved?"

That question is a diagnostic. A client who can answer it precisely — "We need to reduce deployment lead time from three weeks to three days, and we'll know we've done it when change failure rate stays below 2%" — is ready to contract on outcomes. A client who responds with "We just need some developers" hasn't connected the work to the results. Your job is to help them make that connection before the contract is signed, not after.

Move from inputs to baselines. Before proposing any outcome-linked structure, establish: what does the current state look like? Who controls the data? A client who cannot state their current churn rate cannot buy a churn-reduction engagement. Baseline definition is where the outcome conversation becomes real.

When proposing the commercial structure, lead with the hybrid model. "Here is our base engagement — milestone deliverables at market rates. Separately, here is a gain-sharing layer tied to the business outcomes we're both trying to achieve. If we hit the outcomes, we both win. If we don't, you've paid for the work at market rates." That framing is not risky. It is credible. It signals belief in our delivery capability and directly addresses the trust deficit clients carry after years of AI investments that didn't move the business.

One organizational reality deserves direct acknowledgment: most AMs are compensated on hours sold or revenue recognized per billing period. That structure actively works against the behavior this article is asking for. An AM who proposes a gain-sharing model on an opportunity that would have closed as T&M is taking a real personal risk. This is not a behavior problem. It is a structural problem. Leadership needs to align incentives — quota structures, compensation plans, recognition mechanisms — before asking individual AMs to absorb that risk. If you are an AM who sees the opportunity, document the win, show the margin, and make the case upward. The organizational change has to be led from the top.

For delivery managers: a DM who manages to hours and timesheets internally will struggle to credibly propose outcome accountability externally. If you are already running outcome-based sprints — defining success in business result terms, using telemetry to demonstrate progress — you have built the practice that makes outcome-based selling authentic. If you haven't, that is where to start.

This is not a pricing experiment. It is a structural realignment of how we define value — and the clients who want partners willing to bet on their own delivery will find us, or they will find someone else.
