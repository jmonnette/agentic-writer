# Persona Review — EPAM BU Head
**Deck**: The Hours Are Over — East BU QBR April 2026
**Reviewer Persona**: VP/SVP Business Unit Head, full P&L accountability, 15–20 years IT services
**Review Mode**: In-character verbal feedback, followed by structured blind spots analysis

---

## In-Character Feedback: BU Head Speaking After the Deck

---

Okay. I'll give you credit — the title slide did not put me to sleep. "The hours are over." Fine. I get it. And yes, the 70–80% T&M dependency number on that first slide is real. I look at that every week.

Slide 2 — the AI efficiency trap — is the one I want every AM in this room to walk out with tattooed on their hand. Twelve weeks, $150K. Six weeks, $75K. Identical scope. That is happening to us right now on at least three accounts, and I don't think half our AMs have connected the dots yet. The augmentation renegotiation point is just as important — TCS and Infosys are already absorbing this. So yeah, slides 2 through 4 land. You've got my attention.

The competitive data — McKinsey 25%, Accenture $1.5B, Wipro 50% — that's the kind of thing I can bring upstairs and say "this is not theoretical." Good.

Here's where I started leaning back.

Slide 5. "Start the conversation now, before the client does." Completely agree. But then you say: "A hybrid engagement with a higher-margin outcome bonus improves your numbers, not just the client's." And you just... leave it there. That's an assertion. I need the math. What does a hybrid deal actually look like on my P&L versus a T&M equivalent? If I'm running a $2M T&M account and we convert half of it to a hybrid structure with a 15% outcome bonus on the back half, what happens to recognized revenue timing? What happens to margin in the year we make the transition? I don't know, because you didn't show me.

The commercial model taxonomy on slide 6 — actually useful. I'm going to use that table in a conversation with my senior AMs. The "recommended path" note at the bottom is the right instinct: don't start at Level 5. But you said "capacity-based is the natural evolution from staff aug" without telling me what that means contractually for an account I'm trying to retain. What does the client agree to? What do we give up?

Slides 7 and 8 — the pricing mechanisms and the worked examples. This is the closest you get to real mechanics, and I want more of it. The gain-sharing example — the e-commerce recommendation engine, $580K versus $280K T&M equivalent — that's a real number and it got my attention. But I need to know: is that a real deal? Or is that illustrative? Because if it's illustrative, I need you to tell me where the real ones are. One real example with a client name I can check beats five hypotheticals. If we don't have real examples yet, say so — and tell me what deal we're going to make the first one.

Slide 9 — story points — I honestly don't know why this is in a QBR deck. I understand the point. But my AMs are not sitting in rooms debating story point methodology. This slide is for a practitioner audience, not a BU leadership conversation. I'd cut it or move it to an appendix.

Slide 10 — measurement alternatives — same problem. DORA metrics are fine. My DMs know what DORA is. But this slide belongs in a delivery playbook, not a commercial strategy QBR. It's useful content in the wrong room.

Slides 11 through 13 on staff augmentation — this is where the deck gets interesting again and also where it starts to frustrate me. The Level 1 through Level 5 evolution framework is legitimate. And the call to action is right: "most EPAM accounts are at Level 1 or 2. Move one level." I can work with that. It's specific enough.

But then slide 12 — "Why would the client give up control?" — you're coaching AMs on how to have client conversations. That's fine. But I'm sitting here thinking: my AMs aren't equipped to have these conversations without support structure behind them. You're telling them what to say to an Engineering Manager or a CFO, but you haven't told me who's going to be on the call with them. Is there a deal desk? A commercial overlay? Someone from practice leadership who can co-sell this? Because if the answer is "the AM figures it out alone," this deck doesn't change behavior. It gives people vocabulary they won't use.

Slide 14 — objections and responses — okay, here's the thing. You have a response to the legal team objection that says "EPAM needs outcome-contract templates the way it has T&M templates." You're admitting in a client objection script that the infrastructure doesn't exist yet. That's a significant thing to say in a QBR. What's the answer? Who's building those templates? By when? I need a name, not a department.

Slide 15 — AI/Run enabling measurement. This is actually important, and it's buried too late in the deck. The hardest question in any outcome-based conversation is "how will we know?" and AI/Run is the answer. But you gave it one slide, in position 15 of 16, and you framed it as "have it ready." That's not enough. This is a competitive differentiator. If I can say to a client "we can instrument this and show you attribution in a way McKinsey can't" — that's a deal point. It should be threaded through the deck, not appended.

Slide 16 — the closing — nine things to remember, three questions. Fine. Standard. Not memorable.

Here is my bottom line: I agree with the premise. I agree we need to move. What I don't have yet is what I need to actually move.

I don't know which three accounts in my portfolio should be the pilots. I don't know who in the organization I call when an AM finds a willing client. I don't know what the margin math looks like on a hybrid deal during the transition year. And I don't know what changes for AM compensation — you touched on it in slide 5 in one paragraph, and then you never came back to it.

I'm not against this. I need to know how to operationalize it. This deck convinces me the problem is real. It does not convince me we have a plan.

## Blind Spots and Gaps (Outside Persona Voice)

The following gaps are structural issues visible from the BU Head's perspective that the deck does not address — and would need to be resolved before a BU Head acts on this content.

---

### 1. No margin math on a hybrid deal

The deck asserts that outcome-based models are better for margin. It does not show the calculation. A BU Head needs to see a before/after P&L comparison: a T&M deal at current rates versus a hybrid deal at base + outcome bonus. What does recognized revenue look like by quarter? What does margin look like in year one versus year two? The gain-sharing example on slide 8 is the closest the deck comes to this, but it compares total contract value — it does not show timing, margin profile, or what happens in a miss scenario.

**What's needed**: A single worked example showing the margin and revenue timeline of a hybrid deal versus T&M equivalent across 4–6 quarters. Include the miss scenario (outcome bonus doesn't pay out) to show the floor is protected.

---

### 2. AM incentive and compensation — mentioned once, never resolved

Slide 5 makes the argument that an outcome bonus "improves your numbers" and is "not a revenue sacrifice, it's a margin improvement with a base-fee floor." This is correct as a framing point. But it does not address the fundamental question: how does an AM's quota credit work when outcome bonus revenue is recognized 12–18 months post-delivery? The BU Head knows from experience that AMs will not change behavior if the incentive structure fights the model. This is named explicitly in the persona as a non-negotiable question. The deck does not answer it.

**What's needed**: A direct answer to quota credit timing for outcome bonuses, or an acknowledgment that this requires a comp model change — and who owns that decision.

---

### 3. Transition period risk is completely absent

The deck acknowledges that "most EPAM accounts are at Level 1 or 2" and recommends moving one level at a time. It does not address what happens to revenue during the transition. If an account converts from pure T&M to a hybrid model, there will likely be a period of commercial renegotiation, measurement baseline establishment, and client trust-building before the new model is earning. What does that do to the BU's quarterly forecast? The deck has no answer. This is the BU Head's single largest practical concern with any transformation initiative.

**What's needed**: At minimum, an acknowledgment of the transition period and a recommended approach (e.g., "convert at renewal, not mid-contract; build baseline Q1 so hybrid structure activates in Q2").

---

### 4. No prioritization framework for which accounts to start with

The deck advises moving one level at a time but gives no criteria for which accounts are candidates for that first move. A BU Head managing 8–20 accounts needs a prioritization lens — not a universal prescription. The persona's explicit question is: "Which three accounts in my BU should I start with, and why those three?" The deck does not answer this. Slide 13 gets close ("accounts with the highest engineer counts and longest tenure are simultaneously the most valuable candidates for evolution and the most at risk") — but this is a diagnostic observation, not a selection framework.

**What's needed**: A prioritization matrix or set of qualifying criteria: minimum account tenure, minimum trust level, EPAM domain ownership presence, measurement infrastructure feasibility, AM readiness. Even a 2x2 would help.

---

### 5. Organizational support infrastructure is named as absent but not addressed

Slide 14 contains the line: "EPAM needs outcome-contract templates the way it has T&M templates. If those don't exist yet, that's the business case to bring to practice leadership." This is an honest admission buried in an objection response — but it treats a critical infrastructure gap as someone else's problem. A BU Head will not pilot outcome-based deals if there is no deal desk, no legal template, no approved commercial structure to route through. The deck identifies the need but does not indicate who is building this, on what timeline, or what the BU Head should do in the meantime.

**What's needed**: A clear statement of current organizational readiness — what exists, what doesn't, and who owns the gaps. Even "here's the escalation path to get deal support for your first pilot" would be actionable.

---

### 6. AI/Run is underweighted and underpositioned

Slide 15 introduces EPAM AI/Run as the answer to the measurement attribution problem — arguably the most important infrastructure enabler in the deck. It appears in slide 15 of 16 and is framed as a tool to "have ready" when the client asks the measurement question. This undersells a genuine competitive advantage. Competitors making outcome-based commitments face the same attribution problem. If EPAM has tooling that addresses it, that is a differentiator that should be woven through the commercial model conversation from slide 6 onward — not appended at the end.

**What's needed**: Reference to AI/Run measurement capability when introducing outcome-based and gain-sharing models (slides 7–8), not just in the closing. At minimum, the gain-sharing worked example should note that AI/Run is what makes the attribution clause credible.

---

### 7. Slides 9–10 are misdirected for this audience

The story points critique (slide 9) and measurement alternatives table (slide 10) are legitimate and well-constructed content. They belong in a delivery manager playbook or AM commercial training curriculum. In a BU-level QBR, they interrupt the commercial argument and waste time with an audience that is not going to personally negotiate story point structures. These slides should be relocated to supporting materials.

---

### 8. No ask of the room

The closing slide has three questions for AMs to ask their clients. It does not have an ask of the BU Head specifically. What decision or commitment is expected from a BU Head after seeing this deck? Nominate two pilot accounts by Q2? Fund one deal desk resource? Escalate the comp model question to HR? The deck ends on individual AM behavior change, which is the right call to action for the front line — but a BU Head needs to know what role they play in making this happen at the portfolio level.

**What's needed**: One explicit BU Head commitment: "From this BU Head, we need: [X] by [date]."
