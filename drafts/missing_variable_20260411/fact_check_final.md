## Issues Requiring Correction

### Moderate (Should Fix)

1. **"30 years"** (paragraph: "Amy Edmondson's work at Harvard Business School, developed across 30 years..."): Edmondson's foundational psychological safety research was first published in 1999. From 1999 to 2026 is 27 years; multiple sources describe her career as "25+ years." The phrase "30 years" overstates the duration. Suggested correction: change to **"nearly three decades"** or **"more than 25 years"** — both are accurate and defensible without requiring a precise start date.

2. **"single strongest predictor... more predictive than individual talent levels, team composition, or management quality"** (paragraph: "Google's Project Aristotle..."): The study identified five team dynamics and ranked psychological safety as the most important, but did not publish regression coefficients comparing it to "management quality" specifically. The claim is directionally accurate but implies a precision the published research does not support. Suggested correction: change to **"the most important of the five dynamics the study identified — more important than individual talent levels or team composition"** — consistent with what Google's own publications state.

### Minor (Consider)

- The article has no inline citations or source notes for Edmondson's work beyond the paragraph text. For a LinkedIn-distributed article, this is stylistically appropriate. However, if the author publishes to a more formal outlet, a footnote pointing to "Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams" (Administrative Science Quarterly, 1999) would strengthen credibility.

## Unsourced Claims to Address

The following assertions are presented as general truths without a named source. They are plausible and consistent with the broader literature, but lack traceable backing:

1. "Learning Velocity is produced by the willingness to experiment and the tolerance for experiments that fail." — This is the author's analytical framing, presented as a factual mechanism. It reads as interpretation, which is appropriate — but the article would benefit from a signal word like "I'd argue" or "In my experience" to flag it as the author's thesis rather than a researched finding.

2. "PE-backed portfolio companies, under intense performance pressure, are most likely to create [high fear of judgment]." — Paragraph in "Why This Matters Specifically for PE-Backed Organizations." This is a reasonable professional observation but is stated as a near-certainty with no source. No correction required given the article's op-ed nature, but worth noting.
