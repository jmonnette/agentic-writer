# Outline: Where AI Actually Makes Money
**Thesis**: AI is generating real, measurable business value — but almost exclusively for companies that have stopped treating it as a technology problem. The winners are using AI to directly touch revenue: personalizing experiences, optimizing prices, accelerating sales, preventing fraud, and compressing time-to-market for new products. The question isn't whether AI creates value. It's whether your organization is structured to capture it.
**Word Count Target**: 1,900–2,100 words
**Tone**: Direct, evidenced, mildly provocative. Post-hype pragmatism. Confident but not breathless. Challenges the cost-cutting narrative without being naive about the graveyard of failed deployments.

---

## Opening: The PoC Graveyard (10% — ~200 words)
- **Hook**: Pattern 4 (Historical/Temporal Contrast) — The dominant narrative about AI and business value has been about cost: fewer headcounts, cheaper code, automated customer service. That's real. It's also incomplete, and for most organizations it's the wrong frame.
- **Context**: BCG: 74% of companies have not shown tangible value from AI. Only 4% generating substantial value at scale. The gap between "AI everywhere" and "AI value everywhere" is the central problem.
- **Thesis Statement**: The companies closing that gap aren't distinguished by their model selection. They're distinguished by where they're deploying AI — directly against revenue-generating activities. Three distinct mechanisms are doing the real work.
- **Key Transition**: Before examining those mechanisms, one framework explains both the failures and the successes.

---

## Section 1: Three Ways AI Creates Value (8% — ~150 words)
- **Main Point**: Framing section — not all AI value is the same. Distinguish:
  - **Revenue Acceleration**: AI touching the top line directly (personalization, pricing, sales)
  - **Cost Shield that Protects Revenue**: AI preventing revenue erosion (fraud, supply chain failures, downtime)
  - **Asset Creation**: AI generating entirely new monetizable assets (drug candidates, new product capabilities)
- **Evidence**: Setup for the three sections that follow
- **Stance Alignment**: POV Brief — "structure by value mechanism, not by industry"
- **Key Transition**: Revenue acceleration first because it's the most direct answer to the article's question

---

## Section 2: AI as Revenue Accelerator (28% — ~560 words)
- **Main Point**: AI is generating measurable top-line growth through personalization at scale, dynamic pricing, and sales velocity — and the numbers are large enough to be strategic, not incremental.

### Personalization
- **Evidence**: Amazon — 35% of total revenue from AI recommendation systems. McKinsey — personalization drives 5–15% revenue lift; leading companies generate 40% more revenue than average performers from personalization efforts.
- **Analysis**: Amazon's case is the proof-of-concept at extreme scale. The McKinsey range (5–15%) is what the rest of the market can realistically target. The 40% gap between leaders and average performers is the competitive moat.

### Dynamic Pricing
- **Evidence**: Marriott and Hilton — 5–10% RevPAR lift from AI revenue management. Four Seasons Whistler — 28% increase in off-peak revenue. Airlines: PROS/Lufthansa using deep neural networks for per-session pricing optimization, +6% revenue per passenger on ancillaries.
- **Analysis**: Pricing is AI's most direct revenue lever — no intermediary steps between the AI decision and the dollar captured. The hospitality sector is the clearest current case because RevPAR is standardized and auditable.

### Sales Velocity
- **Evidence**: ZoomInfo Copilot — 60% more demos and meetings booked, 90% improvement in email response rates (50,000+ user sample). McKinsey: AI in B2B sales delivers 15–25% ROI increase, 30–50% reduction in lead conversion time.
- **Analysis**: The ZoomInfo data is significant because of sample size — 50K users is statistical validation, not a vendor case study. The mechanism is prioritization (AI identifies the right accounts and moments) rather than automation.

- **Stance Alignment**: POV — augmentation frame; sales AI enables reps to do more of the work that actually closes deals
- **Key Transition**: Revenue acceleration gets the headlines. But there's a second category of value that's equally real — and harder to see.

---

## Section 3: AI as Cost Shield (20% — ~400 words)
- **Main Point**: Some of AI's most auditable value comes from preventing revenue erosion — stopping fraud before it happens, forecasting demand accurately enough to prevent stockouts, and keeping production equipment running. This is profitability at scale.

### Fraud Prevention
- **Evidence**: Visa — AI analyzed 320 billion transactions, prevented $40 billion in fraud. JPMorgan — 400% ROI on fraud detection AI in year one; 30% reduction in operational costs, 20% improvement in alert accuracy.
- **Analysis**: $40B is the single most compelling hard-dollar figure in enterprise AI. The mechanism matters: AI fraud detection doesn't just save money — it enables transaction volume growth that would otherwise be constrained by human review capacity.

### Supply Chain and Demand Forecasting
- **Evidence**: Walmart — AI reduced stockouts significantly, improved inventory turnover (Walmart executives: AI "transformed our approach to demand forecasting, inventory flow, and cost optimization"). Industry: AI reduces demand forecast errors by 20–50%, decreases stockout incidents by 30%.
- **Analysis**: Every stockout is a permanent revenue loss — customers who go elsewhere don't always return. A 1% improvement in forecast accuracy across a $50B retail catalog represents hundreds of millions in recovered revenue. Frame as revenue protection, not cost reduction.

- **Stance Alignment**: POV — augmentation frame; this is AI enabling supply chain teams to make better decisions, not replacing them
- **Key Transition**: Revenue acceleration and cost shielding are improvements to existing business models. The third mechanism is different — AI creating entirely new monetizable assets.

---

## Section 4: AI as Asset Creator (18% — ~360 words)
- **Main Point**: The most structurally interesting form of AI value creation is when AI generates new assets — drug candidates, novel proteins, new product capabilities — that didn't exist before and have direct monetary value.

### Drug Discovery
- **Evidence**: Insilico Medicine — compressed time from drug program initiation to preclinical candidate from 4–6 years to 12–18 months across 20+ internal programs; ISM001-055 reached Phase IIa clinical trials. An AI-discovered compound sold to Exelixis for $80M. 2024–25: highest single-year IND filing count for AI-originated molecules on record.
- **Analysis**: The Exelixis deal is a clean proof point: an AI-discovered compound that was monetized at a named price. The timeline compression (4–6 years → 12–18 months) translates directly to revenue because drug patents expire on fixed schedules — every year of acceleration is a year of additional market exclusivity.

### The Broader Principle
- **Analysis**: Asset creation is AI at its most transformative — not optimizing an existing process, but generating something new that can be valued, sold, or deployed. The same principle applies beyond pharma: AI-designed materials, AI-generated personalized financial products, AI-created training datasets. The mechanism is the same.

- **Key Transition**: Three mechanisms, all generating real documented value. So why are 74% of companies still in the graveyard?

---

## Section 5: Why 4% Win While 74% Fail (12% — ~240 words)
- **Main Point**: The BCG 10-20-70 rule explains both the successes and the failures. The companies winning with AI have invested 70% of their transformation effort in people and process, not technology. The laggards have done the reverse.
- **Evidence**: BCG — organizations following 10-20-70 achieve 2.1x greater ROI and scale twice as many AI initiatives. McKinsey — only 19% of companies see revenue increases >5% from AI; 36% see no change. Deloitte Q4 2024 — 74% of organizations say their most advanced GenAI initiatives are meeting or exceeding ROI expectations (among those with mature initiatives).
- **Analysis**: The separation between leaders and laggards isn't model selection, data quality, or cloud infrastructure. It's organizational capability. Amazon's recommendation engine didn't produce 35% of revenue because Amazon picked the right algorithm in 2003. It produced that because Amazon built the data infrastructure, the experimentation culture, and the organizational discipline to iterate on it for twenty years.
- **Negation-then-affirmation**: This isn't a technology gap. It's a capability gap. And capability is buildable.
- **Stance Alignment**: Author's recurring "culture is infrastructure" argument; BCG 10-20-70 already in library

---

## Conclusion: What This Means for Leaders (4% — ~90 words)
- **Synthesis**: The question isn't whether AI creates business value. The evidence is overwhelming that it does — for companies that deploy it against revenue-generating activities and invest in the organizational capability to use it.
- **Forward Momentum**: Not a market size forecast. A diagnostic: Where in your business is AI currently touching revenue, and where is it touching only process? The answer to that question determines whether you're in the 4% or the 74%.
- **Closing**: Prescriptive diagnostic prompt — actionable for the reader without being prescriptive about specific tools

## Voice Considerations
- Open with Pattern 4 (historical contrast) — past narrative vs. present reality
- Deploy negation-then-affirmation multiple times: "Not a technology gap. A capability gap." / "Not a technology problem. A people and process problem."
- Use bold inline emphasis for the 10-20-70 breakdown and key statistics
- Keep headers declarative and doing rhetorical work (not labels)
- Maintain 18–22 word average sentence length with strategic ultra-short punches after complex sentences
- End with prescriptive diagnostic, not market forecast
- Avoid "disruption," "AI-powered" as generic prefix, market size projections
