# Most AI Projects Don't Fail at the Model. They Fail at the Foundation.

*Co-authored for The Engineer. SME: Jeff Monnette, EPAM Systems, Inc.*

---

Walk into almost any large enterprise today and you will find the same thing. An AI proof of concept that impressed the board. A pilot that wowed the demo audience. And a deployment backlog that has not moved in six months.

We call this "pilot purgatory." Not a shortage of ambition. Not a shortage of budget. A shortage of the engineering infrastructure required to turn a demo into a system that runs in production, scales under load, and delivers measurable results to the business.

The question is no longer whether AI can work. It's why we can't operationalize it.

---

## The 95% Failure Rate Tells You the Wrong Story

A July 2025 study from MIT's NANDA lab found that 95% of GenAI pilots yield no measurable P&L impact. That statistic gets cited as evidence that AI is overhyped. It isn't.

Not a model problem. An engineering readiness problem.

The MIT researchers are explicit about the mechanism: pilot projects collapse not because the models underperform, but because organizations fail to operationalize them. The models move from demo environment to production and encounter the actual enterprise: fragmented data, brittle integrations, undefined ownership, and infrastructure never designed to support AI workloads. A March 2026 Cloudera and HBR Analytic Services report found that **73% of enterprise data leaders say processing and preparing data for AI is challenging**, ranking above model accuracy, computing costs, and talent shortages. The failure is upstream of the model. It is in the plumbing.

This distinction matters because it determines where you invest. If you believe it's a model problem, you chase newer architectures. If you understand it's an engineering problem, you fix your plumbing.

## Your Data Is Not AI-Ready. Probably Not Even Close.

Most enterprises believe their data situation is manageable. The numbers say otherwise.

According to a March 2026 Cloudera and HBR Analytic Services report, only **7% of enterprises say their data is completely AI-ready**. Seven percent. That means 93 out of 100 organizations attempting to scale AI are building on a foundation they have already admitted is compromised.

When enterprise data leaders, not model teams, not vendor sales reps, but the people who own the pipelines, rank their primary barrier to AI success, 73% point to data quality and completeness before anything else. Not the model. Not compute. Not talent. The data. A separate industry survey of more than 400 data leaders found that **41% say lack of real-time data access prevents AI models from delivering timely insights**. Consistent with what we observe in engagements: organizations standing up AI capability on top of siloed, inconsistently governed, partially documented data.

The model performs beautifully on the curated test set. It behaves unpredictably on real production data. And the reason runs deeper than cleanliness. Structuring data so AI agents can reason over it in business terms is not the same problem as having clean data. A semantic data layer must sit between raw enterprise data and the AI systems consuming it, translating technical schema into business concepts agents can actually interpret. Without it, even well-governed data remains opaque to agents: they can retrieve records, but they cannot understand what those records mean in context. Data readiness for AI is a harder bar than data readiness for traditional analytics.

Before you scale AI, understand what you actually have. A data readiness audit is not optional preparation. It is the first act of the project.

## Agentic AI Makes the Integration Problem Dangerous, Not Just Inconvenient

Here is where the stakes get higher.

The integration challenge is not new. Connecting AI tooling to legacy systems, managing authentication across environments, ensuring AI outputs can actually trigger downstream actions. These are familiar engineering problems. Most teams have learned to work around them in copilot-style tools. A developer gets a suggestion. A human reviews it. The human acts.

Agentic AI breaks that model entirely.

Agents don't suggest. They act. They call APIs, write to databases, trigger workflows, and spawn sub-agents to complete tasks autonomously. When your integration layer is fragile, a human-in-the-loop system produces bad suggestions. An agentic system produces bad actions. That is a different category of risk.

Deloitte's AI Pulse Check 2025 found that **nearly 60% of AI leaders cite integrating with legacy systems and addressing risk and compliance as their organization's primary challenges in adopting agentic AI**. That finding, troubling in a copilot world, becomes a serious governance problem in an agentic one.

Here is what that governance problem looks like in practice. An agent is tasked with updating customer records across three systems: a CRM, an ERP, and a legacy billing platform. It completes the first two calls successfully. On the third, the billing platform's API times out. The agent has no rollback logic. It marks the task complete. The CRM and ERP now reflect the updated record. The billing system does not. A human copilot would have flagged the discrepancy. The agent closed the ticket.

There is a second failure mode that receives less attention: memory. Most enterprise AI agents today are amnesiac. Each session starts cold, with no access to prior context, prior decisions, or accumulated institutional knowledge. An agent that cannot remember what it did last week will repeat errors, lose context across handoffs, and require humans to re-establish ground truth on every engagement. Persistent agentic memory is an emerging architectural pattern: a unified knowledge layer that any AI tool can plug into, enabling agents to build on previous interactions and maintain continuity across sessions. Nate Jones has built one illustrative example of this pattern (OB1, github.com/NateBJones-Projects/OB1), showing what the architecture can look like in practice. Agents operating with that continuity don't just execute tasks. They build on prior context, avoid repeating errors, and maintain relevance across handoffs. This architecture is early, but the problem it solves is not: enterprises deploying agents without persistent memory are building systems that scale in number but not in intelligence.

Gartner's August 2025 data shows less than 5% of enterprise applications feature AI agents. Gartner predicts that figure reaches 40% by end of 2026. The timeline is compressed. Most organizations will be deploying agents before they have solved the integration problems that make agents safe to deploy.

Gartner's June 2025 analysis reinforces this directly: **over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027** due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls. That is not a prediction about AI capability. It is a prediction about engineering discipline.

For organizations already running agents in production, the answer is not to restart. It is to retrofit: layer governance, observability, and rollback capability onto what is already running, before it scales further.

## The 5% Who Are Winning Are Not Using Better Models

The companies pulling ahead are not doing so because they selected the right foundation model or hired more data scientists. BCG's "Widening AI Value Gap" report from September 2025 found that **AI leaders achieve 1.7x revenue growth and 3.6x three-year total shareholder return** compared to laggards.

The separating variable is not technology selection. It is execution discipline.

Across the organizations we work with at EPAM that have cracked into the top quartile, the pattern is consistent. Clean, governed, accessible data. Integration layers built for programmatic access, not just human workflows. Clear ownership of AI systems beyond the initial build. Monitoring and observability applied to AI outputs the way they are applied to any production system. This aligns with BCG's own 10-20-70 principle: roughly 10% of the value gap is explained by algorithms and model work, 20% by data and infrastructure, and 70% by people, process, and organizational discipline. The gap is not in the models. It is in the operating model built around them.

This is not sophisticated. It is foundational. The gap between leaders and laggards in AI is, at its core, an engineering maturity gap.

## Technical Debt Will Compound Faster Than You Think

Here is the uncomfortable arithmetic.

Legacy systems slow down human-paced development. They cripple AI-paced development. And in agentic architectures, they become existential: an autonomous system needs reliable, well-documented integration points to operate safely. Technical debt is not a background drag on AI velocity. In an agentic context, it is a reliability and governance risk that compounds with every agent you add.

Here is what that compounding looks like in practice. An organization deploys three agents: one to ingest support tickets, one to classify them, one to trigger resolution workflows. Each agent works in isolation. But the ticketing system's API was built for human-rate traffic and starts throttling under agentic load. The classification agent times out waiting for responses and begins returning defaults. The resolution agent acts on those defaults. No single component failed. The system, as a whole, did. And because none of the agents were instrumented for this interaction, nobody knows why tickets are misrouted until a customer escalates. Practitioners see this pattern consistently: debt that was invisible to human workflows becomes the failure point in agentic ones.

The organizations deprioritizing infrastructure investment today, in favor of standing up more AI pilots, are making a specific bet: that they can scale AI delivery later, after they get buy-in, without first addressing the foundation. Most will lose that bet. Not because AI is hard. Because you cannot build a ten-story structure on a one-story foundation and expect it to hold.

## Fix the Plumbing First. Then Deploy the AI.

The path forward is not complicated. It requires discipline.

Start with a data readiness assessment: inventory what you have, where it lives, how it is governed, and what integration work is required to make it accessible to AI systems. "AI-ready" data is not just clean data. It is lineage-tracked, event-driven, governed with clear ownership, accessible via well-documented APIs, and structured so agents can reason over it in business terms, not just retrieve it. If you can only start one place, start with data: everything else builds on it. The 7% statistic is a ceiling, not an average. Most organizations are further from ready than they believe, and the gap becomes visible the moment you move from demo to production.

Build integration with agents in mind, not just copilots. If your AI strategy currently relies on tools that sit beside your workflows rather than inside them, that architecture will not survive the agentic transition. Design integration layers that support programmatic access, event-driven triggers, idempotent APIs, circuit breakers, and robust error handling. An agent that cannot gracefully handle a failed API call, with rollback, retry logic, and an audit trail, is not ready for production. Full stop.

Apply engineering discipline to AI the way you apply it to any production system. That means observability: log what the model receives, what it produces, and what downstream action results. It means testing: define expected behaviors and build regression suites. It means ownership: someone is accountable for this system after the launch celebration. And it means cost governance. Token consumption is invisible at pilot scale and brutal at production scale. A customer service agent handling 50,000 interactions per day, each requiring multiple LLM calls with large context windows, can generate token costs that dwarf the infrastructure budget the original business case justified. Agentic workflows compound this further: across enterprise deployments, teams consistently report token consumption running an order of magnitude or more above equivalent prompt-response interactions, driven by tool calls, context accumulation, and multi-step reasoning chains. Organizations approve pilots based on demo economics, then hit a production cost wall that was never in the business case. The project gets scaled back, not because the AI failed, but because nobody ran the math before launch. Model token consumption as part of production readiness: estimate call volume and context window size at scale, choose models appropriate to the task, and build cost observability alongside performance observability so finance can see what they are paying for.

Evolve the measurement framework alongside the infrastructure. Engineering teams deploying AI are still being evaluated on engineering metrics: velocity, deployment frequency, cycle time. These do not capture AI value. The right frame shifts to business KPIs: decisions accelerated, revenue protected, cost avoided, time-to-insight reduced. This is not a soft governance exercise. Teams that cannot demonstrate AI's impact in business terms lose the infrastructure budget required to sustain it. That is the mechanism by which pilot purgatory persists.

The infrastructure work is necessary but not sufficient. The 70% is organizational: clear ownership, aligned incentives, and the discipline to sequence deliberately when executive timelines are pushing for speed.

Address technical debt with urgency proportional to your AI ambitions. Organizations projecting aggressive AI roadmaps while deferring infrastructure investment are not being bold. They are accumulating risk they have not yet priced.

---

## The Question Is Not Whether You're Ready for AI

The question isn't whether your organization wants to deploy AI. Everyone does. The question is whether your engineering foundation can support AI that actually works in production: at scale, with governance, under load, and with agents that can be trusted to act without a human reviewing every output.

Most cannot. Yet.

That is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to sequence correctly: data, integration, observability, then scale. The organizations building that foundation now are the ones still deploying AI in 2027 while others are busy explaining canceled agentic projects to their boards. And the ones sustaining that investment are the ones that learned to speak in business KPIs: decisions made faster, revenue protected, costs avoided. Not story points.

Ask yourself this: if you doubled the number of AI agents running in your environment tomorrow, would your infrastructure hold? Would you know what they were doing? Would you be able to stop them if something went wrong?

Most organizations cannot answer yes to all three. That is not a readiness gap. That is the AI strategy gap. It has nothing to do with the model.
