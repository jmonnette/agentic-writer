# Most AI Projects Don't Fail at the Model. They Fail at the Foundation.

*Co-authored for The Engineer. SME: Jeff Monnette, EPAM Systems, Inc.*

---

Walk into almost any large enterprise today and you will find the same thing. A proof of concept that impressed the board. A pilot that wowed the demo audience. And a deployment backlog that has not moved in six months.

We call this "pilot purgatory." Not a shortage of ambition. Not a shortage of budget. A shortage of the engineering infrastructure required to turn a demo into a system that runs in production, scales under load, and delivers measurable results to the business.

The conversation has shifted. The question is no longer whether AI can work. It's why we can't ship it.

---

## The 95% Failure Rate Tells You the Wrong Story

A July 2025 study from MIT's NANDA lab found that 95% of GenAI pilots yield no measurable P&L impact. That statistic gets cited as evidence that AI is overhyped. It isn't.

Not a model problem. An engineering readiness problem.

When AI projects fail to reach production, the root cause is almost never the model. GPT-4, Gemini, Claude — the frontier models are genuinely capable. The problem sits underneath them: fragmented data, brittle integrations, undefined ownership, and infrastructure that was never designed to support AI workloads. The models are ready. The organizations running them are not.

This distinction matters because it determines where you invest. If you believe it's a model problem, you chase newer architectures. If you understand it's an engineering problem, you fix your plumbing.

---

## Your Data Is Not AI-Ready. Probably Not Even Close.

Most enterprises believe their data situation is manageable. The numbers say otherwise.

According to a March 2026 Cloudera and HBR Analytic Services report, only **7% of enterprises say their data is completely AI-ready**. Seven percent. That means 93 out of 100 organizations attempting to scale AI are building on a foundation they have already admitted is compromised.

A May 2025 Fivetran report surveying more than 400 data leaders put a sharper edge on this. **49% of enterprise technology leaders say their current data architecture cannot handle AI demands.** More telling: 68% of organizations with less than half their data centralized reported lost revenue tied to failed or delayed AI projects. The data problem is not a technical abstraction. It has a dollar value, and it is showing up in your results.

The pattern is consistent across engagements: an organization stands up an AI capability on top of siloed, inconsistently governed, partially documented data. The model performs beautifully on the curated test set. It behaves unpredictably on real production data. The team spends the next four months debugging data quality issues they assumed were already solved.

Before you scale AI, understand what you actually have. A data readiness audit is not optional preparation. It is the first act of the project.

## Agentic AI Makes the Integration Problem Dangerous, Not Just Inconvenient

Here is where the stakes get higher.

The integration challenge is not new. Connecting AI tooling to legacy systems, managing authentication across environments, ensuring AI outputs can actually trigger downstream actions — familiar engineering problems. Most teams have learned to work around them in copilot-style tools. A developer gets a suggestion. A human reviews it. The human acts.

Agentic AI breaks that model entirely.

Agents don't suggest. They act. They call APIs, write to databases, trigger workflows, and spawn sub-agents to complete tasks autonomously. When your integration layer is fragile, a human-in-the-loop system produces bad suggestions. An agentic system produces bad actions. That is a different category of risk.

An October 2025 Zapier enterprise survey found **78% of enterprises struggle to connect AI tools with existing systems**. That statistic, troubling in a copilot world, becomes a serious governance problem in an agentic one. An agent operating across disconnected systems with inconsistent data contracts does not just underperform. It executes confidently on bad information.

Gartner's August 2025 data shows that less than 5% of enterprise applications feature AI agents today. Gartner predicts that figure reaches 40% by end of 2026. The timeline is compressed. Most organizations will be deploying agents before they have solved the integration problems that make agents safe to deploy.

Gartner's June 2025 analysis reinforces this directly: **over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027** due to escalating costs, unclear business value, or inadequate risk controls. That is not a prediction about AI capability. It is a prediction about engineering discipline. Organizations that skip the foundational work will build agents that cannot be trusted, cannot be audited, and cannot be explained when something goes wrong.

## The 5% Who Are Winning Are Not Using Better Models

The companies pulling ahead are not doing so because they selected the right foundation model or hired more data scientists. BCG's "Widening AI Value Gap" report from September 2025 found that **AI leaders achieve 1.7x revenue growth and 3.6x three-year total shareholder return** compared to laggards.

The separating variable is not technology selection. It is execution discipline.

What the leaders share: clean, governed, accessible data. Integration layers built to support programmatic access, not just human workflows. Clear ownership of AI systems beyond the initial build. Monitoring and observability applied to AI outputs the same way they are applied to any production system. Engineering practices extended to cover the model as a component, not a black box that someone else owns.

This is not sophisticated. It is foundational. The gap between leaders and laggards in AI is, at its core, an engineering maturity gap.

---

## Technical Debt Will Compound Faster Than You Think

Here is the uncomfortable arithmetic.

A 2025 Cognizant study projects that **79% of enterprises will retire less than half of their technology debt by 2030**. Most organizations already know their legacy architecture is a constraint. What they underestimate is how much AI amplifies that constraint.

Legacy systems slow down human-paced development. They cripple AI-paced development. And they become existential risks in agentic architectures, where an autonomous system needs reliable, well-documented integration points to operate safely.

The organizations deprioritizing infrastructure work today, in favor of standing up more AI pilots, are making a specific bet: that they can scale AI delivery later, after they get buy-in, without first addressing the foundation. Most will lose that bet. Not because AI is hard. Because you cannot build a ten-storey structure on a one-storey foundation and expect it to hold.

## Fix the Plumbing First. Then Deploy the AI.

The path forward is not complicated. It requires discipline.

Start with a data readiness assessment: inventory what you have, where it lives, how it is governed, and what integration work is required to make it accessible to AI systems. Be honest about the gaps. The 7% AI-ready statistic is a ceiling, not an average — most organizations are further from ready than they believe.

Build integration with agents in mind, not just copilots. If your AI strategy currently relies on tools that sit beside your workflows rather than inside them, that architecture will not survive the agentic transition. Design integration layers that support programmatic access, event-driven triggers, and robust error handling. An agent that cannot gracefully handle a failed API call is not ready for production.

Apply engineering discipline to AI the way you apply it to any production system. That means observability: log what the model receives, what it produces, and what downstream action results. It means testing: define expected behaviors and build regression suites. It means ownership: someone is accountable for this system after the launch celebration.

And address technical debt with urgency proportional to your AI ambitions. Organizations projecting aggressive AI roadmaps while deferring infrastructure investment are not being bold. They are accumulating risk they have not yet priced.

---

## The Question Is Not Whether You're Ready for AI

The question isn't whether your organization wants to deploy AI. Everyone does. The question is whether your engineering foundation can support AI that actually works in production: at scale, with governance, under load, and with agents that can be trusted to act without a human reviewing every output.

Most cannot. Yet.

That is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to sequence correctly: data, integration, observability, then scale. The organizations building that foundation now are the ones still deploying AI in 2027 while others are busy explaining canceled agentic projects to their boards.

Ask yourself this: if you doubled the number of AI agents running in your environment tomorrow, would your infrastructure hold? Would you know what they were doing? Would you be able to stop them if something went wrong?

If the answer to any of those is uncertain, you know where to start.
