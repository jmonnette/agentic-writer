# AI Helped Write This Article. Does That Make It AI Slop?
**Draft**: FINAL
**Date**: 2026-04-19
**Status**: Publication ready

---

You know something, but you can't explain it yet. Not to yourself, not to anyone else.

The knowledge is real. Sitting there, fully formed in some pre-verbal layer of your thinking, the product of years of pattern recognition, client work, and hard-won experience. But when you try to put it into words, something goes wrong. What arrives on the page feels flatter than what's in your head. The precision drains out. The articulation fails the idea.

This is the problem of intuitive knowledge. It's more common among experienced practitioners than most people admit.

The people who've spent a decade inside a problem domain often have the deepest insights and the hardest time expressing them. Michael Polanyi called this tacit knowledge: we know more than we can tell. The insight precedes the argument. The felt-sense of the answer arrives before the evidence, before the framing, before the structure. We know the conclusion is true. We've watched it play out too many times to doubt it. But we can't yet construct the argument that would convince someone else.

That gap between knowing and articulating has always been the challenging part of writing. Writing, properly understood, is not recording thoughts you've already completed. It's the process of discovering what you actually think.

The emergence of large language models as thought partners has changed how that discovery process works.

---

Last year, my clients started asking a question I'd been hearing in variations for months: "I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?"

I knew the answer. Immediately, intuitively, with the specific irritation of someone being asked to explain why water is wet. The question was a category error. Comparing a solo weekend build to enterprise software delivery is comparing a bicycle sprint to a freight rail operation. They don't share a constraint structure.

But that felt-knowledge alone doesn't produce an explanation. What I had was a practitioner's intuition and a conviction that the conventional framing was wrong. What I needed was an argument.

Partnering with Claude to write an article on the topic helped turn a feeling into a defensible case. The process began with me articulating what I tacitly knew. Claude helped translate this into structured prose, pulling in the supporting evidence I knew existed but hadn't compiled: DORA metrics on change approval processes, Atlassian's State of DevEx data showing developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding, Stripe's Developer Coefficient report on technical debt burden.

Claude didn't formulate the original idea. I did. Claude helped by translating my idea into words that would help others understand. It did in minutes what would have taken me hours, but the speed is incidental. The argument is what matters. A conviction I'd held for years was finally made legible to someone other than me. Claude helped bring into the world something useful that would not have otherwise existed.

---

The words feel like yours.

Not approximately yours. Not "close enough." Yours. Specific and recognizable, the same way you'd recognize a paraphrase of something you'd said aloud and someone had played back accurately.

The dominant cultural narrative treats that feeling as a warning sign. The concern: if you can't tell what you wrote from what the AI wrote, you've lost authorship. You've produced "AI slop." You should feel uneasy.

That concern is a category error.

"AI slop" is real. It exists when AI generates ideas the author does not hold, in a voice the author has not developed, on a topic the author has not thought about. The output is hollow because the input was hollow. No translation happening — only fabrication.

But when the input is your lived knowledge, your practical experience, your genuine conviction about how something works: the AI isn't generating. It's translating. The output feels like yours because it is built from yours. The raw material was your intuition. The AI gave it structure and surface. The argument, the claim, the underlying understanding were already there; AI just made them accessible to others.

Feeling that the words are yours is not a red flag. It is confirmation the process worked.

---

The "AI slop" critique, applied wholesale to all AI-assisted writing, confuses the tool with the input. A translation is not an original. But a bad translation can fail the source, and a good translation can honor it. The quality of the output depends almost entirely on the quality of what was brought to the process.

What can make AI-assisted writing hollow isn't the AI. It's the absence of a real point of view before the session begins. The test isn't confidence. It's whether you can be challenged on it. Tacit knowledge survives pushback. Calcified belief doesn't.

Sitting down with a vague assignment and asking AI to generate something compelling about a topic you haven't thought hard about will result in AI slop. The machine fills a vacuum. But come with a decade of observations, a specific argument you've been struggling to articulate clearly, a practitioner's irritation at the way conventional wisdom gets something wrong, and you are not asking for generation. You are asking for translation. Those are not the same request, and they do not produce the same result.

Before dismissing something as AI slop, the real question isn't whether AI wrote it. It's whether the author had something worth saying before they started.
