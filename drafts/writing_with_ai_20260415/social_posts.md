## LinkedIn

### Primary

There's a difference between asking AI to generate an idea and asking it to translate one you already hold. Most critics treat these as the same request. They're not.

"AI slop" is real. It exists when AI generates ideas the author does not hold, in a voice the author has not developed, on a topic the author has not thought about. The input is hollow. The output is hollow.

But when the input is lived knowledge, practical experience, and a genuine conviction about how something works, the AI is not generating. It is translating. The output feels like yours because it is built from yours.

Michael Polanyi called this the problem of tacit knowledge: we know more than we can tell. Experienced practitioners often have the deepest insights and the hardest time expressing them. The gap between knowing and articulating has always been the hard part of writing. AI can close that gap without replacing the knowledge that has to come first.

There is a simple test. Tacit knowledge survives pushback. Calcified belief does not.

Before dismissing something as AI slop, the real question is not whether AI wrote it. It is whether the author had something worth saying before they started.

### Alternate

The dominant narrative treats AI-assisted writing as a threat to authorship. That framing is wrong, but not for the reason most defenders of AI writing claim.

The quality of AI-assisted writing does not depend on whether you used AI. It depends on what you brought to the session before you opened the chat window.

Come with nothing to say and AI will generate something that sounds like an argument. That is AI slop, and the critics are right to call it out. Come with a decade of observations, a specific conviction, and a practitioner's irritation at the way conventional wisdom gets something wrong, and you are not asking for generation. You are asking for translation.

Those are not the same request. They do not produce the same result.

The test that separates them is simple: tacit knowledge survives pushback. If you can defend the argument when someone challenges it, the knowledge was real before the AI touched it. If the argument collapses under a single hard question, the AI was doing all the work.

Authorship is not about who typed the words. It is about who held the understanding.
