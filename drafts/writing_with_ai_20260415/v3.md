# Writing With AI Is Translation, Not Generation
**Draft**: FINAL
**Date**: 2026-04-18
**Status**: Publication ready

---

You know something. You can't explain it yet. Not to yourself, not to anyone else.

The knowledge is real. Sitting there, fully formed in some pre-verbal layer of your thinking, the product of years of pattern recognition, client work, and hard-won experience. But when you try to put it into words, something goes wrong. What arrives on the page feels flatter than what's in your head. The precision drains out. The articulation fails the idea.

This is the problem of intuitive knowledge. It's more common among experienced practitioners than most people admit.

The people who've spent a decade inside a problem domain often have the deepest insights and the hardest time expressing them. Michael Polanyi called this tacit knowledge: we can know more than we can tell. The insight precedes the argument. The felt-sense of the answer arrives before the evidence, before the framing, before the structure. We know the conclusion is true. We've watched it play out too many times to doubt it. But we can't yet construct the argument that would convince someone else.

That gap between knowing and articulating has always been the real work of writing. Writing, properly understood, is not recording thoughts you've already completed. It's the process of discovering what you actually think.

What AI changed is how that process works.

---

Last year, my clients started asking a question I'd been hearing in variations for months: "I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?"

I knew the answer. Immediately, intuitively, with the specific irritation of someone being asked to explain why water is wet. The question was a category error. Comparing a solo weekend build to enterprise software delivery is comparing a bicycle sprint to a freight rail operation. They don't share a constraint structure.

But that felt-knowledge alone doesn't produce an article. What I had was a practitioner's intuition and a conviction that the conventional framing was wrong. What I needed was an argument.

Writing "Know Your Constraint: Why Vibe Coding Can't Overcome the Enterprise Tax" with AI is where the distinction becomes concrete. The process began with me articulating what I already believed — and then translating that into structured prose, pulling in the supporting evidence I knew existed but hadn't compiled: DORA metrics on change approval processes, Atlassian's State of DevEx data showing developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding, Stripe's Developer Coefficient report on technical debt burden.

The AI didn't know those things applied. I knew they applied. The AI translated my knowing into an argument.

I can't hand you an independent transcript of what happened. But the article exists, the arguments hold, and the data checks out. The output can be evaluated on its own merits.

---

The words feel like yours.

Not approximately yours. Not "close enough." Yours. Specific and recognizable, the same way you'd recognize a paraphrase of something you'd said aloud and someone had played back accurately.

The dominant cultural narrative treats that feeling as a warning sign. The concern: if you can't tell what you wrote from what the AI wrote, you've lost authorship. You've produced "AI slop." You should feel uneasy.

That concern is a category error.

"AI slop" is real. It exists when AI generates ideas the author does not hold, in a voice the author has not developed, on a topic the author has not thought about. The output is hollow because the input was hollow. No translation happening — only fabrication.

But when the input is your lived knowledge, your practitioner experience, your genuine conviction about how something works: the AI isn't generating. It's translating. The output feels like yours because it is built from yours. The raw material was your intuition. The AI gave it structure and surface. The argument, the claim, the underlying understanding: that was already there.

Feeling that the words are yours is not a red flag. It is confirmation the process worked.

---

The "AI slop" critique, applied wholesale to all AI-assisted writing, confuses the tool with the input. A translation is not an original. But a bad translation can fail the source, and a good translation can honor it. The quality of the output depends almost entirely on the quality of what was brought to the process.

What makes AI-assisted writing hollow isn't the AI. It's the absence of a real point of view before the session begins.

Sit down with a vague assignment and ask AI to generate something compelling about a topic you haven't thought hard about, and you will get AI slop. The machine fills a vacuum. But come with a decade of observations, a specific argument you've been unable to articulate, a practitioner's irritation at the way conventional wisdom gets something wrong, and you are not asking for generation. You are asking for translation. Those are not the same request, and they do not produce the same result.

The criticism lands on real behavior. It just misidentifies which behavior.

---

The real question isn't whether AI wrote it. It's whether there was something real to translate.

That is the diagnostic to carry into every session: not "am I using AI?" but "do I have something specific to say?" If yes — if the intuitive knowledge is there, genuine, waiting to be structured — the tool is available to do what it does well.

If the answer is no, the tool will faithfully translate your emptiness into sentences. That's on you, not the machine.

Start there.
