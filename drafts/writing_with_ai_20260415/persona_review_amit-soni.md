## Reading Experience

### What Worked

**The Translation Frame Is Defensible**

The core distinction — AI as translator of pre-existing knowledge versus AI as generator of hollow content — is real and underused. Most discourse about AI writing conflates these two modes completely. The framing that "AI slop" is a function of input quality, not tool category, is a structural argument worth making. I agree with it.

> "Sitting down with a vague assignment and asking AI to generate something compelling about a topic you haven't thought hard about will result in AI slop. The machine fills a vacuum."

This is correct. I have seen this in PE portfolio companies where teams ask AI to produce strategy documents they have not thought through. The output looks like a strategy document. It has none of the substance.

**The Polanyi Citation Is Earned**

Citing Michael Polanyi's "The Tacit Dimension" and then connecting it to a practitioner's lived experience is the right move. This is not name-dropping. The author is using the framework accurately. That is not common.

**The Embedded Example Has Real Specificity**

The vibe coding scenario — clients asking why enterprise teams can't ship as fast as a solo weekend build — is a genuine practitioner observation. The author had a real insight. The article is at its strongest in this section.

### What Didn't Work

**The Argument Stops Where It Gets Interesting**

The article establishes that translation is different from generation. Fine. It does not then answer the harder question: what conditions in an organization or in a practitioner's development make high-quality translation possible? What separates someone who can use AI to translate effectively from someone who cannot?

The author says: come with a decade of observations and a specific argument. That is the precondition. But what does developing that precondition look like? What is the pathway? The article declares the existence of the gap but does not give me a framework for closing it.

> "Come with a decade of observations, a specific argument you've been struggling to articulate clearly, a practitioner's irritation at the way conventional wisdom gets something wrong, and you are not asking for generation."

This is descriptive. It is not operational. I cannot give this to a portfolio company and say: here is how to develop the input quality that makes AI translation valuable.

**No Quantification of the Value**

The author says Claude "did in minutes what would have taken me hours." That is a productivity claim. I want the math. Hours to what? What is the output delta? If AI compresses a 12-hour drafting process into 90 minutes, that is interesting but it is still a task thinking argument — you are going faster at the same thing. The article claims this is more than productivity, but it never proves it. It asserts that translation brings "something useful into the world that would not have otherwise existed." That is an existence claim, not a value claim. What did it make possible downstream? Who read the enterprise tax article? What decisions did it influence?

The math does not close.

**The Critique of "AI Slop" Is Handled Too Cleanly**

> "Before dismissing something as AI slop, the real question isn't whether AI wrote it. It's whether the author had something worth saying before they started."

I agree with this. But it does not address the harder version of the objection: what about practitioners who believe they have something worth saying but do not? The translation frame assumes the practitioner's self-assessment is reliable. In my experience running transformation programs, it often is not. Executives who have been in the same domain for 20 years can have deeply calcified beliefs dressed up as tacit knowledge. AI-assisted translation of bad intuition is still bad output. The article does not acknowledge this risk.

### What Confused Me

**The Audience Is Unclear**

Is this written for practitioners who want permission to use AI for writing? For skeptics who need the "slop" objection reframed? For writers? For executives? The Polanyi citation suggests an intellectually serious reader. The vibe coding example suggests a technology practitioner. The conclusion sounds like reassurance to someone who already uses AI and feels guilty about it. These are three different articles trying to coexist.

## Key Concerns and Objections

**Concern 1: Translation Requires Calibration the Article Ignores**

The translation frame is compelling, but translation is a skill. A bad translator produces a bad translation even when the source text is excellent. The article implies that if your input quality is high, the output quality follows. That is not how this works. Prompt design, iteration discipline, knowing when to override the AI's framing — these are real capabilities. The article treats them as invisible.

**Concern 2: This Is Still Largely a Productivity Argument**

The author gets the biggest lift from the claim that AI makes tacit knowledge accessible — which is a knowledge creation argument. But the embedded example is about article writing speed. Writing faster is productivity. The article never fully separates those two claims. If the real argument is that AI enables knowledge externalisation that would not otherwise happen, that is a systems-level claim. Prove it with a case where the article would not have been written at all, and show what the article changed downstream. Right now, the author proves the weaker version.

**Unanswered Questions**

- What is the failure rate of this process? When does high-input-quality still produce low-output-quality, and why?
- If translation is the right frame, what is the training program for developing better translators? What does that look like inside an organization?
- The author mentions Claude specifically. What about the translation breaks down when the practitioner cannot describe their own model clearly? What is the minimum viable level of self-knowledge required?

---

## Missing Elements

**A Framework, Not Just a Frame**

The translation metaphor is a reframe. It is not a framework. A framework would give me: here are the conditions under which AI translation succeeds; here are the inputs required; here are the failure modes. Right now I have a persuasive metaphor and one worked example.

**The Organizational Dimension**

I run AI programs inside companies. Writing is one surface. The more important surface is knowledge transfer at scale — can teams translate institutional knowledge through AI? Can junior practitioners accelerate toward expert-level judgment? The article stays at the individual practitioner level. That is a real limitation for my context.

**The Risk Side**

What does low-quality translation produce that is worse than no translation? A confident-sounding, well-structured article built on faulty intuition is more dangerous than a clumsy article. The article should acknowledge this and give practitioners a quality test.

---

## Assumptions I Don't Share

**Assumption 1: The Practitioner Knows What They Know**

The article assumes that experienced practitioners have reliable access to their own tacit knowledge. In my work, the gap is often not between knowing and articulating — it is between believing you know and actually knowing. AI makes overconfidence faster, not just communication faster.

**Assumption 2: The Problem Is Expression, Not Evidence**

The author frames the core problem as the gap between knowing and articulating. Sometimes the problem is that the practitioner has not done the evidence work and AI helps them paper over that gap rather than close it. The article gestures at this with the "AI slop" warning but resolves it too quickly.

## Overall Assessment

**Would I Finish Reading?**: Yes

**Why**: The argument is clean enough to follow and specific enough to engage. I disagreed with parts of it, which is more valuable than agreeing with nothing. The Polanyi frame earned my attention and the vibe coding example was a genuine practitioner observation, not a consultant's hypothetical.

**What I'd Take Away**:
- The translation/generation distinction is a useful reframe I will probably use in my own work
- The "slop is a function of input quality" argument is defensible and I will cite it
- The article underdelivers on its strongest implied claim — that this is about knowledge creation, not productivity

**Would I Share This?**: Maybe, with a note

**With Whom**: Other practitioners who are writing about AI and feel awkward about their process. Not portfolio company leaders — they need the organizational version of this argument, which does not exist yet.

**Why/Why Not**: The argument is good enough to share, but it stops short of the version that would make it genuinely useful to operators. I would share it with the caveat that the harder questions remain open.

**Impact on My Thinking**: Reinforces something I already believed. Does not change my view but gives me better language for it.

## Recommendations for This Audience

### Critical Changes

1. **Quantify the value claim or drop it**: If AI made the article possible that would not have otherwise existed, prove it. Show what the article changed. If this is a productivity argument, own that — productivity is still valuable. But do not claim systems-level impact with task-level evidence.

2. **Address the self-assessment problem**: The practitioner must assess their own input quality. That assessment is often wrong. The article needs at least one paragraph on how to know whether your tacit knowledge is actually reliable versus calcified.

### Helpful Improvements

- Add a failure mode: describe what a high-input, poor-translation session looks like and why it fails. This would make the framework actionable rather than just inspirational.
- Name the organizational version of the argument. Even one sentence acknowledging that this applies differently at team scale would make this more transferable.

### Optional Enhancements

- A simple diagnostic: three questions a practitioner should answer before starting an AI-assisted writing session. This would convert the frame into a tool.
