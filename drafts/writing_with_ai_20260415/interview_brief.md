# Interview Brief: Writing With AI
**Date**: 2026-04-15
**Topic**: How writing with AI works — specifically the process of translating intuitive, hard-to-articulate knowledge into published articles

---

## The Core Idea
**Main Argument**: Writing with AI is not about generating content — it is about translating knowledge you already hold intuitively into a form that can be shared. The AI is the translator, not the author.

**Angle**: Most discourse about "AI writing" focuses on authenticity and originality as threats. This piece inverts that frame: the surprising discovery is that the output feels genuinely yours, because the AI is surfacing what you already knew. That feeling of recognition — not detection anxiety — is the defining experience of writing this way.

**Origin**: A firsthand practice of using AI to write articles across topics like enterprise software delivery, AI adoption culture, and engineering leadership. The process prompted a meta-question: what is actually happening when this works well?

---

## Audience & Stakes
**Primary Reader**: Knowledge workers, leaders, and practitioners who write as part of their professional identity — but who are skeptical of AI writing tools because they equate AI-generated text with "AI slop" or inauthenticity.

**Why Now**: The "AI slop" narrative is dominant and chilling. People who could benefit most from this process are opting out because they misunderstand what the process actually is. The moment to correct the frame is now, while the skepticism is still active and before it calcifies.

**Desired Outcome**: The reader finishes with a new mental model — writing with AI as translation, not generation — and feels permission to try the process without compromising their sense of authorship.

**Anticipated Pushback**: "If AI is doing the writing, it isn't really your voice." The article needs to address this directly. The counter is that the AI reflects your articles, your arguments, and your patterns back at you — the words feel like yours because they are built from yours.

## Substance & Signals

**Key Examples / Data**:
- The author's own article library (in `/library`) serves as the primary evidence base. These articles were produced through this process and demonstrate the output quality.
- Strongest concrete example: "Know Your Constraint: Why Vibe Coding Can't Overcome the Enterprise Tax." That article began with an intuitive frustration — clients asking why their teams can't ship as fast as a weekend vibe-coder — which the author knew the answer to but couldn't fully articulate. The article translated that felt-knowledge into a structured argument backed by DORA metrics, Atlassian DevEx data, and Stripe's Developer Coefficient report. The translation process is the story.
- The consistent discovery moment: when the AI-generated words appear on screen, they feel like the author's own. Not a surprise to be explained away — a signal that the process is working correctly.

**Controversial Claims**:
- Thinking intuitively means knowing something to be true but being unable to articulate it to yourself or to others. Writing with AI is how that gap closes.
- The feeling that AI-generated words are "yours" is not a red flag — it is the whole point.
- The "AI slop" concern is a category error: it applies to AI generating ideas you don't hold, not AI articulating ideas you do hold.

## Ghostwriter Guidance
- Lead with the translation frame immediately. Do not open with an AI writing provocation or a defensive disclaimer about authenticity. Open with the act of knowing something you can't yet say.
- The strongest opening hook is the phenomenology of intuitive knowledge: "You know something. You can't explain it yet — not to yourself, not to anyone else." Start there.
- The Vibe Coding article is the primary concrete example. Use it to show how a client conversation (an intuitive signal) became a structured argument with hard evidence. The article itself is the proof.
- The discovery moment — output feeling like your own words — should appear late in the piece as the payoff, not as reassurance up front. Let the reader arrive at it.
- Tone: practitioner reporting, not advocate pitching. Curious, direct, slightly contrarian. No evangelism.
- Address the "AI slop" objection head-on in the body of the piece, not in a footnote. Name it, then dismantle it with the category-error argument.
- Structure suggestion:
  1. The problem of intuitive knowledge (you know but can't say)
  2. What the translation process actually looks like (with Vibe Coding as the example)
  3. The discovery moment — why the output feels like yours
  4. The category error in the "AI slop" critique
  5. What this means for how practitioners should think about writing with AI
