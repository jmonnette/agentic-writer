## In Ethan's Words

**The hook.**

"You know something, but you can't explain it yet."

Yeah. That lands. That's the actual experience. I've sat in rooms with engineers who have fifteen years of delivery pattern-matching in their heads and cannot get it out in a form that moves anyone. This is a real problem and it's underdiagnosed. The opening earned my attention.

I kept reading.

---

**What works.**

The vibe coding anecdote is the best thing in the article. Not because it's a perfect example — I'll get to that — but because it's honest. "I knew the answer. Immediately, intuitively, with the specific irritation of someone being asked to explain why water is wet." That's a real sentence from someone who has actually been in the conversation. I recognize that irritation. That's the sentence that tells me the author has done the thing, not just thought about it.

The translation frame itself is good. It's not a novel idea — there are serious people who've written about AI as thought partner, AI as writing tool, etc. — but "translation, not generation" is a clean, defensible distinction that does real work. It gives people a mental model they can use to evaluate their own process. That's worth something.

The Polanyi reference earns its place. Tacit knowledge is the actual mechanism being described here, and naming it correctly shows the author has done more than skim the surface of the concept. It would have been easy to just say "gut feel" and move on.

The "AI slop" reframe is sharp. The argument — that slop comes from hollow input, not from AI involvement — is correct, and the way it's structured is clean: define the real problem, draw the distinction, close with the actual diagnostic question. "It's whether the author had something worth saying before they started." That's the line. That's the whole article in a sentence.

---

**Where my skepticism fires.**

The vibe coding example does the argument a disservice, and I want to be direct about this.

You're writing an article about tacit knowledge — about the deep, experience-built pattern recognition that experienced practitioners struggle to articulate. That's a sophisticated, substantive claim. The example you use to illustrate AI-as-translation is: I used Claude to help write an article about vibe coding.

That's a fine example of AI assistance. But it's not a particularly hard knowledge translation problem. Writing an article is a writing problem. The tacit knowledge being translated there — "vibe coding doesn't map to enterprise delivery" — is something most thoughtful practitioners in this space could articulate in a conversation. You didn't need fifteen years of pre-verbal pattern recognition to get there. You needed to sit down and write.

The gap between the claim and the example is real. The article sets up the hardest version of the problem — deep practitioner knowledge that resists articulation — and then demonstrates the solution with a relatively lightweight case. I kept waiting for the harder example. The one where the knowledge was genuinely resistant. The one where the author started with something truly inarticulate and the AI helped surface something that wouldn't have come out otherwise. That example isn't here.

This matters because the people who most need this argument are the ones with the most deeply embedded tacit knowledge — the thirty-year engineers, the delivery leads who have seen every failure mode but can't write a post-mortem that teaches anyone anything. What does this process look like for them? You've told me it worked for a relatively articulate author writing about a topic they understood well. I want to know if it works when the translation problem is actually hard.

---

**The thing that's missing.**

You describe what translation-mode AI writing feels like from the author's side. You do not tell me what the process actually is.

Walk me through it. Where does it start? Do you dump a brain dump into the prompt? Do you have a conversation? Do you write badly on purpose and let the AI reorganize it? Do you give it examples of your prior writing so it knows your voice? What does the session look like at minute one and what does it look like at minute thirty?

This is the operational gap in the article. You've made a compelling case for why this kind of writing is legitimate. You haven't given anyone a clear path to doing it. "Come with a decade of observations" is not a process description. The reader who is convinced by your argument still doesn't know what to do Monday morning.

I understand this might be a scope choice — maybe you're writing the "why" article and saving the "how" for another one. If so, say that. But right now the article ends at the point where it should start getting useful.

---

**The concern I keep running into.**

The article makes a strong argument for experienced practitioners. It's essentially telling people with genuine accumulated expertise: don't let cultural anxiety about AI stop you from using it to get your knowledge out of your head and into the world. That argument is correct and it's worth making.

But it doesn't acknowledge the failure mode in the other direction. What stops someone with shallow knowledge from convincing themselves they have tacit insight and using this frame to justify producing slop? "I have practitioner intuition" is a very easy thing for someone with three years of experience to believe about themselves. The article sets up the distinction — translation vs. generation — but relies entirely on author self-assessment to enforce it.

In my experience, the people most likely to mistake AI generation for AI translation are exactly the people who shouldn't be doing that. The article gives them a convenient rationalization. You could argue that's not your problem to solve. But the article would be stronger with one honest sentence acknowledging that this frame requires the author to be brutally honest with themselves about whether they're actually bringing something.

**Would I finish it?**

Did finish it. It's short and it earns the read.

**What I'd take away.**

The translation/generation distinction is a useful frame and I'll use it. I may share this with people who are paralyzed by the authenticity question around AI writing. The argument that hollowness comes from hollow input, not from AI involvement, is correct and worth repeating.

**Would I share it?**

Yes. With a specific audience: people who have expertise and are hesitating to write because they're worried that using AI makes the output inauthentic. For that person, this article is exactly right.

I would not share it with someone who is trying to figure out whether they should use AI-assisted writing at all, because the operational question — what does this actually look like in practice — isn't answered.

**Would I push back?**

On the example gap, yes. I'd tell you the vibe coding anecdote is too easy and you know it. Give me the hard case.

---

## Bottom Line

This is good work. The core argument is clean, defensible, and true. The Polanyi frame is correct. The reframe of AI slop is the sharpest moment in the article and it will stick.

The gaps are real: the example undersells the claim, the process is missing, and the failure mode in the other direction deserves one honest sentence.

But the instinct is right. You're asking the right question — not "did AI write this?" but "did the author have something to say?" — and you've written it in a way that is tight, honest, and grounded in your actual experience.

I'd share this. Not because it's complete. Because it's asking the right question.
