# Writing With AI Is Translation, Not Generation
**Draft**: v1
**Date**: 2026-04-18
**Status**: First draft — awaiting critique

---

You know something. You can't explain it yet — not to yourself, not to anyone else.

The knowledge is real. It's sitting there, fully formed in some pre-verbal layer of your thinking, the product of years of pattern recognition, client work, and hard-won experience. But when you try to put it into words, something goes wrong. What arrives on the page feels flatter than what's in your head. The precision drains out. The articulation fails the idea.

This is the problem of intuitive knowledge. And it's more common among experienced practitioners than most people admit.

The people who've spent a decade inside a problem domain often have the deepest insights and the hardest time expressing them. The insight precedes the argument. The felt-sense of the answer arrives before the evidence, before the framing, before the structure. We know the conclusion is true — we've watched it play out too many times to doubt it — but we cannot yet construct the argument that would convince someone else. Sometimes we can't even convince ourselves.

That gap between knowing and articulating has always been the real work of writing. Writing, properly understood, is not recording thoughts you've already completed. It's the process of discovering what you actually think.

What AI changed is how that process works.

---

Last year, my clients started asking a question I'd been hearing in variations for months: "I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?"

I knew the answer. I knew it the way you know things that have been confirmed by too many engagements to count — immediately, intuitively, with the specific irritation of someone being asked to explain why water is wet. The question was based on a category error. Comparing a solo weekend build to enterprise software delivery is comparing a bicycle sprint to a freight rail operation. They don't share a constraint structure.

But that felt-knowledge alone doesn't produce an article. What I had was a practitioner's intuition and a strong conviction that the conventional framing was wrong. What I needed was an argument.

Writing "Know Your Constraint: Why Vibe Coding Can't Overcome the Enterprise Tax" with AI is where the distinction I want to make becomes concrete. The process did not begin with the AI generating ideas I didn't hold. It began with the AI asking me to articulate what I already believed — and then translating that articulation into structured prose, pulling in the supporting evidence I knew existed but hadn't compiled: DORA metrics on change approval processes, Atlassian's State of DevEx data showing developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding, Stripe's Developer Coefficient report on technical debt burden.

The AI didn't know those things applied. I knew they applied. The AI translated my knowing into an argument.

That distinction matters more than people give it credit for.

---

Here's what the translation process actually feels like from the inside. You begin with a prompt that is less a question and more a forcing function — an attempt to externalize what you already believe. You write toward the AI, not at it. The output comes back, and something happens that I suspect most people who've had this experience recognize but haven't named.

The words feel like yours.

Not approximately yours. Not "close enough." Yours — in a way that is specific and recognizable, the same way you'd recognize a paraphrase of something you'd said aloud and someone had played back accurately.

The dominant cultural narrative right now treats that feeling as a warning sign. The concern goes: if you can't tell what you wrote from what the AI wrote, you've lost authorship. You've produced "AI slop." You should feel uneasy about what you're doing.

That concern is a category error.

"AI slop" is a real phenomenon. It exists when AI is used to generate ideas the author does not hold, in a voice the author has not developed, on a topic the author has not thought about. The output is hollow because the input was hollow. There was no translation happening — only fabrication.

But that is not the only way to use these tools. When the input is your lived knowledge, your practitioner experience, your genuine and specific conviction about how something works — the AI isn't generating. It's translating. The output feels like yours because it is built from yours. The raw material was your intuition. The AI gave it structure and surface. The argument, the claim, the underlying understanding: that was already there.

Feeling that the words are yours is not a red flag. It is confirmation the process worked.

---

The "AI slop" critique, applied wholesale to all AI-assisted writing, confuses the tool with the input. A transcript is not a speech. A translation is not an original. But a bad translation can fail the source, and a good translation can honor it. The quality of the output depends almost entirely on the quality and specificity of what was brought to the process.

What makes AI-assisted writing hollow isn't the AI. It's the absence of a real point of view before the session begins.

If you sit down with a vague assignment and ask AI to generate something compelling about a topic you haven't thought hard about, you will get AI slop. The machine is filling a vacuum. But if you come with a decade of observations, a specific argument you've been unable to articulate, a practitioner's irritation at the way conventional wisdom gets something wrong — you are not asking for generation. You are asking for translation. Those are not the same request, and they do not produce the same result.

The criticism lands on real behavior. It just misidentifies which behavior. The problem is never the tool. The problem is using the tool in the absence of something real to translate.

---

This matters for how practitioners — people who write as part of their professional identity — should actually think about the question.

The authenticity concern is legitimate in one direction and completely backwards in another. Legitimate: if you are using AI to hold opinions you don't hold, argue positions you haven't examined, or produce work that has no relationship to your actual understanding of a domain — that is a problem. Not primarily an ethical problem, though it is that too. A practical one. The work will not hold up under scrutiny. The ideas will be generic. The specific friction of real expertise will be absent.

But that concern should not extend to the act of using AI to articulate things you genuinely know. Closing the gap between felt-knowledge and expressed argument is not inauthenticity. It's the actual work of writing, accomplished through a different process.

Practitioners who are chilling on this because of "AI slop" anxiety are applying the right standard to the wrong situation. The standard should be: does the output reflect what you actually know, believe, and have experienced? If yes, the process is working. If no, the process is the problem — and the tool is incidental.

---

The real question, then, isn't whether AI wrote it. It's whether there was something real to translate.

That is the diagnostic practitioners should carry into every session: not "am I using AI?" but "do I have something specific to say?" If the answer is yes — if the intuitive knowledge is there, accumulated, genuine, waiting to be structured — the tool is available to do what it does well.

If the answer is no, the tool will faithfully translate your emptiness into sentences. That's on you, not the machine.

Start there.
