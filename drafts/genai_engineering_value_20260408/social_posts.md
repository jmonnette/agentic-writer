### Primary

Your CFO is not asking how many story points your team completed last quarter.

She's asking whether the $4 million you spent on AI tools came back as more money. And most engineering leaders cannot answer that question. Not because the answer is bad. Because they're reporting the wrong units.

Here's the thing the industry won't say plainly: story points were never designed to answer a business question. They were designed to answer "can we finish this before Friday?" Somewhere in the Agile scaling era, that internal planning tool got promoted to a KPI. Executives treated it as a productivity proxy. Predictably, teams gamed it. The signal collapsed. The reporting habit stayed.

AI spending has turned up the pressure on this dysfunction to a point where it can no longer be ignored. A controlled trial by METR found experienced developers using AI tools on their own codebases estimated they'd complete tasks 20% faster, then actually took 19% longer. The finding isn't primarily about AI. It's about measurement: developers can't accurately self-assess their own productivity, which means output counts reported upward are doubly unreliable.

The organizations that prove engineering value through 2027 won't be the ones with the most sophisticated models or the largest tooling budgets. They'll be the ones that built measurement discipline first: into how requirements are written, how delivery is instrumented, how engineering value gets reported.

Not story points. Dollars. That's where this starts.

### Alternate

56% of CEOs report zero ROI from their AI investments. Boards are not becoming more patient.

Most engineering leaders I talk to know this is coming. They're preparing. And they're preparing with the wrong metrics.

Velocity. Throughput. Deployment frequency. Story points completed. These are engineer-units. They describe what the team did. They cannot be translated into what the business got. The unit conversion doesn't exist.

This is not a communication problem. It's a metrics architecture problem. AI has made it urgent in a way it wasn't before: every budget conversation now ends with someone asking for the dollar figure, and "we merged 98% more pull requests" is not that number.

There's a version of this that works. It requires three columns on a dashboard, a capacity math exercise your CFO can interrogate, and a deliberate decision about how recovered engineering time gets captured before it gets absorbed by scope expansion.

It also requires honest accounting of what AI is actually delivering. Per the best available research, productivity gains are highly variable and often considerably lower than the vendor case studies suggest.
