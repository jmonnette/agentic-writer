**Position**: AI Champions are change agents first, technical experts second. The right selection criterion is influence, not coding skill. Peer-driven adoption through Champions consistently outperforms top-down training programs.
**Confidence**: High

**Confidence**: High
**Source**: [[The AI Hype is Dead - Long Live AI]], [[A Healthy Culture is Your Secret Weapon for AI Adoption]]
**Quote**: "1&1 Telecommunications went from concept to production in 6 months through peer-driven adoption via Champions."
**Context**: The author returns to this point in multiple articles. The contrast is always between top-down mandates (which create resistance) and embedded peer advocates (which create pull). ## Recommendations for Ghostwriter

1. **Open with the 6% problem**: Only 6% of companies have scaled AI beyond pilots. The Guild is not a nice-to-have community — it is the organizational infrastructure that breaks the pilot-to-nothing pattern. Establish stakes immediately.

2. **Define the Guild as cultural infrastructure, not a technical committee**: Every design choice — who leads it, who belongs, what participation looks like — should reinforce that this is a culture-change vehicle. Do not let it drift toward tool governance or procurement.

3. **Answer the five article questions in this order**:
   - **Objectives first** (why it exists — break pilot purgatory, build the 70%, make enterprise AI feel like personal AI)
   - **Who belongs** (cross-functional T-shaped practitioners — not just engineers; enthusiastic adopters, not necessarily top performers)
   - **Who leads** (influential practitioner with dedicated bandwidth, not a technical architect or a committee; part-time at small organizations, director-level dedicated role at scale)
   - **What participation looks like** (cadenced knowledge sharing, failure-first culture, real project application, not another training program)
   - **What members get out of it** (build the wider horizontal bar, career differentiation, peer network, explicit cover to experiment)

4. **Use the enablement-vs-governance distinction as the organizing spine**: Every structural recommendation should be tested against this: "Does this make the Guild feel like an enablement community or a governance body?" If the latter, cut it.

5. **Extend the Champions concept cross-functionally without abandoning the engineering frame**: The author's established work is engineering-centric. Acknowledge this and explicitly broaden the Guild to include product, data, and operations practitioners — but do not pretend the engineering leadership audience is not the primary reader.

6. **Address the CoE relationship explicitly**: Practitioners reading this will ask "is this the same as the CoE Champions network?" Give a clear answer. Recommended framing: the Guild is the practitioner community; a CoE is the coordinating infrastructure that may or may not exist alongside it. They are complementary, not duplicative.

7. **Flag Canada-specific content for Researcher**: Any treatment of Canadian enterprise context (regulatory, cultural, bilingual workforce) requires fresh research. Do not extrapolate from US-centric library content.

8. **Avoid hype language**: The author's current register is post-hype and pragmatic. Avoid phrases like "AI revolution," "transformative potential," or "unlock the power of AI." Use instead: "sustainable adoption," "capability expansion," "practitioners," "the unglamorous work."

9. **Use a maturity progression**: Give leaders a 3- or 4-stage model for standing up and scaling a Guild (e.g., Seed → Structured → Scaled → Embedded). This mirrors the CoE maturity model and will feel consistent to readers familiar with that article.

10. **Close with urgency grounded in evidence, not FOMO**: The competitive advantage argument should be data-backed (BCG 10-20-70, 2.1x ROI, 2x scale). Avoid fear-based urgency; use evidence-based urgency. The author's voice is confident and analytical, not alarmist.
