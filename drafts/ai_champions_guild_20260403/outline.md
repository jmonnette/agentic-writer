# Outline: The AI Champions and Practitioners Guild
**Thesis**: A formal Champions and Practitioners Guild is the organizational infrastructure that converts isolated AI experiments into durable, enterprise-wide adoption — and the way you build it determines whether it becomes a cultural accelerant or another program nobody uses.
**Word Count Target**: 1,000–1,400 words
**Tone**: Pragmatic, direct, practitioner-facing. Post-hype register. Engineering leadership is the primary reader; product, data, and operations leaders are secondary.

---

## Opening (10% — ~120 words)

- **Hook**: Pattern 4 (Temporal Contrast) + negation-then-affirmation. Open with the familiar failure: AI pilot launches, some excitement, then... nothing. Only 4% of companies generating substantial AI value at scale (BCG). This isn't a technology problem. It's a people problem.
- **Context**: Brief frame — most enterprises respond to this with more tools, more training curricula, more governance bodies. None of those are the answer.
- **Thesis Statement**: The answer is embedded practitioners — a Champions and Practitioners Guild. Define it immediately as cultural infrastructure, not a committee.
- **Canada note**: One sentence acknowledging the US/Canada scope — the model applies in both markets; Canadian readers should note PIPEDA/Quebec Law 25 shapes how governance activities within the Guild get structured.

---

## Section 1: What the Guild Is — And What It Isn't (10% — ~120 words)

- **Main Point**: Define the Guild's identity by contrast. It is not a governance body, a tool selection committee, or a training program. It is the practitioner community layer — the connective tissue between top-down AI strategy and frontline work.
- **Relationship to CoE**: Briefly address the "is this the same as the CoE Champions network?" question directly. The Guild is the community; a CoE is the coordinating infrastructure that may or may not exist alongside it. Complementary, not duplicative.
- **BCG 10-20-70 framing**: This is the 70% — the people and process investment that drives 2.1x ROI. Budget framing to preempt the "why fund a community instead of buying tools?" objection.
- **Stance Alignment**: Enablement over governance. "Enablement builds trust and adoption. Governance builds resistance."
- **Key Transition**: Having defined what it is, walk through how to build it — starting with who belongs.

---

## Section 2: Who Belongs (15% — ~180 words)

- **Main Point**: The selection criterion is curiosity and willingness to help peers — not technical expertise. Connectors beat coders.
- **Evidence**:
  - CSO Online: "Invite employees who are curious about AI and interested in helping others — no technical expertise required. AI champions are connectors."
  - Cross-functional composition: operations, finance, HR, marketing, legal, technology. Citi's 4,200 AI Accelerators span all functions across 182,000 employees.
  - Opt-in vs. assigned: self-nomination or manager nomination with opt-in confirmation. Mandatory participation creates resentful obstacles, not advocates.
- **Practical benchmarks**: Aim for 5–10% of your initial AI user base. One champion lead per 10–20 champions (CSO Online / Lead with AI).
- **T-shaped professional connection**: The Guild is how practitioners build the wider horizontal bar — cross-functional exposure, not deeper specialization.
- **Stance Alignment**: Peer networks beat mandates. Augmentation, not replacement.
- **Key Transition**: Cross-functional composition raises the leadership question — who actually runs this?

---

## Section 3: Who Leads It — and Is It a Full-Time Job? (20% — ~240 words)

- **Main Point**: Two-tier structure. One near-full-time program lead; many part-time volunteer champions. The answer to "full-time?" is: yes for the lead, no for everyone else.
- **Tier 1 — Program Lead**:
  - Near-full-time or fully dedicated role, housed in CoE or CTO org.
  - Owns program design, metrics, communications, training resources, community health.
  - At small organizations: dedicated 50–80% of time. At Citi/PwC scale: a team.
  - This is not the CTO. It is a practitioner-leader with organizational credibility — not a coordinator who "fits it in."
- **Tier 2 — Individual Champions**:
  - Part-time volunteers: 30–60 minutes per week (GitHub benchmark).
  - Embedded in their home teams. Primary job remains primary.
  - Rotating quarterly membership keeps energy fresh, prevents burnout, prevents ownership consolidating in a small group.
- **Executive Sponsorship**:
  - A named executive sponsor is non-negotiable. Not ceremonial — active. McKinsey: only 28% of companies have direct CEO involvement in AI governance. That's the failure baseline.
  - Champions need something safe to champion. Approved tools, clear data policies, explicit use cases must exist before champions launch. Don't put advocates in front of questions they can't answer.
- **Stance Alignment**: Lean structures. CoE seeds expertise, doesn't hoard it. Full centralization defeats the purpose.
- **Key Transition**: Leadership structure is set. Now: what does day-to-day participation actually look like?

---

## Section 4: What Participation Looks Like (20% — ~240 words)

- **Main Point**: Participation is local, practical, and visible — not attending another all-hands webinar. The unit of impact is one workflow changed in one colleague's daily work.
- **Core activities**:
  - Run lunch-and-learns and practical workshops on real workflows (not AI theory)
  - Curate and share prompt libraries within the team's domain
  - Document use cases — what worked, what didn't, and why
  - Surface friction points upward to the program lead and CoE
  - Model responsible AI use publicly in their own work
  - Onboard new team members to approved tools
- **PwC example**: 3,200 champions hosting "prompting parties" — hands-on, peer-led sessions that build fluency through doing, not watching. 54% of PwC's global workforce now uses AI tools weekly.
- **Psychological safety design**: The Guild must create explicit cover for experimentation — including failures. Members share what didn't work, not just wins. "Failure budgets" in practice: designated time and no-blame reporting on experiments that flopped.
- **OKR-structured participation (trivago model)**: Champions activities tied to measurable outcomes — adoption rate, use cases documented, workshops run. Connects participation to visible organizational value.
- **Stance Alignment**: Peer-driven adoption. Psychological safety. The personal adoption paradox — enterprise AI that feels like personal AI.
- **Key Transition**: Understanding the participation model invites the practical question every potential champion asks: what's in it for me?

---

## Section 5: What Members Get Out of It (15% — ~180 words)

- **Main Point**: The value proposition for champions is unusually strong for a volunteer role. They are building the most commercially valuable skill set of the decade, in public, with institutional backing.
- **Evidence**:
  - AI fluency ahead of peers: Cambridge Spark frames the champion role as a formal career accelerator — "your expertise in AI will put you above others."
  - Career visibility: GitHub — "unique opportunities for skill development, increased visibility, and influence as the go-to technical leaders shaping their organizations' future."
  - Labor market signal: Microsoft (2024) — 71% of leaders prefer hiring less experienced candidates with AI skills over more experienced candidates without them. Champions are building exactly those skills.
  - Early access: champions get first look at new tools, first invitations to pilots, direct lines to CoE leadership.
  - Influence without authority: champions shape how governance policies become actual workflow — a meaningful organizational role most employees never have.
  - Peer network: internal community plus potential external connections (OpenAI Academy, Cambridge Spark alumni networks).
- **Stance Alignment**: T-shaped professionals. Augmentation, not replacement. Career differentiation.
- **Key Transition**: Lead into close with the "so what now?" synthesis.

---

## Conclusion (10% — ~120 words)

- **Synthesis Signal**: "The bottom line" or "Ultimately"
- **Core reframe**: Negation-then-affirmation structure. This isn't a community of practice for its own sake. It's the organizational muscle that determines whether AI investment compounds or evaporates.
- **BCG callback**: Organizations that invest in the 70% — people and process — achieve 2.1x ROI and scale 2x as many initiatives. The Guild is how you build that 70%.
- **Urgency without FOMO**: Only 4% of companies are generating substantial AI value at scale. The gap between organizations that build this infrastructure and those that don't is widening. Not alarmist — analytical.
- **Closing diagnostic prompt**: A "you" question to help leaders assess their own readiness. Something like: "Do you have a named program lead with dedicated time? If not, you don't have a champions program — you have a Slack channel."
- **Stance Alignment**: Post-hype pragmatism. Evidence-based urgency. Direct and slightly impatient with theater.

## Voice Considerations
- Register: Pragmatic, direct, slightly impatient with hype and theater. Respects practitioners. Names organizational dysfunction clearly.
- Negation-then-affirmation: deploy at opening ("This isn't a training problem") and closing ("This isn't a community for its own sake")
- Ultra-short sentences for punch after complex analytical sentences
- Avoid: "AI revolution," "transformative potential," "unlock the power of AI," governance, compliance, mandates, approval gates
- Preferred: sustainable adoption, capability expansion, practitioners, enablement, the unglamorous work, peer networks, cultural infrastructure
- Statistical density: 5–8 cited stats distributed across article
- Headers should do rhetorical work — declarative claims, not generic labels
- Canada note: one clean sentence flagging PIPEDA/Quebec Law 25 for Canadian readers without derailing the US-primary frame
