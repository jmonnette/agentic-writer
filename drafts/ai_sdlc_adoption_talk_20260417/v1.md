## Opening — The Honest Confession (~3 min)

Let me start with something most sessions like this won't say out loud.

Most "Manager's Guide to AI Adoption" content is not actually written for managers. It's written for C-suite audiences and then handed down to the people who have to execute it. Strip away the polished deck, and what you're left with is: "set the vision, create psychological safety, celebrate wins." That's real advice. It's also wildly incomplete when you're the one sitting in a sprint review trying to explain why the team's velocity didn't change despite six months of AI tooling investment.

This session is for that person. The delivery lead. The engagement manager. The engineering manager who is accountable to a client, accountable to a team, and trying to make something genuinely work — not just report that it's working.

[SPEAKER NOTE: Make eye contact here. Let this land. A lot of people in this room have felt the gap between the framework and the reality. Name it directly.]

Here's the number that should frame everything we talk about today. McKinsey's data shows 78% of companies now use AI in at least one business function. Only 6% have scaled it broadly. That gap — 78 to 6 — is not a technology problem. The tools work. The models are real. The demos are impressive.

That gap is a people problem. An organizational problem. And increasingly, a manager problem.

That's what we're here to talk about.

## Section 1 — The Maturity Map: What Each Level Actually Looks Like (~8 min)

## SLIDE: The AI SDLC Maturity Model (L0–L3)

Before we can talk about movement, we need a shared map.

The AI SDLC maturity model has four levels. I'll give you the textbook version first, then I'll tell you what each one actually feels like from inside a team.

**L0 — No AI use.** The textbook version: no AI tools in the workflow, no experimentation, no adoption. The real version: it's not that nobody knows about AI. It's that nobody has been given permission to try it — or nobody knows what "trying it" even looks like in the context of real work. L0 is mostly a permission problem dressed up as a knowledge problem.

**L1 — Individual, ad hoc AI use.** Textbook: individual contributors use AI tools on their own initiative, inconsistently, without shared practices. Real version: you have a handful of people quietly using Copilot or Claude on their own time, getting real value from it, and keeping it mostly to themselves — because they're not sure if they're supposed to, or because they don't want to be held to a standard they set for themselves. L1 is productivity in the shadows.

[SPEAKER NOTE: Pause here. Ask the room: "How many of you think your team is in L1 right now?" This is often where people realize their "L2 team" is actually L1.]

**L2 — Team-level, consistent AI use in practices.** Textbook: AI use is embedded in team rituals — code review, standups, planning, testing. Real version: the team has agreed on what they use AI for, when, and how. There are norms. Not mandates — norms. Somebody has said out loud, "Here's how we use AI on this team," and others have adopted it because it actually helps. The tell for L2 is that new team members get onboarded into AI practices the same way they get onboarded into coding standards.

**L3 — AI-native practices embedded in the SDLC.** Textbook: AI is not a bolt-on; it's architecturally present in how the team ships software. Real version: the team designs for AI from the start. Requirements capture considers what AI can validate. Test strategies include AI-assisted coverage analysis. The team has stopped asking "can AI help here?" and started asking "why wouldn't AI be involved?" L3 is rare. Most enterprise teams are nowhere near it. And that's okay — because L3 is not the goal for this session. Getting unstuck from L1 is.

## Section 2 — Transitions: What Actually Moves Teams (~10 min)

## SLIDE: The Transition Problem

Knowing the levels is easy. Moving between them is the hard part.

The most important thing I can tell you about transitions: they are not caused by awareness, and they are not caused by tools. Teams do not move from L0 to L1 because they attended a training session. They do not move from L1 to L2 because everyone got a license.

Teams move because of a trigger event, followed by structured permission, followed by a shared practice that sticks.

Let me walk through the two transitions that matter most. Most teams in enterprise delivery are stuck somewhere between L0 and L2. L2 to L3 is a different problem for a different session.

### The L0 → L1 Transition

The trigger at L0 is almost always a person, not a program. Someone on the team — or adjacent to the team — has a visible win with an AI tool. They share it. The right person sees it. That's the spark.

What blocks L0 teams isn't skepticism. It's ambiguity. Nobody told them it was okay to experiment. Nobody told them what counts as success. Nobody told them what to do if the AI is wrong. In the absence of guidance, most professional people default to doing nothing rather than doing the wrong thing. That's not laziness. That's risk management.

[SPEAKER NOTE: This is where you can share a real example from a client engagement if you have one — a specific moment where someone showed the team something small and it opened the door.]

**What actually moves L0 to L1**: One manager says, publicly and explicitly, "I want you to try this. Here's a low-stakes place to try it. And if it doesn't work, that's information, not failure." That's it. That's the move. The tool doesn't matter. The permission matters.

### The L1 → L2 Transition

This one is harder. A lot harder. And it's where most teams get stuck for a long time.

L1 can feel like progress. Usage is happening. People are experimenting. Nobody is unhappy. But L1 is actually unstable — because it's invisible and inconsistent. The value is trapped in individuals, not in the team. When the person who "gets" AI rolls off the project, the capability leaves with them.

The L1→L2 transition requires three things happening at roughly the same time: a shared vocabulary, a shared practice, and someone who holds both of those accountable.

**Shared vocabulary**: The team has to be able to talk about how they use AI without it feeling weird or boastful. That means the manager has to model it. If you never mention how AI helped you think through a problem, your team won't either.

**Shared practice**: At least one team ritual — standup, retro, code review, planning — has to incorporate AI in a defined way. Not mandated. Defined. "In our retros, we talk about where AI helped and where it got in the way." That's a practice.

**Someone accountable**: This is where champions come in — and I'll get to them in detail shortly.

What stalls L1→L2? Three things, reliably. First, the team tries to do everything at once — AI in all the things, all the time — and it collapses under its own ambition. Second, a bad early experience poisons the well: the AI gets something wrong in a visible way, nobody has a framework for handling it, and suddenly the skeptics have ammunition. Third, the manager moves on to the next initiative before this one has roots.

[SPEAKER NOTE: The third one is the one that stings. Be direct here. A lot of AI adoption stalls because the sponsoring manager stopped showing up for it.]

## Section 3 — The Manager's Trap List: What NOT to Do (~7 min)

## SLIDE: What NOT to Do (The Hard Version)

I want to be direct in this section. These are patterns I have seen repeatedly. They feel productive. They are not.

**Trap 1: Allocate licenses to everyone.**

This is the most common mistake. The assumption is that access equals adoption. It does not. Allocating licenses to everyone does not transform a project to L2. What it does is spend budget, generate a usage dashboard that looks active, and create a false sense of progress.

If you have 50 licenses and 12 people actually using the tool in meaningful ways, you don't have an adoption success. You have 12 early adopters who don't have a team yet.

**Trap 2: Mandate use.**

"Everyone will use AI in their workflow by Q3." That sentence has killed more adoption programs than any amount of skepticism.

People don't resist AI. They resist uncertainty. When you mandate without supporting, you create anxiety, which creates avoidance, which creates performative compliance — people running code through a tool to say they did it, not because it helped. You get the metric. You lose the behavior.

**Trap 3: Send everyone to training and call it done.**

Training has a role. A limited role. The assumption that a 4-hour AI literacy module translates into changed day-to-day practice is not supported by anything we know about how adults learn new behaviors. Training gets people to L0.5 at best — aware, not practicing.

**Trap 4: Skip psychological safety.**

Fear is the silent killer of AI adoption. It doesn't announce itself. It shows up as "I tried it and it wasn't that useful" when what the person actually felt was "I tried it and I wasn't sure I was using it right and I didn't want to look incompetent in front of my team."

If the dominant framing in your organization is that AI might eliminate jobs — and that framing is everywhere right now — your team will engage with AI tools defensively, not generatively. Adoption requires that people feel safe enough to look dumb while learning. That safety is a management responsibility. You can't delegate it.

[SPEAKER NOTE: This is a good place to pause and ask: "What does your team think AI means for their job security? When did you last talk about that directly?" Most managers haven't had this conversation explicitly.]

**Trap 5: Measure tool logins.**

If you are measuring AI adoption by tool logins, tokens generated, or seats activated, you are measuring the wrong thing. You are measuring activity, not capability.

The right question is: is your team doing things they couldn't do before? Are they testing more edge cases? Generating documentation that actually gets read? Moving faster on the parts that used to be bottlenecks?

Capability expansion. That's the metric. Not activity.

## Section 4 — Finding and Using Champions (~7 min)

## SLIDE: Champions > Mandates

Peer-driven adoption consistently outperforms top-down mandates. The research on this is not ambiguous. What is ambiguous is how to actually build it.

Let me be specific, because "find champions" is advice that sounds actionable and isn't.

### Who the Right Champion Is (And Isn't)

The instinct is to pick the best AI user. The most technically proficient person. The one who has already gone deep on prompting strategies or built something cool with the API.

That's often the wrong person.

The right champion is the person who is both enthusiastic about AI and trusted by their peers. Those two things don't always coexist. A technically brilliant early adopter who is socially isolated on the team will produce zero adoption spread. Trust is the transport mechanism. Without it, nothing moves.

Look for: the person others ask for advice when they're stuck. The person who explains things without condescension. The person whose opinion carries weight in informal conversations, not just in official forums.

That person, if they're genuinely excited about AI, is your champion.

### What to Give Them

Champions need three things to function: time, visibility, and a real mandate.

**Time**: If you expect someone to champion AI adoption on top of their full delivery load, it won't work. It will burn them out and they'll quietly stop. Protect some of their capacity. Even 10% is meaningful.

**Visibility**: Champions need to be able to show the team what good looks like. That means creating moments — demos, retros, informal show-and-tells — where the champion can share what they've learned without it feeling like a performance review.

**A real mandate**: Not "spread AI enthusiasm." Something specific: "Own the team's AI practices for this sprint cycle. Define how we use AI in code review. Run the retro question." Specificity turns a champion into a force multiplier. Vagueness turns them into a well-meaning volunteer.

### What to Ask Back

This is the part most manager guidance skips. Champions are not just evangelists. They are your signal system.

Ask them to tell you what's not working. Where the team is stuck. What objections keep coming up. What the AI tools get wrong in your specific domain context. This intelligence is what lets you act as a manager — removing blockers, adjusting the approach, flagging issues up the chain before they become credibility problems.

[SPEAKER NOTE: Champions who feel like they're just supposed to sell the program will eventually stop being honest with you. Champions who feel like actual partners in making the team better will tell you the truth. Which one you get depends entirely on how you set up the relationship.]

## Section 5 — Bridging Frameworks and Real Delivery (~5 min)

## SLIDE: The Gap Between the Model and the Client Call

Here's the situation most of you have been in or will be in. You have a client who wants a competitive estimate. Part of that estimate assumes AI productivity gains — because everyone is assuming AI productivity gains now. And you have a team that's still finding its footing with these tools, still figuring out where they actually help versus where they create new kinds of work.

How do you hold both of those realities without selling out either one?

First, be honest about where the team is on the maturity curve — internally. Not to the client in those terms, but to yourself and your delivery leadership. An L1 team cannot reliably deliver on L2 promises. If your estimate assumes AI-accelerated delivery and your team hasn't yet developed shared practices around those tools, you've created a gap that will show up as a miss in month three.

Second, reframe the productivity conversation. The question isn't "how much faster will we ship?" The question is: "what will we be able to do that we couldn't do before?" Those are different questions with different answers. Faster is a weak promise because it's easy to measure and easy to miss. Capability expansion is a stronger promise because it opens conversations about what the client actually needs — not just how quickly they get what they ordered.

[SPEAKER NOTE: This reframe is also useful for managing up. The question "what's the ROI?" is the wrong question at L1. The right question is "what new capability are we building?" Lead with that.]

Third, under-promise on tool productivity and over-deliver on practice. Clients remember whether the work was good, whether the team was trustworthy, and whether the delivery lead was straight with them. They don't remember whether you hit the AI productivity target in the estimate. Build the practices. The numbers follow.

## Close — The Manager's Real Job (~3 min)

## SLIDE: The Honest Summary

Let me leave you with the thing I think most AI transformation advice gets wrong about the manager's role.

It is not your job to be the AI expert. Your team will exceed your AI knowledge within six months — if you let them. That's a success condition, not a threat.

Your job is to remove the organizational friction that prevents your team from becoming AI-capable. That means giving explicit permission to experiment. Protecting time for champions. Having the direct conversation about job security before it becomes ambient anxiety. Measuring capability, not activity. Saying no to the license mandate that looks like progress and isn't.

The bottleneck in AI adoption is not the technology. It is not even the skills gap. According to the Atlassian 2025 State of DevEx Survey, developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding. The rest is the Enterprise Tax — meetings, approvals, context switching, legacy system friction. AI tools that only speed up the 16% don't change delivery. The manager who removes friction from the 84% does.

That's the job. Not AI expertise. Organizational friction removal.

Start there.
