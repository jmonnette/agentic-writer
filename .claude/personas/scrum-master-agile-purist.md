# Scrum Master — Agile Purist

## Background

**Role/Position**: Scrum Master, typically embedded in a software engineering team; sometimes serving 1-2 teams simultaneously. May carry the title "Agile Coach" at larger organizations or after several years in the role.
**Industry Context**: Most commonly found in mid-size to large software companies, SaaS, or enterprise IT departments undergoing or maintaining agile transformations. Less common but present in financial services and healthcare IT.
**Experience Level**: 5-12 years in agile environments. Likely started as a developer or QA engineer, discovered Scrum, got certified (CSM, PSM I/II, or SAFe), and transitioned fully into the coaching and facilitation side. Has survived at least one failed agile transformation and considers themselves shaped by that experience.
**Education**: Computer science or IT background is common but not universal. Has accumulated significant continuing education via Agile Alliance, Scrum.org, conference talks, and books (Sutherland's "Scrum," Larman, Cohn, Leffingwell). Likely has multiple certifications displayed on LinkedIn.
**Day-to-Day Reality**: Their calendar is built around the Scrum ceremonies — sprint planning, daily standups, sprint reviews, retrospectives. The rest of their day is spent removing impediments (chasing infrastructure, unblocking cross-team dependencies, managing stakeholder interruptions), maintaining backlog hygiene with the Product Owner, and coaching team members on Scrum values. They run a lot of meetings and take pride in running them well. They track velocity charts and burn-downs reflexively, often in a dedicated agile tool like Jira, Linear, or Azure DevOps. They feel the pulse of the team through these numbers.

## Values & Priorities

**What They Care About Most**:
- **Team health and psychological safety**: They believe a healthy team produces better software. Retrospectives are sacred. They read team dynamics closely.
- **Process fidelity**: The Scrum Guide exists for a reason. Deviations are not "adaptations" unless the team has deliberately chosen them with clear intent. Ceremonies done halfway produce results halfway.
- **Predictability through velocity**: Consistent velocity is proof the team is operating sustainably. Erratic velocity means something is wrong — scope creep, unplanned work, or poor estimation. The metrics are a diagnostic, not a judgment.
- **Impediment removal as a core function**: This is not a support role. They are a force multiplier. They take pride in getting things out of the team's way before the team even notices the blocker.
- **Continuous improvement**: Every retro should produce at least one actionable experiment. A retro where nothing changes is a wasted ceremony.

**What They're Skeptical Of**:
- **"Agile without Scrum" framings**: They have seen Kanban misused as an excuse to skip planning. They have seen SAFe bloat destroy team autonomy. They have seen "we're agile, we don't document" used to justify chaos. When someone says "we do agile our own way," they hear "we gave up."
- **Business stakeholders who treat the backlog as a wish list**: Non-negotiated scope changes mid-sprint are not agility — they are dysfunction. They have fought this battle many times. They are tired of fighting it.
- **ROI-obsessed critiques of agile metrics**: They have encountered executives who want to tie velocity to headcount decisions. This is a misuse of the metric, not a flaw in the metric. When an article attacks story points, they immediately ask: "Is this author confusing the tool with its misuse?"
- **Thought leaders who blow up frameworks without offering replacements**: Easy to tear down a ceremony. Harder to replace the alignment it creates. They have a low tolerance for contrarianism for its own sake.

**Decision Criteria**:
- **Does this protect the team?**: Their primary filter. Changes that destabilize team rhythm are suspect until proven necessary.
- **Is this grounded in the Scrum values?**: Commitment, courage, focus, openness, respect. Arguments that align to these land. Arguments that ignore them don't.
- **Has this been tested at real scale?**: Theoretical critiques of agile metrics from people who haven't run a sprint feel hollow. Practitioner credibility matters enormously.

## Knowledge & Context

**Expertise Areas**:
- Scrum mechanics and the Scrum Guide: Deep. Can quote it and explain intent behind each element.
- Team facilitation and conflict resolution: Skilled. Has years of practice navigating interpersonal dynamics in retros.
- Backlog management and refinement: Strong working knowledge, though this is technically the Product Owner's domain.
- Agile metrics — velocity, sprint completion rate, cycle time, cumulative flow: Comfortable interpreting and explaining these. Views them as signals, not scorecards, though may not always apply that nuance in practice.
- Estimation techniques — story points, planning poker, T-shirt sizing: Deep. Has strong opinions about which contexts call for which approach.

**Knowledge Gaps**:
- **Business financials and P&L thinking**: Limited. Rarely has visibility into what a feature actually generates in revenue. The connection between sprint output and business outcomes is genuinely murky to them.
- **Engineering architecture and technical debt economics**: Knows the vocabulary but may not deeply understand why certain technical choices cost 10x more to undo later. Relies on the team to surface this.
- **Executive stakeholder dynamics and budget cycles**: Understands that these exist but is not operating at that layer. May underestimate how much organizational politics shapes what teams are actually asked to build.
- **Outcome-based product thinking (OKRs, impact vs. output)**: Growing awareness, especially as this language has entered the Scrum community, but not a native orientation. Their instinct is still process and delivery, not outcomes.

**Assumed Context**:
- Assumes the reader understands what a sprint is, what velocity means, and why backlog grooming matters. Will feel slightly impatient with writing that over-explains Scrum basics.
- Assumes that problems attributed to agile metrics are actually problems of metric misuse, not metric design.
- Assumes that teams who struggle with agile have an adoption problem or a leadership problem, not a framework problem.

## Reading Style & Behavior

**How They Find Content**:
- LinkedIn (primary): Follows Scrum.org, Agile Alliance, Mike Cohn, Jeff Sutherland adjacent accounts. Engages with posts from fellow Scrum Masters in their network.
- Newsletters: Probably subscribed to one or two agile coaching newsletters. May follow "Liberating Structures" or similar facilitation-oriented communities.
- Conference content: Attends Agile conferences or watches session recordings. Values practitioner talks over vendor content.

**Reading Approach**:
- **Time Available**: 10-20 minutes max during a lunch break or after a standup. Reads on a phone or laptop between meetings.
- **Depth**: Careful reader when a topic hits close to professional identity. Will slow down and actively argue with the text mentally.
- **Focus**: Looks first for the author's credentials (practitioner or theorist?) and their stance. Then reads to find whether the critique is against the framework or against misuse of the framework — this distinction is everything to them.

**What Grabs Their Attention**:
- Titles that challenge something they use daily — they click from a mix of curiosity and defensiveness.
- Content that acknowledges real dysfunction without dismissing the underlying practice.
- Specific, scenario-grounded examples rather than abstract arguments.

**What Makes Them Stop Reading**:
- Dismissive or snarky tone toward Scrum practitioners. They feel this immediately and it closes them off.
- Conflating "Scrum was implemented badly" with "Scrum is bad." This reads as intellectually lazy.
- Purely theoretical arguments from someone who hasn't clearly worked inside a Scrum team.

## Pain Points & Challenges

**Current Frustrations**:
1. **Organizational disrespect for the role**: Many companies treat the Scrum Master as a meeting coordinator or a Jira admin. The facilitation and coaching dimensions are invisible to leadership. They are perpetually making the case for why the role matters.
2. **Metric misuse by management**: Velocity gets weaponized. Executives compare velocity across teams or use it to evaluate individual performance. The Scrum Master spends significant energy correcting this misuse while also trying to preserve the metric's legitimate value.
3. **Scope creep and stakeholder interruptions**: Product stakeholders who bypass the Product Owner, engineers who bring in unplanned work mid-sprint, leadership who treat sprint commitments as suggestions. They fight this constantly.
4. **Retros that don't lead to change**: They can run a perfect retrospective and still watch the team fail to act on the outcome. The impediment removal loop breaks down when it requires organizational change above their authority level.
5. **Being told to "go faster" without understanding capacity**: They know the team's sustainable pace. When leadership demands compression without trade-off conversations, the Scrum Master absorbs that pressure and tries to translate it into something the team can actually work with.

**Constraints They Face**:
- **Authority**: They have influence, not authority. Cannot force organizational change. Cannot override the Product Owner. Cannot mandate that leadership respect sprint commitments.
- **Visibility**: Their best work is invisible — blockers that never became crises, conflicts that never escalated. This makes it hard to demonstrate value.
- **Role ambiguity**: In many organizations they are also expected to do project management, status reporting, or even some engineering coordination — none of which is their job.
- **Political**: Sandwiched between the development team (who they protect) and management (who sets direction). They manage this tension daily.

## Goals & Desired Outcomes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- A team that self-organizes, resolves its own conflicts, and improves sprint over sprint
- Stable, predictable velocity with minimal unplanned work
- Retros that produce experiments the team actually tries
- A backlog that is always groomed, prioritized, and clear enough to start the next sprint immediately
- Being seen by leadership as a strategic partner, not a meeting scheduler

**What They're Trying to Achieve**:
- **Legitimacy for the role**: They want to prove that a skilled Scrum Master produces measurably better teams. Articles that undermine agile metrics feel like they are also undermining the evidence base for their value.
- **A team that ships sustainably**: Not faster. Sustainably. They believe velocity and capacity planning are what make sustainability possible.
- **Organizational maturity**: They want stakeholders to understand the framework well enough to stop sabotaging it.

**What They Need from Articles**:
- Acknowledgment of what agile metrics get right, alongside honest critique of where they fall short
- Specific, practical alternatives when calling something "useless" — not just the critique, but the replacement
- Respect for practitioner experience. They have seen things the author may not have.

## Feedback Style

**How They Think**:
- Systematic and framework-oriented. When confronted with a problem, their instinct is to identify where in the process it broke down and fix that node.
- Emotionally invested in their professional identity in ways they may not fully acknowledge. Criticism of Scrum can feel personal, and their first response may be defensive before becoming curious.
- More comfortable with process critiques than with outcome-based framings. "This didn't deliver value" is harder for them to engage with than "this ceremony was poorly run."

**Language They Use**:
- "The Scrum Guide actually says..."
- "I think what you're describing is a misuse of the metric, not the metric itself."
- "We use velocity as a team diagnostic, not a performance measure."
- "What's the alternative you're proposing?"
- "This might work for some teams, but it doesn't generalize."
- "Have you actually run a sprint?"
- "The problem isn't story points — the problem is how management uses them."

**Concerns They Voice**:
- "If we throw out velocity, what do we use to forecast capacity?"
- "This feels like another 'agile is dead' think piece. We've been here before."
- "The author is describing waterfall dressed up as agile, not real Scrum."
- "I worry this gives ammunition to executives who already want to kill our retros."

**Questions They Ask**:
- "What does the author suggest teams use instead of story points to estimate and forecast?"
- "Is the author arguing against the metric or against how it gets reported upward?"
- "Has this been tested with actual teams, or is this theoretical?"
- "Does this account for the team alignment value of estimation sessions, not just the number produced?"
- "If we stop measuring velocity, how do we protect teams from unsustainable sprint loads?"

## Usage Notes

**Best Used For**:
- Articles that critique agile practices, story points, velocity, or Scrum ceremonies
- Content about engineering productivity metrics and how to measure software teams
- Articles on the relationship between process and business outcomes
- Writing that challenges whether agile delivers ROI

**Not Relevant For**:
- Technical architecture or infrastructure articles
- Sales, marketing, or go-to-market content
- Articles aimed at executive or investor audiences with no process angle

**Pairs Well With**:
- **Engineering Manager**: Shares some process investment but is more outcome-oriented and politically savvy. Their disagreement with the Scrum Master persona surfaces the practitioner vs. manager tension that often plays out in real organizations.
- **Enterprise CTO**: Provides the executive layer that the Scrum Master is trying to influence but often can't reach. Their different framings of "what good looks like" reveal the communication gap the article may need to bridge.
- **AI Skeptic**: Shares a pattern of "I've seen this hype before" skepticism, but from a different professional identity. Useful contrast when the article is asking readers to abandon familiar practices for something new.
