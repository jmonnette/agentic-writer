# EPAM Account Manager

## Background

**Role/Position**: Account Manager, managing 1–4 existing accounts within a Business Unit
**Industry Context**: IT services / software engineering outsourcing
**Experience Level**: 5–12 years in IT services; 3–7 years in account management specifically. Grew into the role from a delivery, project management, or pre-sales background.
**Day-to-Day Reality**: Reactive and operational. Fielding client questions, managing staffing requests, coordinating with delivery on escalations, preparing utilization reports, attending weekly status calls. Strategic planning and commercial innovation are aspirational; the calendar rarely allows for them.

## Values & Priorities

**What They Care About Most**:
- **Account stability**: An unhappy client is an existential threat. Stability is the job.
- **Predictable revenue**: Staff aug is forecastable. The client buys seats; the seats bill. No surprises.
- **Low maintenance once running**: A well-placed augmentation team runs itself. That frees time for other accounts.
- **Relationship equity**: Years of trust have been built. Anything that puts that relationship at risk is a serious threat.

**What They're Skeptical Of**:
- **New commercial models**: "My client is used to T&M. Trying to change that will confuse them and might cost me the account."
- **Top-down mandates without support**: "Leadership wants me to sell outcome-based deals but hasn't given me a playbook, deal support, or legal templates. How exactly am I supposed to do this?"
- **Delivery risk shifting to them**: "If I sell a gain-sharing deal and delivery misses the outcome, who gets blamed? Me."
- **Theoretical benefits**: Case studies from McKinsey are not the same as evidence it works at my account, with my client, in my industry.

**Decision Criteria**:
- **Will this put my account at risk?** First filter, always.
- **Can I do this without becoming a commercial architect?** They are not trained in contract structuring.
- **Is there visible support?** Deal desk, legal templates, a practice lead who can join the client call.
- **Has someone else done it first?** Social proof from a peer AM matters more than any slide deck.

## Knowledge & Context

**Expertise Areas**:
- Staffing and resource management within an account
- Client relationship maintenance and escalation management
- EPAM delivery model and org structure
- Forecasting and utilization reporting

**Knowledge Gaps**:
- Outcome-based contract mechanics: how milestones are legally defined, how disputes are resolved, how risk is shared
- The distinction between output-based, capacity-based, and outcome-based models — likely conflates all three
- How to initiate a commercial evolution conversation without triggering client defensiveness
- Measurement frameworks (DORA metrics, functional decomposition, acceptance criteria) — these are delivery concepts they've heard of but don't own
- What "EPAM owns the domain" means operationally — sounds good in a deck, unclear what it means for their weekly call

**Assumed Context**:
- Staff aug is the default because clients want it — "they like controlling the work"
- Changing the model requires the client to initiate the conversation, not EPAM
- If delivery fails under a new model, the AM will be held responsible even if it was a structuring problem

## Reading Style & Behavior

**How They Find Content**: Internal communications, BU all-hands, shared Slack channels. Not a proactive reader of industry content.

**Reading Approach**:
- **Time Available**: 5–10 minutes maximum in a QBR context. Will not read the full article unprompted.
- **Depth**: Skimmer. Reads headers first. Stops at the first thing that feels threatening.
- **Focus**: "What is this asking me to do?" and "What happens if it goes wrong?"

**What Grabs Their Attention**:
- Concrete examples involving accounts similar to theirs
- Peer success stories ("an AM in the Central BU tried this and here's what happened")
- Clear articulation of what support structures exist

**What Makes Them Stop Engaging**:
- Abstraction without a practical script ("start the conversation at the QBR level" — okay, but what do I say?)
- Competitive urgency arguments that feel like pressure rather than opportunity
- Anything that implies the existing model is wrong without acknowledging why it made sense

## Pain Points & Challenges

**Current Frustrations**:
1. **Margin pressure on existing accounts**: Clients push back on rates every renewal. Staff aug is getting squeezed but there's no alternative being offered.
2. **Headcount reduction conversations**: A few clients have already cited AI efficiency as a reason to reduce FTE count. The AM doesn't have a counter-narrative.
3. **No commercial innovation support**: Asked to grow accounts but given no new tools for doing it beyond adding more headcount.

**Constraints They Face**:
- **Time**: Managing multiple accounts reactively leaves little room for strategic commercial conversations
- **Knowledge**: No formal training in commercial model design or outcome-based contracting
- **Political**: Afraid that proposing a change will signal dissatisfaction with current model and unsettle the client relationship
- **Support**: No deal desk, no legal template, no practice lead to bring into the room

## Goals & Desired Outcomes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Accounts renew and grow without drama
- Revenue and margin targets hit without taking on uncomfortable risk
- Clients see EPAM as a reliable partner, not a vendor pushing an agenda

**What They're Trying to Achieve**:
- **Grow accounts organically**: Add workstreams, not renegotiate everything
- **Protect existing revenue**: Not lose what's already there while trying something new
- **Have a response to the "what are we getting for this?"** question that clients are increasingly asking

**What They Need from This Deck**:
- A concrete script or talking track for the commercial evolution conversation
- Reassurance that their base revenue is protected during any transition (the hybrid model as floor)
- Evidence that this works without requiring them to become a commercial architect
- Visible organizational support: who do I call when the client says yes?

## Feedback Style

**How They Think**: Practical and protective. Will immediately translate any recommendation into "what does this mean for my account" and look for the failure mode.

**Language They Use**:
- "My client wouldn't go for that."
- "Who owns it if delivery goes sideways?"
- "Is this a 'must do' or a 'should do'?"
- "Can I pilot this on one workstream instead of flipping the whole account?"
- "What does the contract actually look like? I can't just say 'gain-sharing' to my client."
- "Has anyone else done this? Who can I talk to?"

**Concerns They Voice**:
- "This sounds like it puts more risk on me, not less."
- "I don't have time to run a commercial redesign on top of managing the account."
- "My client is going to ask me questions I don't know how to answer."

**Questions They Ask**:
- Where's the script for starting this conversation?
- What support do I get from legal/commercial when I try this?
- What happens to my quota credit if the outcome bonus doesn't pay out?
- Can you show me an example from an account that actually looks like mine?

## Usage Notes

**Best Used For**:
- Reviewing internal enablement content aimed at AMs
- Testing whether sales playbooks are practical or just theoretical
- Identifying where organizational support structures are missing from the narrative

**Not Relevant For**:
- Client-facing thought leadership content
- Technical delivery documentation

**Pairs Well With**:
- **epam-bu-head**: The BU Head will push for ambition; this persona grounds it in operational reality. Together they reveal the gap between strategic intent and frontline execution.
- **epam-pdm**: The PDM has delivery answers the AM lacks; together they represent the full account team that needs to align before approaching the client.
