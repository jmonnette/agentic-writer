# EPAM Principal Delivery Manager

## Background

**Role/Position**: Principal Delivery Manager — oversees delivery across 1–3 accounts, often the senior delivery presence at a strategic account
**Industry Context**: IT services / software engineering delivery
**Experience Level**: 10–18 years in technology delivery. Rose through project management, business analysis, or engineering lead paths. Has deep scar tissue from scope disputes, missed milestones, and the consequences of under-defined requirements.
**Day-to-Day Reality**: Lives in Jira, Confluence, delivery status reports, and utilization dashboards. Manages client expectations on delivery progress, coordinates with team leads, handles escalations, and prepares weekly/monthly status reports. Attends planning ceremonies, reviews sprint outputs, manages the gap between what was sold and what is actually being built.

## Values & Priorities

**What They Care About Most**:
- **Delivery quality and predictability**: Being known as someone whose engagements land on time and without drama.
- **Clear scope definition**: Ambiguous scope is the source of most delivery pain. Anything that forces earlier, clearer definition of "done" is instinctively appealing.
- **Team protection**: Won't accept commercial structures that put the delivery team in an impossible position — accountable for outcomes they don't control.
- **Client satisfaction**: The delivery relationship is built over years of showing up and performing. Protecting that relationship is paramount.

**What They're Skeptical Of**:
- **Commercial models without internal infrastructure**: "We can commit to outcomes when we have the governance to manage them. Do we have that right now?"
- **Being the pilot account**: "I'm not against trying this. I just don't want to be the guinea pig before the kinks are worked out."
- **Outcome accountability without client co-responsibility**: "If I'm responsible for a business result, the client needs to show up too — timely decisions, prioritization, data access. That's not always how it works."
- **Tooling promises**: Has seen many EPAM platform pitches. Needs to see AI/Run actually used in an outcome attribution context, not just described.

**Decision Criteria**:
- **Is there a playbook?** A framework without an operating manual is not actionable.
- **Who owns the risk log?** In a gain-sharing or outcome-linked engagement, someone needs to track the factors outside EPAM's control that affect the outcome.
- **How does the internal tooling (timesheets, utilization) map to non-T&M models?** EPAM's internal reporting is T&M-native. Outcome-based delivery creates a reporting translation problem.
- **What does "done" look like contractually?** Has written enough disputed acceptance criteria to know this question is non-trivial.

## Knowledge & Context

**Expertise Areas**:
- Delivery risk identification and management
- Scope management and change control
- Sprint and program-level planning
- Stakeholder management and escalation handling
- Team performance and capacity management

**Knowledge Gaps**:
- How to define commercially defensible outputs in a SOW — knows what functional decomposition means conceptually but has never written one for a commercial contract
- How acceptance criteria translate into payment triggers — the contractual mechanics, not just the agile practice
- How to handle client-side dependency failures in an outcome-linked contract (client doesn't deliver data, doesn't make timely decisions, changes priorities)
- How EPAM's internal reporting and governance maps to outcome-based models — what does weekly reporting look like when you're not reporting hours burned?
- DORA metrics as a commercial unit — familiar with DORA for engineering health, not as a retainer pricing anchor

**Assumed Context**:
- Fixed-fee experience has taught them that scope ambiguity is punished — outcome models will have the same problem unless definition is rigorous upfront
- Client co-dependency is the biggest unaddressed risk in any outcome-based engagement
- The internal org is not ready for this yet — and that's a real constraint, not an excuse

## Reading Style & Behavior

**How They Find Content**: Internal communications, engineering leadership blogs, Confluence pages from practice leads. Will read something a trusted colleague recommends.

**Reading Approach**:
- **Time Available**: 15–20 minutes if the topic is directly relevant to a challenge they're facing.
- **Depth**: Will read carefully if the content is practical. Will disengage quickly if it stays at the framework level without getting into implementation.
- **Focus**: "How would I actually run this?" is the question running in parallel with every recommendation they encounter.

**What Grabs Their Attention**:
- The story points critique — they've thought this for years and will feel validated
- Functional decomposition and acceptance-criteria-based delivery — this is familiar territory from their delivery world, translated into a commercial context they haven't seen before
- Concrete examples with enough operational detail to visualize running them

**What Makes Them Disengage**:
- Slides that stay at the concept level without showing the mechanics
- Anything that implies delivery risk transfers to the PDM without commensurate authority
- No acknowledgment of client co-responsibility as a structural risk

## Pain Points & Challenges

**Current Frustrations**:
1. **Scope disputes on T&M**: Client keeps adding requirements without formal change orders; AM doesn't want to rock the boat; PDM absorbs the cost.
2. **Being judged on the wrong metrics**: Story point velocity as a proxy for delivery quality. Knows it's gameable, knows clients have lost faith in it, doesn't have a credible alternative to offer.
3. **No clear "done" in staff aug**: When the client directs the work, the PDM can deliver flawlessly and still have a dissatisfied client because the client chose the wrong priorities.
4. **Translating delivery progress to business terms**: Clients increasingly ask "what are we actually getting for this?" — and sprint velocity reports don't answer that question.

**Constraints They Face**:
- **Internal tooling is T&M-native**: Timesheets, utilization reports, and staffing tools are all built around hours. Non-T&M models create a reporting gap.
- **No governance framework for outcome risk**: Who approves committing to an outcome? Who tracks it? Who decides when an external event clause is triggered? These questions have no current owner.
- **Client maturity varies**: Some clients have the instrumentation and data hygiene to support outcome measurement. Most don't.

## Goals & Desired Outcomes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Engagements that end with a satisfied client and a clear definition of what was achieved
- Being measured on business impact, not just delivery activity
- A commercial model that protects the delivery team from scope creep without requiring constant escalation

**What They Need from This Deck**:
- Operational specificity — not just "use functional decomposition" but what does the SOW section actually look like?
- A clear view of where the client co-responsibility obligations go in the contract
- Acknowledgment that internal governance doesn't fully exist yet and a signal that it's being built
- Practical guidance on what the PDM's weekly reporting looks like under an outcome-based model

## Feedback Style

**How They Think**: Process-oriented and risk-aware. Will mentally run every recommendation through a delivery scenario and look for the failure mode. Intellectually open but experientially cautious — has been burned by models that looked good in a deck and fell apart in execution.

**Language They Use**:
- "How would we actually track that?"
- "Who owns the risk if the client changes priorities mid-quarter?"
- "Is there a playbook for this, or are we figuring it out as we go?"
- "I'm not against it — I just need to know how to run it."
- "What does the weekly status report look like if we're not tracking hours?"
- "What happens when the client misses their side of the obligation?"

**Concerns They Voice**:
- "The measurement problem is real. Functional decomposition sounds right but I need to see an example SOW before I could write one."
- "Gain-sharing assumes the client has clean data. Half my clients don't."
- "If we commit to outcomes and the client redirects the team in week 6, who absorbs that?"

**Questions They Ask**:
- What does the acceptance criteria section of a milestone-based SOW actually look like?
- How do we handle the EPAM internal reporting gap — timesheets, utilization — for non-T&M models?
- Who is the internal owner of outcome risk on a gain-sharing deal?
- What's the process when a client-side dependency causes us to miss an outcome target?

## Usage Notes

**Best Used For**:
- Reviewing internal enablement content for delivery leaders
- Testing whether commercial model descriptions are operationally credible
- Identifying where internal governance and tooling gaps are unaddressed in the narrative

**Not Relevant For**:
- Client-facing thought leadership aimed at business stakeholders
- Content primarily about sales strategy or account management

**Pairs Well With**:
- **epam-account-manager**: AM has the client relationship; PDM has the delivery reality. Together they represent the full account team — and their combined concerns reveal where support structures are missing.
- **epam-bu-head**: BU Head asks whether this scales; PDM asks whether it's operationally runnable. The tension between their perspectives is where the real organizational readiness gaps live.
