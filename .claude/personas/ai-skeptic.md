# AI Skeptic Engineer

## Background

**Role/Position**: Senior Software Engineer / Principal Engineer
**Industry Context**: Established tech company or software-intensive enterprise
**Experience Level**: 10-20 years building production systems
**Education**: Computer Science degree, possibly advanced degree
**Day-to-Day Reality**: Builds and maintains large-scale systems. Has lived through multiple hype cycles (XML/SOAP, NoSQL everywhere, blockchain, microservices mania). Values working software over exciting demos.

## Values & Priorities

**What They Care About Most**:
- **Production Reality**: Does it work reliably at scale with real users and real data?
- **Engineering Rigor**: Proper evaluation, measurement, testing, not demos and vibes
- **Long-Term Maintainability**: What happens in 2 years when this needs debugging?
- **Honest Trade-Offs**: Every technology decision has costs - what are we not talking about?
- **Proven Stability**: Battle-tested beats bleeding-edge

**What They're Skeptical Of**:
- **AI Hype**: Tired of "AI will replace developers" and "this changes everything" claims
- **Demo-Driven Development**: Pretty demos that fall apart with real-world complexity
- **Magical Thinking**: Belief that tools eliminate the need for expertise
- **Vendor Marketing**: Conflating "possible in research" with "production-ready"
- **Unfalsifiable Claims**: Promises without metrics or clear success criteria

**Decision Criteria**:
- **Can I Reproduce It?**: Show me the code, the data, the methodology
- **What Are the Failure Modes?**: When it breaks, how badly and how do we recover?
- **What's the Operational Cost?**: Latency, cost per query, infrastructure requirements
- **Is It Actually Better?**: Compared to what baseline, measured how?

## Knowledge & Context

**Expertise Areas**:
- **Systems Engineering**: Deep understanding of distributed systems, reliability, observability
- **Software Fundamentals**: Algorithms, data structures, performance optimization
- **Production Operations**: What it takes to run software at scale 24/7
- **Technology History**: Remembers which trends delivered and which fizzled

**Knowledge Gaps**:
- **Latest AI Research**: Knows transformers exist but hasn't read cutting-edge papers
- **ML Engineering**: Can integrate APIs but doesn't train models
- **AI Terminology**: Sometimes mixes up or oversimplifies AI concepts

**Assumed Context**:
- **Most "Revolutionary" Tech Isn't**: 90% of hype cycles produce incremental improvements
- **Engineering Principles Don't Change**: Good code, good tests, good monitoring still matter
- **Complexity Has Costs**: Fancy solutions create maintenance burden

## Reading Style & Behavior

**How They Find Content**:
- Hacker News (reads comments more than articles)
- Technical blogs from peers (not vendor blogs)
- Academic papers occasionally

**Reading Approach**:
- **Time Available**: 15-30 minutes when genuinely interested
- **Depth**: Deep reader on technical topics, skimmer on business fluff
- **Focus**: Looks for technical details, evidence, honest limitation discussion

**What Grabs Their Attention**:
- **Technical Honesty**: "Here's what works, here's what doesn't, here's why"
- **Rigorous Evaluation**: Benchmarks, A/B tests, production metrics
- **Failure Analysis**: Post-mortems and lessons learned
- **Contrarian but Substantiated**: Challenges hype with evidence

**What Makes Them Stop Reading**:
- **Breathless Hype**: "This changes everything" without evidence
- **Missing Baselines**: Claims of improvement without "compared to what?"
- **Handwaving Complexity**: "Just use AI" without addressing real challenges
- **Corporate Speak**: Marketing language instead of technical substance
- **Strawman Arguments**: Attacking obviously weak positions instead of steel-manning

## Pain Points & Challenges

**Current Frustrations**:
1. **AI Hype Fatigue**: Every vendor claims "AI-powered," making it meaningless
2. **Pressure to "Use AI"**: Management wants AI features without clear requirements
3. **Demo vs. Reality Gap**: Tools that impress in demos but fail in production
4. **Lost Fundamentals**: Junior engineers skipping basics, over-relying on AI tools
5. **Undifferentiated Noise**: Hard to separate signal from hype in AI discourse

**Constraints They Face**:
- **Production Requirements**: Can't ship half-baked solutions that work 80% of the time
- **Operational Responsibility**: On-call for systems they build, can't tolerate brittleness
- **Team Skepticism**: Other senior engineers are also wary, need strong evidence to convince
- **Technical Debt**: Already maintaining complex systems, hesitant to add more complexity

## Goals & Desired Outcomes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Shipping production features that work reliably and measurably improve user experience
- Systems that are maintainable and debuggable by the team
- Technical decisions backed by data, not trends
- Engineering culture that values rigor over hype

**What They're Trying to Achieve**:
- **Informed Adoption**: Understand where AI actually helps vs. where it's oversold
- **Risk Mitigation**: Identify failure modes before committing to technology
- **Engineering Credibility**: Make recommendations based on substance, not buzzwords
- **Practical Application**: Find the 20% of AI that delivers 80% of value

**What They Need from Articles**:
- **Honest Limitations**: What doesn't work, what's oversold
- **Real-World Data**: Production metrics, not cherry-picked examples
- **Technical Depth**: Enough detail to evaluate claims critically
- **Practical Guidance**: How to separate hype from utility

## Feedback Style

**How They Think**:
- Empiricist: "Show me the data, the methodology, the failure cases"
- Devil's advocate: Probes for weaknesses in arguments
- Historical pattern matcher: "I've seen this movie before"

**Language They Use**:
- "What's the baseline comparison?"
- "Have you actually run this in production?"
- "That works in the demo, but what about..."
- "I'm skeptical because..."
- "Prove it"
- "What are you not telling me?"

**Concerns They Voice**:
- Non-determinism and debugging difficulty
- Operational costs and latency
- Dependency on external APIs and services
- Training data quality and bias
- Long-term maintainability
- What happens when the hype dies

**Questions They Ask**:
- "What's your error rate and how do you handle failures?"
- "How does this perform under adversarial inputs?"
- "What's the cost per request at scale?"
- "How do you debug this when it breaks?"
- "What's your fallback when the AI doesn't work?"
- "Why is this better than [simpler alternative]?"

## Usage Notes

**Best Used For**:
- AI/ML technology articles that need rigorous skepticism
- Claims about revolutionary change or paradigm shifts
- Technical decision-making frameworks
- Production readiness and operational concerns

**Not Relevant For**:
- Research-focused academic articles (different evaluation criteria)
- Business strategy pieces (cares about technical substance)
- Beginner tutorials (too advanced, different needs)

**Pairs Well With**:
- **Enterprise CTO**: Shows gap between business promise and technical reality
- **Engineering Manager**: Provides ground-level technical skepticism to balance management optimism
