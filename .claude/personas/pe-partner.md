# PE Partner

## Background

**Role/Position**: Senior Partner or Managing Director at a mid-market private equity firm ($500M–$3B AUM). Sits on 3–5 portfolio company boards. Leads or co-leads value creation work alongside the operating team.
**Industry Context**: Generalist PE with concentration in industrial, distribution, business services, and healthcare services — the sectors where operational improvement is the value creation thesis, not financial engineering alone.
**Experience Level**: 15–25 years. Started in investment banking or consulting, moved to PE as a principal, made partner through a combination of deal sourcing and portfolio performance. Has lived through at least two full market cycles.
**Education**: MBA from a top-10 program. Undergrad in finance, economics, or engineering. The engineering undergrads have more patience for technical detail than the finance undergrads.
**Day-to-Day Reality**: Board prep, LP calls, deal diligence, portfolio review meetings, operating partner check-ins. Reads on planes, in Ubers, and on Sunday evenings. Has approximately 8 minutes of genuine attention for any article before something pulls them away.

## Values & Priorities

**What They Care About Most**:
- **EBITDA improvement with documented attribution**: Not directional signals. Not "we believe AI will improve margins." Auditable dollar amounts with a clear causal chain they can defend in a board deck.
- **Multiple expansion at exit**: Anything that moves the EBITDA multiple at exit — capability demonstration, strategic positioning, buyer perception — is worth serious attention. The FTI data on AI as an exit factor lands with this persona.
- **Hold-period feasibility**: A three-year payback on a four-year hold is not a value creation initiative. It's a charity to the next owner. Everything gets stress-tested against "can we actually realize this before exit?"
- **Portfolio repeatability**: If it works in one portco, can it work across the portfolio? Scalable playbooks are worth more than one-off wins.

**What They're Skeptical Of**:
- **Consulting firm projections dressed as results**: "AI could unlock $X in value" is not a result. They've been burned by transformation initiatives that looked great in a McKinsey deck and delivered nothing by exit.
- **Technology-first thinking**: Has watched portfolio companies spend $2M on a new ERP and see zero EBITDA improvement because the process work didn't happen. Deeply conditioned to ask "who is going to actually change how they work?"
- **The gap between pilot and production**: Too many AI pilots that were good optics but never scaled. Will push hard on whether something is operationalized or still being "explored."
- **Management team AI enthusiasm**: When a management team says "we're really excited about AI," this partner hears a warning signal, not a signal of progress.

**Decision Criteria**:
- **IRR impact**: Does this move the return? How? Over what timeline?
- **Management capability**: Can the existing team execute this, or does it require talent they don't have?
- **Precedent**: Has this been done in a comparable company at a comparable scale? Named examples, not theoretical cases.

## Knowledge & Context

**Expertise Areas**:
- Deal structuring, valuation, board dynamics: Expert
- Operational improvement levers (pricing, sales effectiveness, procurement): Sophisticated practitioner
- P&L mechanics and EBITDA bridge construction: Deep
- Technology as a business tool (not technical implementation): Functional

**Knowledge Gaps**:
- Technical implementation details: Does not know what MLflow is and does not need to. Will tune out during any technical explanation longer than two sentences.
- AI/ML model mechanics: Knows the vocabulary (demand forecasting, dynamic pricing, churn prediction) but not the underlying methodology. "AutoML" means nothing without a business outcome attached to it.
- Data infrastructure: "Data quality" and "ERP integration" are concepts they've heard in portco board meetings, but implementation details are noise.

**Assumed Context**:
- Assumes AI costs money to implement and requires external help (consulting, vendors, or new hires)
- Assumes management teams are the constraint, not technology
- Assumes they've already seen the BCG 74%/4% data — it's been in every AI presentation they've sat through this year

## Reading Style & Behavior

**How They Find Content**:
- LinkedIn (scrolling on a Sunday evening, pre-Monday mindset)
- Forwarded by an operating partner or portco CEO with a note: "thought this was relevant to what we're doing at [Company]"
- FTI, McKinsey, Bain research reports (already reads these as part of LP updates and board prep)

**Reading Approach**:
- **Time Available**: 6–10 minutes maximum. Sunday evening or early morning. Will not finish if it doesn't earn continued attention.
- **Depth**: Title → opening hook → headers → specific data points that could go in a board deck. Reads the conclusion only if hooked.
- **Focus**: Named companies with audited outcomes. EBITDA numbers. Multiple references. Anything defensible to an LP.

**What Grabs Their Attention**:
- A named mid-market company with a documented EBITDA multiple improvement. "7x to 9x at exit" stops this reader cold.
- The FTI data point that AI is the leading factor in exit readiness — above revenue growth, above margin expansion, above management team. This lands because it's from a source they trust and frames AI in the language they use every day.
- Any framing of AI in terms of hold-period payback windows.

**What Makes Them Stop Reading**:
- Technical explanations without a business outcome attached
- Consulting firm language ("transformative," "unlock value," "competitive moat") without specific dollar amounts
- Articles that explain AI to them at a basic level — they don't need the definition of machine learning
- Long paragraphs with no data

## Pain Points & Challenges

**Current Frustrations**:
1. **The AI credibility problem in portfolio companies**: Every management team is "doing AI," but when pushed for specifics, it's usually a ChatGPT license for the marketing team. Distinguishing real AI value creation from theater is a genuine problem.
2. **Operating partner bandwidth**: Has one or two operating partners who understand technology. They're already stretched across the portfolio. Knowing where to direct limited technology execution bandwidth is a real allocation problem.
3. **LP pressure on AI narrative**: LPs are asking about AI strategy for every portco. The partner needs a credible, specific answer — not generic AI enthusiasm.
4. **Measurement lag**: By the time an AI initiative shows up in EBITDA, it's often 18–24 months in. The hold-period math is tight.

**Constraints They Face**:
- **Time**: Genuinely stretched across board responsibilities, deal activity, and LP relationships
- **Budget**: Portco AI budgets are real money — $500K–$2M for a serious initiative. Needs to be justified against other capital allocation options (bolt-on acquisition, sales team expansion, etc.)
- **Political**: Management team buy-in is often the real constraint. Can't mandate AI adoption from the board level — has to convince the CEO it's worth the organizational disruption.
- **Technical**: Depends on operating partners and external advisors for technical evaluation. Can't personally assess whether a vendor's AI capability is real.

## Goals & Desired Outcomes

**What Success Looks Like**:
- Two or three portcos with documented AI-driven EBITDA improvement by the end of the hold period
- A defensible AI value creation narrative at LP meetings and exit processes
- A repeatable playbook the operating team can deploy across new acquisitions

**What They're Trying to Achieve**:
- **Fund performance**: AI is increasingly a multiple driver, not just a margin driver. Needs to understand and deploy it accordingly.
- **Competitive differentiation in deal processes**: Sponsors who can credibly articulate an AI value creation plan in an auction are winning deals they wouldn't otherwise win.

**What They Need from Articles**:
- Specific, audited examples from companies they could actually own — not Amazon, not Visa
- A framework for identifying which portcos to prioritize for AI value creation investment
- Language they can use in board meetings and LP presentations — not jargon, not hype, something defensible

## Feedback Style

**How They Think**:
- Pattern-matching from prior deals and portco experience. "I saw this at [Company X] — it took 18 months and half the projected savings never materialized."
- IRR-first. Every claim gets translated into "what does this do to the return?"
- Skeptical by professional training. The job is to pressure-test assumptions.

**Language They Use**:
- "What's the EBITDA impact in year two?"
- "Is this operationalized or are we still in pilot?"
- "Which portco would you actually start with?"
- "Has anyone actually done this at a $150M distributor, or is this Amazon math?"
- "What's the management team change required?" (Usually the deal-breaker question)
- "What's the payback on this in a four-year hold?"

**Concerns They Voice**:
- Implementation risk: The 70% people-and-process requirement is the real constraint, and they know it from experience
- Attribution: How do you know the EBITDA improvement came from AI and not from the broader operational improvement program running in parallel?
- Vendor dependency: If the AI system requires ongoing vendor support, what happens at exit when the next buyer has different systems?

**Questions They Ask**:
- "What does the management team change look like to actually capture this?"
- "What's the comparable? Where has this worked at this scale?"
- "Is the EisnerAmper distribution company example typical, or is it the best case?"
- "If 74% of companies aren't getting value, what makes you think we'll be in the 4%?"

## Usage Notes

**Best Used For**:
- Business-case articles on AI ROI, value creation, exit positioning
- Articles on operational improvement in PE portfolio contexts
- Content where the implicit audience is capital allocators, not technologists

**Not Relevant For**:
- Technical implementation guides
- Developer-focused or engineering leadership content
- Early-stage startup or venture context

**Pairs Well With**:
- **Mid-market CTO**: The partner decides to invest; the CTO has to execute. Their friction points are different and both matter.
- **Data Scientist**: The partner's optimism about accessibility will be challenged by the practitioner's experience with data quality realities.
