# Style Analysis: 2026-03-29

**Documents Analyzed**:
- "Gen AI is Non-Deterministic: Why it Matters and How it Changes the Way We Work With Software" (2024-09-29)
- "Know Your Constraint: Why Vibe Coding Can't Overcome the Enterprise Tax" (2026-03-21)

**Total Words**: ~4,900
**Analysis Focus**: Comprehensive (sentence, vocabulary, rhetoric, structure)

---

## Key Findings

### Sentence Patterns

**Average Length**: 18-22 words (range: 3-45 words)
**Structure Distribution**:
- Simple sentences: ~35%
- Compound sentences: ~25%
- Complex sentences: ~40%

**Signature Moves**:
1. **Strategic Ultra-Short Sentences for Emphasis**
   - "It's a fair question." (4 words)
   - "The demos are impressive. The tools are real." (7 words each)
   - "That's why most teams only do it once every three years." (11 words)

2. **Colon-Driven Lists for Enumeration**
   - Heavy use of colons to introduce bulleted lists
   - Pattern: Claim statement + colon + bulleted supporting points
   - Example: "Here is what they don't have to deal with:" followed by 5 bullets

3. **Question-Answer Flow**
   - Opens with stakeholder question, then systematically answers
   - Pattern: "My client stakeholders regularly ask a question that goes something like this: '[quote]'"

4. **Parallel Construction in Lists**
   - Consistent grammatical structure across list items
   - Example: "Embrace Probabilistic Thinking: / Focus on Robustness Over Precision: / Adopt Statistical Evaluation Methods:"

### Paragraph Architecture

**Average Paragraph Length**: 3-5 sentences
**Range**: 1 sentence (rare, for emphasis) to 8 sentences (complex arguments)

**Topic Sentence Patterns**:
- **Direct Statement**: 70% of paragraphs lead with explicit claim
- **Evidence First**: 20% present data/scenario before interpretation
- **Question Hook**: 10% open with rhetorical question

**Development Pattern**: Claim → Evidence → Elaboration (consistent)

**Transitions**:
- Minimal explicit transition words ("However," "Moreover" rare)
- Logical flow through content repetition and pronouns
- Section headers do heavy lifting for macro-transitions

### Vocabulary Profile

**Lexical Density**: High (60-65% content words)

**Most Frequent Content Words**:
- AI, model(s), LLM(s), system(s)
- Developer(s), team(s), stakeholder(s)
- Output(s), process, time, work
- Non-determinism, consistency, variability

**Characteristic Verbs** (Strong, Specific):
- Technical: "implement," "deploy," "integrate," "generate," "refine"
- Analytical: "evaluate," "assess," "identify," "measure," "track"
- Action: "embrace," "leverage," "harness," "cultivate," "prioritize"
- Avoids: weak verbs like "do," "make," "get" in favor of precise alternatives

**Jargon Usage**: ~15-20 technical terms per 1000 words
- Always contextualized on first use
- Mix of industry terms (RAG, DORA, VSM) and plain language explanations
- Pattern: "Term (explanation)" or "Term: explanation"

**Avoided Terms**:
- Flowery language, poeticism
- Unnecessary adverbs ("very," "really," "quite")
- Corporate buzzwords used ironically if at all

### Rhetorical Patterns

**Question Frequency**: ~3-4 per 1000 words
- **Rhetorical Questions** (70%): Frame sections, challenge assumptions
  - "Where Does the Time Actually Go?"
  - "Why AI Coding Tools Do Not Address the Problem"
- **Stakeholder Questions** (30%): Voice reader concerns then address
  - "'I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?'"

**Direct Address**:
- **Second Person "You"**: Frequent in prescriptive sections
  - "If your requirements are 'garbage in,' AI will simply give you 'garbage out'"
  - "You can buy the most advanced AI tools on the market, but..."
- **First Person "We"**: Occasional, inclusive expert stance
  - "we must first understand," "we are working with"
- **First Person "I"**: Rare, only in "Know Your Constraint" opening
  - "My client stakeholders regularly ask..."

**List/Enumeration Style**:
- **Bulleted Lists**: Dominant format (80% of lists)
- **Numbered Lists**: Used for sequential processes or ranked priorities
- **Inline Lists**: Rare, only for 2-3 short items

**Emphasis Techniques**:
- **Bold Headers**: Heavy use for section navigation
- **Italics**: Minimal, only for genuine emphasis or terminology
- **Quotes**: For source material, stakeholder voices, and key phrases
- **Em-Dashes**: Rare (~2 per 1000 words)—used for clarifying asides
- **Colons**: Frequent (~8-10 per 1000 words) for list introduction

### Stylistic Signatures

#### Opening Strategies

**Pattern 1: Stakeholder Question Hook** (seen in "Know Your Constraint")
> "My client stakeholders regularly ask a question that goes something like this: 'I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?'"

Immediately grounds in real-world scenario, voices reader concern, sets up article as answer.

**Pattern 2: Direct Thesis Statement** (seen in "Gen AI is Non-Deterministic")
> "Non-determinism in generative AI, especially in large language models (LLMs), offers both opportunities and challenges."

No preamble. States exactly what article will explore in first sentence.

**Pattern 3: Triple-Beat Introduction**
- Statement of claim
- Acknowledgment it's reasonable/understandable
- Pivot to critique or complication

Example:
> "It's a fair question. The demos are impressive. The tools are real. The expectations they create are completely understandable. It's also based on a flawed mental model..."

#### Evidence Integration

**Quantitative Data Pattern**:
- Always attributes source
- Often embeds interpretation within presentation
- Format: "According to [Source], [stat]. [Implication]."

Examples:
> "According to the Atlassian 2025 State of DevEx Survey, developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding."

> "According to Stripe's Developer Coefficient report, developers spend an average of 17.3 hours per week simply dealing with technical debt and 'bad code.' That is nearly 42% of their entire work week spent servicing the past rather than building the future."

**List-Heavy Evidence**:
- Complex points broken into bulleted sub-points
- Never more than 5-6 bullets per list
- Each bullet is a complete thought (not fragments)

#### Argument Development

**Signposting Headers**:
- Headers are declarative claims, not generic labels
- Examples: "Challenges of Non-Determinism in LLMs" vs. just "Challenges"
- "Why AI Coding Tools Do Not Address the Problem" vs. just "AI Tools"

**Structural Pattern**:
1. State the landscape/challenge
2. Enumerate the problems with evidence
3. Acknowledge benefits/nuance
4. Provide solutions/techniques
5. Emphasize cultural/mindset shift

**Counterargument Handling**:
- Dedicates full sections to objections
- Pattern: "Despite these [challenges/concerns]..." or "While [X is true]..."
- Never dismissive—engages seriously with opposing view

#### Closing Strategies

**Synthesis Pattern** (both articles):
- Explicitly uses "In conclusion" or "Ultimately"
- Restates core tension/balance
- Points to broader implications
- Ends with actionable insight or call to diagnostic thinking

Example from "Gen AI":
> "Ultimately, the key to successfully integrating non-deterministic AI lies in balancing flexibility with consistency, creativity with reliability, and innovation with robust management techniques."

Example from "Know Your Constraint":
> "Ask: Where does our delivery time actually go? Map it. Use the metrics. Then decide where AI belongs."

### Tonal Patterns

**Formality**: Professional but accessible (7/10 on formality scale)
- Academic rigor without academic stuffiness
- Technical precision without excluding informed laypeople

**Confidence Markers**:
- Definitive claims backed by data: "The bottleneck isn't X; it's Y"
- Acknowledges complexity: "While X is true, Y is also important"
- Prescriptive when warranted: "You must," "Organizations need to"

**Skepticism/Critique**:
- Moderate use of scare quotes for hype terms: "vibe coding," "quick questions"
- Direct challenge to conventional wisdom
- Data-driven skepticism, not cynicism

**Humor/Irony**: Minimal but present
- Dry observations: "That's why most teams only do it once every three years"
- Vivid metaphors with slight edge: "garbage in, garbage out at higher velocity"

## Recommendations for Skill Update

### Additions

**1. Opening Strategy: The Triple-Beat Introduction**
Pattern: State claim → Acknowledge it's reasonable → Pivot to complication
Provides fairness before critique, disarms defensiveness.

**2. Data Citation Format**
Consistent pattern: "According to [Source Year], [precise stat]. [Immediate interpretation/implication]."
Never orphans data without context.

**3. Colon Usage for List Introduction**
Frequency: ~8-10 per 1000 words
Purpose: Signal enumeration, create expectation of elaboration
Format: Complete independent clause + colon + bulleted list

**4. Section Headers as Argument Claims**
Not generic labels ("Background") but declarative statements ("Why AI Coding Tools Do Not Address the Problem")
Headers do rhetorical work, not just organizational work

**5. Strategic Ultra-Short Sentences**
Pattern: Long complex sentence(s) establishing context → Ultra-short declarative for punch
Example pattern: [35 words] + [4 words] creates rhythm and emphasis

### Refinements

**Current Skill Says**: "Skeptical Inquiry: Question assumptions, probe deeper"
**Refine To**: "Data-Driven Skepticism: Challenge conventional wisdom with metrics and research. Uses scare quotes sparingly for hype terms. Acknowledges reasonableness of opposing view before critiquing."

**Current Skill Says**: "Limited Jargon: Technical terms only when necessary; explain when used"
**Refine To**: "Selective Technical Precision: ~15-20 technical terms per 1000 words. Always contextualized on first use using pattern: 'Term (explanation)' or 'Term: definition'. Mix industry acronyms (RAG, DORA, VSM) with plain language."

**Current Skill Says**: "Transition Economy: Use transitions sparingly but effectively"
**Refine To**: "Minimal Explicit Transitions: Rarely uses 'However,' 'Moreover,' 'Furthermore.' Achieves flow through content repetition, pronouns, and logical progression. Section headers handle macro-transitions."

### Corrections

**Current Skill Says**: "Strategic Fragments: Occasional sentence fragments for emphasis. Like this."
**Reality**: Actually uses complete ultra-short sentences (3-7 words) for emphasis more than true fragments. Fragments are very rare.
**Update To**: "Strategic Ultra-Short Sentences: Uses complete but very short sentences (3-7 words) after longer complex ones for emphasis and rhythm. True fragments are rare."

**Current Skill Says**: "Anglo-Saxon Base: Prefer simple, direct words over Latinate alternatives"
**Reality**: Mix is more nuanced—uses precise Latinate terms when they're the exact word ("probabilistic," "deterministic," "consolidation") but avoids pretentious vocabulary.
**Update To**: "Precision Over Simplicity: Choose the exact word, whether Anglo-Saxon or Latinate. 'Probabilistic' over 'chance-based' because it's more precise. Avoid pretentious vocabulary but don't dumb down technical concepts."

## Variation Notes

### Topic-Dependent Patterns

**Technical Deep-Dives** ("Gen AI is Non-Deterministic"):
- More definition-heavy in early sections
- Higher ratio of bulleted technique lists
- More hedging around emerging tech ("shows promise," "represents a step forward")

**Business/Process Critique** ("Know Your Constraint"):
- Opens with stakeholder voice/scenario
- Heavier use of rhetorical questions as section headers
- More prescriptive/diagnostic tone
- Vivid metaphors (pipe/bucket analogy)

### Consistent Across Topics

- Data citation patterns (identical across both)
- List-heavy structure
- Section header style
- Closing synthesis pattern
- Direct address in prescriptive sections

### Sample Size Note

With only 2 articles (~4,900 words), these patterns should be considered **emerging** rather than **fully established**. Recommend re-running Stylist after 3-5 more articles to confirm patterns or identify evolution.

4. **Metaphor/Analogy Usage**: Pipe/bucket, garbage in/out
