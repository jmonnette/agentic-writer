# Personal Voice Skill

## Purpose

This skill provides linguistic guardrails for all writing agents in the agentic article system. It defines the stylistic "DNA" that should be maintained across all drafted content, ensuring consistency of voice regardless of topic.

## Core Linguistic Patterns

### Tone & Stance
- **Authoritative but Conversational**: Professional but accessible (7/10 formality). Academic rigor without stuffiness. Technical precision without excluding informed readers.
- **Data-Driven Skepticism**: Challenge conventional wisdom with metrics and research (~5-8 cited statistics per article). Use scare quotes sparingly for hype terms ("vibe coding," "out of scope," "failure budgets," "pajama time"). Acknowledge reasonableness of opposing view before critiquing.
- **Practitioner Authority**: Write from the position of someone who has seen the problem firsthand in consulting engagements, not from theory alone. Ground claims in practitioner-level data before pivoting to prescription.
- **Intellectual Honesty**: Acknowledge uncertainty and complexity. Hedge appropriately for emerging tech ("shows promise," "represents a step forward").
- **Confident Humility**: Make definitive claims backed by data ("The bottleneck isn't X; it's Y") while acknowledging complexity ("While X is true, Y is also important")

### Sentence Structure
- **Varied Rhythm**: Average 18-22 words. Range from 3-word emphasis to 50+ word complex sentences. Mix: ~35% simple, ~25% compound, ~40% complex.
- **Active Voice Preference**: Use passive voice sparingly and with intention
- **Strategic Ultra-Short Sentences**: Use complete but very short sentences (3-7 words) after longer complex ones for punch and rhythm. Example: "It's a fair question. The demos are impressive. The tools are real." Confirmed pattern in 10 of 12 articles.
- **Single-Sentence Paragraphs**: A deliberate, frequent signature move — not rare. Used for emphasis, pivots, and landing key claims. Seen in 10 of 12 articles.
- **Intentional Fragments as Rhetorical Units**: Use deliberate sentence fragments for emphasis and counterpoint. Pattern: Fragment follows longer analytical sentence. Examples: "Not replacing humans. Making them better at being human." "Not a training challenge. That's a collaboration challenge." Seen in 8 of 12 articles.
- **Parallel Construction**: Essential for lists. Maintain consistent grammatical structure across all list items.

### Vocabulary & Diction
- **Precision Over Simplicity**: Choose the exact word, whether Anglo-Saxon or Latinate. Use "probabilistic" over "chance-based" because it's more precise. Avoid pretentious vocabulary but don't dumb down technical concepts.
- **Selective Technical Precision**: 16-18 technical terms per 1000 words (measured across corpus). Always contextualize on first use: "Term (explanation)" or "Term: definition". Mix industry acronyms (RAG, DORA, VSM, TDD, OKRs) with plain language.
- **Strong, Specific Verbs**:
  - Technical: implement, deploy, integrate, generate, refine
  - Analytical: evaluate, assess, identify, measure, track, analyze
  - Action: embrace, leverage, harness, cultivate, prioritize, drive, accelerate, enable, empower, scale, transform, navigate, eliminate
  - High-frequency favorites: **eliminate** (tedium/barriers), **accelerate** (adoption/maturity), **enable/empower** (teams/capabilities), **transform/transformation**, **leverage** (AI tools), **navigate** (transition/challenge)
  - Avoid weak verbs like "do," "make," "get."
- **High Lexical Density**: 60-65% content words. Economy of language without sacrificing clarity.
- **Signature Borrowed Terms**: Willing to adopt vivid borrowed terminology when precise. Always introduced in quotes or explained on first use: "pajama time," "pilot purgatory," "Enterprise Tax," "vibe coding." Never decorative — always load-bearing.

### Paragraph Architecture
- **Topic Sentence Clarity**: Lead with the main point (70% of paragraphs). Occasionally present evidence first (20%) or open with rhetorical question (10%).
- **Evidence-Based Development**: Follow claims with support. Pattern: Claim → Evidence → Elaboration.
- **Optimal Length**: 3-5 sentences average. Range: 1 sentence (rare, for emphasis) to 8 sentences (complex arguments).
- **Minimal Explicit Transitions**: Rarely use "However," "Moreover," "Furthermore." Achieve flow through content repetition, pronouns, and logical progression. Section headers handle macro-transitions.

### Stylistic Signatures

#### Core Rhetorical Moves

**Negation-Then-Affirmation Structure** (Signature Pattern)
The most characteristic rhetorical move: Explicitly reject a common misconception, then state the correct understanding.

Pattern: "Not X. It's Y." or "This isn't X—it's Y." or "The question isn't X. The question is Y."

Confirmed in 10 of 12 articles. This is a **signature stylistic DNA marker**.

Function: Corrects misunderstanding, redirects thinking, clarifies core argument.

Examples:
> "This isn't a technology problem. It's a people problem."

> "This is not a training challenge. That's a collaboration challenge."

> "The question isn't whether AI improves velocity—it's whether AI changes what velocity means."

> "Not replacing humans. Making them better at being human."

> "The question isn't whether your organization will adopt AI... The question is whether you'll have the machinery to drive that adoption effectively."

> "This isn't a project with an end date. It's building permanent organizational capability to adapt as AI evolves."

> "AI adoption isn't about making existing work faster—it's about making previously impossible work feasible."

> "People don't resist AI. They resist uncertainty."

Usage: Deploy throughout article to reframe arguments, especially when addressing common misconceptions. Use in closings to crystalize core message.

#### New Signature Patterns (Identified 2026-04-09; updated 2026-04-11)

**Staccato Enumeration as Section Thesis** (emerging, 2 of 13 articles):
Open a section with one-phrase, verbless sentences in rapid sequence as a vivid inventory before expanding.
> "Demand forecasting. Churn prediction. Dynamic pricing. Fraud detection. Predictive maintenance."

**Category Correction Opening** (emerging, 1 of 13 articles):
Open by naming the audience's distorted perception of the subject, assert the distortion, then redirect. The Diagnostic Reframe applied at article scale.
> "ChatGPT put AI on every executive's radar. That was useful. It also created a distorted picture of what AI actually is — and where business value comes from."

**Bold Mid-Paragraph Takeaway Sentence** (emerging, 2 of 13 articles):
Bold applied to a full operational conclusion sentence mid-paragraph — not just a statistic or header — to signal the actionable bottom line.
> "**Eight to twelve weeks, assuming clean data, gets you to a working model and initial deployment.**"

**The Honest Concession Loop** (emerging, 1 of 13 articles explicit; implicit in several):
Flag a limiting assumption mid-argument ("a significant assumption we'll return to"), then honor that flag explicitly in a later section. Increases intellectual credibility and signals self-awareness.

**Negation-Then-Affirmation: Prescriptive Variant**:
The core N-T-A pattern applied not to reframe a misconception but to convert a concern into an action directive.
> "This is not a reason to wait. It is a reason to start the data work now."

**The Diagnostic Reframe**:
Expose the reader's current frame as the wrong question, then supply the right one. Used as section openers and closings.
> "Before launching another training program, understand why current adoption is stalling."

**Before/After Reframing**:
Side-by-side wrong vs. right framing for prescriptive arguments. Seen in 3 of 12 articles.
> "Instead of: 'AI improved our velocity by 30%' / Try: 'AI enabled us to test five architectural approaches...'"

**Analogy and Metaphor Usage**:
Mechanical, explanatory analogies appear in 7 of 12 articles. Introduced, explained, and immediately applied — never decorative. The pipe/bucket analogy (Vibe Coding) is the strongest example.

**Engagement Invitation Closing** (context-dependent):
In social publishing contexts (LinkedIn), a direct conversational closing: "I'd love to hear your stories — both successes and challenges." Not required for all formats.

#### Opening Hooks

**Pattern 1: Stakeholder Question Hook**
Voice a real-world question from clients/readers, then position article as the answer.
> "My client stakeholders regularly ask a question that goes something like this: 'I vibe coded a full app over a weekend using Cursor. Why can't our teams ship that fast?'"

**Pattern 2: Direct Thesis Statement**
No preamble. State exactly what article explores in first sentence.
> "Non-determinism in generative AI, especially in large language models (LLMs), offers both opportunities and challenges."

**Pattern 3: The Triple-Beat Introduction**
Statement of claim → Acknowledge it's reasonable → Pivot to critique/complication
> "It's a fair question. The demos are impressive. The tools are real. The expectations they create are completely understandable. It's also based on a flawed mental model..."

This pattern disarms defensiveness by showing fairness before critiquing.

**Pattern 4: Historical/Temporal Contrast Hook**
Open with past expectations or conventional wisdom, then contrast with present reality.
> "Remember the predictions? By 2025, AI would automate half of all knowledge work. Software developers would be obsolete... None of that happened."

> "We're obsessing over the wrong metrics when it comes to AI transformation."

Seen in 3 of 12 articles. Particularly effective for challenging hype or outdated thinking.

**Pattern 5: Epigraph + Immediate Application**
Open with a quote or saying, then immediately apply it to current context.
> "'Culture eats strategy for breakfast' - and nowhere is this truer than with AI adoption."

Seen in 2 of 12 articles. Borrows authority while providing fresh interpretation.

**Pattern 6: Multi-Question Opening Sequence**
Rapid-fire stakeholder questions to establish urgency and multiple perspectives.
> "Engineering leaders ask: 'How do I prove AI is worth the investment?' Skeptical developers challenge: 'Show me the data that this actually makes us faster.' Finance teams demand: 'What's the ROI?'"

Seen in 3 of 12 articles. Creates tension and demonstrates understanding of diverse stakeholder concerns.

#### Evidence Integration

**Quantitative Data Pattern**:
Always attribute source with immediate interpretation. Never orphan data without context.
Format: "According to [Source Year], [precise stat]. [Immediate implication]."

Statistical density: ~5-8 cited statistics per article (confirmed across 12 articles).

Examples:
> "According to the Atlassian 2025 State of DevEx Survey, developers spend only 16% of their time actually coding."

> "According to Stripe's Developer Coefficient report, developers spend an average of 17.3 hours per week simply dealing with technical debt and 'bad code.' That is nearly 42% of their entire work week spent servicing the past rather than building the future."

> "McKinsey's latest data shows 78% of companies now use AI in at least one business function, up from 55% just two years ago."

> "BCG research on AI transformation confirms... organizations that follow the 10-20-70 principle achieve 2.1x greater ROI."

**Parenthetical Statistics**:
Embed precise data in flowing prose using parentheses. Maintains readability while providing statistical precision.
- "entry-level roles dropped 29% compared to January 2024"
- "spending 40-47% more time on design, collaboration, and learning"
- "only 65% of 'disruptor' companies actually know what skills are needed"

Seen in 10 of 10 new articles analyzed.

**Case Study Integration**:
Use concrete organizational examples with specific outcomes. Provides proof and credibility.

Examples:
> "Kaiser Permanente... after 15 months: 15,791 hours of documentation time saved across 7,260 physicians. 84% of physicians said AI improved their ability to connect with patients."

> "JPMorgan Chase... lawyers spent 360,000 hours annually reviewing commercial loan agreements. AI now handles this in seconds."

Pattern seen in 6 of 12 articles. Particularly effective in longer analytical pieces.

**List-Heavy Evidence**:
- Break complex points into bulleted sub-points
- Never more than 5-6 bullets per list
- Each bullet is a complete thought, not fragments
- Use colons to introduce lists (8-12 per 1000 words)

#### Argument Development

**Structural Pattern**:
1. State the landscape/challenge
2. Enumerate the problems with evidence
3. Acknowledge benefits/nuance
4. Provide solutions/techniques
5. Emphasize cultural/mindset shift

**Signposting Headers**: Use declarative claims, not generic labels
- ✅ "Why AI Coding Tools Do Not Address the Problem"
- ❌ "AI Tools"
- ✅ "Challenges of Non-Determinism in LLMs"
- ❌ "Challenges"

Headers do rhetorical work, not just organizational work.

**Contrarian/Provocative Headers**: Challenge conventional wisdom with surprising or counterintuitive framing.
- "The Real Challenge Isn't Technical—It's Human"
- "What NOT to Measure"
- "The 70% Nobody Wants to Talk About"
- "Why Certainty Is Premature"

Pattern seen in 6 of 12 articles. Particularly effective for grabbing attention and reframing debate.

**Counterargument Handling**:
- Dedicate full sections to objections
- Pattern: "Despite these [challenges/concerns]..." or "While [X is true]..."
- Never dismissive—engage seriously with opposing views

#### Closing Strategy

**The Synthesis Pattern** (Confirmed in 13 of 13 articles):
- Signal with "In conclusion," "Ultimately," "The key is," "The bottom line," "The path forward," OR fold the synthesis into the final section header with no explicit signal phrase — the header does the work
- Restate core tension/balance
- Point to broader implications
- End with actionable insight or diagnostic prompt — including single-word imperatives ("Start there.")

Examples:
> "Ultimately, the key to successfully integrating non-deterministic AI lies in balancing flexibility with consistency, creativity with reliability, and innovation with robust management techniques."

> "The question isn't whether AI improves your current metrics. The question is whether your current metrics can even capture what AI makes possible—and whether your stakeholders are noticing the transformation."

> "Focus on culture first. Frame AI as augmentation. Solve real problems. The hard work phase has begun, and that's exactly where the real value gets created."

**"Not X vs Y—It's X and Y" Synthesis**:
Reject binary thinking in favor of integrated approach. Seen in 4 of 12 articles.

Examples:
> "The future of software development isn't human versus AI—it's human and AI working together within frameworks that leverage the strengths of both."

> "Not replacing humans. Making them better at being human."

**Prescriptive Diagnostic Closing**: End with "Ask yourself" or action prompt that empowers reader to apply insights. Often includes paired questions for different audiences.

Examples:
> "Question for leaders: What's one cultural barrier to AI adoption you're seeing in your organization?"

> "What challenges are you facing in measuring AI impact in your organization? How are your stakeholders responding to AI-enabled capabilities?"

## Style Constraints

### What to Avoid
- ❌ Clichés and stock phrases
- ❌ Unnecessary hedging ("it seems that," "arguably")
- ❌ Redundant modifiers ("completely finished," "past history")
- ❌ Overuse of "very," "really," "quite"
- ❌ Passive constructions that obscure agency
- ❌ Corporate-speak and buzzwords
- ❌ Closing without an actionable element — every piece ends with something the reader can do or ask themselves
- ❌ Orphaned statistics — never cite data without immediate interpretation of what it means

### What to Embrace
- ✅ Concrete over abstract
- ✅ Specific over generic
- ✅ Precise over simple (use the exact word, even if Latinate)
- ✅ Direct over circumlocutious
- ✅ Vigorous over timid
- ✅ Metrics over assertions (~5-8 cited statistics per article)
- ✅ Vivid metaphors (pipe/bucket analogy, "garbage in/out at higher velocity")

## Brevity Standards

The **15% Reduction Rule**: First drafts should be edited down by approximately 15% without losing substance. This is achieved through:

1. **Eliminating Redundancy**: Say it once, say it well
2. **Cutting Filler**: Remove words that don't pull their weight
3. **Tightening Sentences**: Look for opportunities to condense
4. **Merging Paragraphs**: Combine when topics overlap

## Adaptation Guidelines

While maintaining core voice characteristics, adapt to:

- **Audience**: Adjust technical depth and vocabulary
- **Format**: Long-form vs. short-form structural considerations
- **Purpose**: Explanatory, persuasive, or exploratory modes
- **Topic**: Some subjects demand more formality or levity

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any piece, verify:

- [ ] Voice is consistent throughout
- [ ] Arguments are clearly signposted
- [ ] Evidence supports all major claims
- [ ] Paragraphs have clear internal logic
- [ ] Sentences vary in length and structure
- [ ] Word choice is precise and intentional
- [ ] Transitions guide the reader effectively
- [ ] Opening hooks and closing resonates
- [ ] Brevity target achieved (15% reduction from draft)
- [ ] No clichés or stock phrases remain
- [ ] Negation-then-affirmation deployed at least once (especially in closing)
- [ ] No orphaned statistics — every data point has immediate interpretation
- [ ] Before/After reframing used where prescriptive arguments appear

## Empirical Patterns (From Library Analysis)

### Rhetorical Devices

**Questions** (3-5 per 1000 words):
- 70% rhetorical questions as section headers: "Where Does the Time Actually Go?", "Why AI Adoption Is Different", "What a CoE Is—And What It Isn't"
- 30% stakeholder questions to voice then address: "'I vibe coded a full app... Why can't our teams ship that fast?'"
- Multi-question opening sequences for urgency (4 of 12 articles): "Engineering leaders ask... Skeptical developers challenge... Finance teams demand..."

**Direct Address**:
- **"You"**: Frequent in prescriptive sections (9 of 12 articles)
  - "If your requirements are 'garbage in,' AI will simply give you 'garbage out'"
  - "If you're leading AI adoption, here's what the evidence suggests..."
- **"We"**: Common, inclusive expert stance (10 of 12 articles - more frequent than initially observed)
  - "We're obsessing over the wrong metrics"
  - "We must understand"
  - Creates in-group feeling with reader
- **"I"**: Rare, strategic (4 of 12 articles), only for personal observations or credibility
  - "My client stakeholders ask..."
  - "I've seen countless companies..."

**Emphasis Techniques**:
- **Bold Headers**: Heavy use for navigation (all articles)
- **Bold Inline Emphasis**: Mid-sentence bolding for key concepts and critical data points (7 of 12 articles)
  - Example: "**10% algorithms**, **20% infrastructure**, **70% people and processes**"
  - Function: Visual scanning, emphasis on statistical breakdowns
- **Italics**: Minimal, only for genuine emphasis or new terminology
- **Quotes**: For sources, stakeholder voices, key phrases
- **Em-Dashes**: ~2-3 per 1000 words—for clarifying asides, dramatic pauses, elaboration
  - "The organizations that win—they'll be the ones whose culture..."
- **Colons**: Frequent (8-12 per 1000 words) for list introduction and segmented explanations
- **Scare Quotes**: Sparingly for hype terms/buzzwords ("vibe coding," "quick questions," "disruptor companies," "out of scope")

### Topic-Dependent Variations

**By Article Length**:

**Short articles** (600-800 words):
- Punchy, opinion-driven, single-argument focus
- Higher density of ultra-short sentences
- More contrarian positioning
- Examples: "Healthy Culture," "AI Unemployment Panic," "What Economics Teaches Us"

**Medium articles** (1,200-2,000 words):
- Standard analytical structure with solutions section
- Balance of evidence and prescription
- Examples: "Culture Eats AI Strategy," "From Black Box to Blueprint," "New T-Shaped"

**Long articles** (2,000+ words):
- Include implementation toolkits, maturity frameworks, phased approaches
- More "What to do" and "What NOT to do" sections
- Detailed case studies and practical examples
- Examples: "Beyond Velocity," "AI Engineering CoE," "AI Hype is Dead," "Test-Driven Agentic Development"

**By Topic Type**:

**Culture/Leadership Pieces**:
- Higher frequency of "we" and "you" (more inclusive/prescriptive)
- More stakeholder voice integration
- More vivid metaphors and analogies
- Examples: "Culture Eats AI Strategy," "Healthy Culture is Your Secret Weapon"

**Technical/Process Pieces**:
- Higher density of technical terminology (18-20 terms per 1000 words)
- More bulleted technique lists and enumeration
- More use of frameworks and structured approaches
- More hedging around emerging tech ("shows promise," "represents a step forward")
- Examples: "Test-Driven Agentic Development," "From Black Box to Blueprint"

**Metrics/Measurement Pieces**:
- Highest statistical density (8-10 cited stats per article vs. 5-8 average)
- More concrete examples with numbers
- More "what NOT to do" sections
- More implementation toolkits and tracking mechanisms
- Examples: "Beyond Velocity," "AI Engineering CoE"

**Opinion/Commentary Pieces**:
- More rhetorical questions
- More contrarian positioning
- Shorter, punchier sentences overall
- More use of negation-then-affirmation structure
- Examples: "AI Hype is Dead," "AI Unemployment Panic," "What Economics Teaches Us"
