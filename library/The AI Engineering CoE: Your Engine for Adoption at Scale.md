The AI Engineering CoE: Your Engine for Adoption at Scale
Jeffrey Monnette

November 30, 2025
The most common question I hear from engineering leaders right now isn't "Should we adopt AI?" That debate is over. The question is: "How do we drive adoption at scale without chaos?"

Most organizations are stuck in a frustrating middle ground. Pockets of engineers are experimenting with AI tools. Some teams see dramatic productivity gains. Others struggle to get started. There's no consistency in practices, no systematic way to share what's working, and leadership has limited visibility into what's actually happening.

The result? Fragmented adoption, duplicated effort, and a growing gap between AI-forward teams and everyone else. Engineering leaders know AI matters but lack the organizational machinery to drive maturity.

This is the problem an AI Engineering Center of Excellence solves.

What a CoE Is—And What It Isn't
Let me be direct about what I'm proposing, because "Center of Excellence" means different things to different people.

An AI Engineering CoE is a small, dedicated team focused on accelerating AI adoption across engineering teams. Their job is research, guidance, enablement, and coaching—not governance, compliance, or tool procurement.

This is not:

A bureaucratic standards body that slows teams down
An ivory tower research lab disconnected from delivery
A governance function that approves or rejects AI usage
A centralized team that "does AI" for other teams

This is:

A hub of expertise that helps engineering teams help themselves
A research function that stays current as AI capabilities evolve
A coaching organization that scales knowledge across the enterprise
A bridge between AI strategy and engineering execution

The distinction matters. Organizations that treat their CoE as a governance body end up with compliance overhead that stifles adoption. Organizations that treat it as an enablement function accelerate maturity across the board.

The Core Purpose: Solving the "How" Problem
Engineering teams face a common set of challenges when adopting AI:

They don't know where to start. Which workflows benefit most from AI assistance? What tools should they use? How do they measure success?

They don't know what good looks like. What does mature AI-assisted development actually look like in practice? What are the patterns and anti-patterns?

They're reinventing the wheel. Teams across the organization are solving the same problems independently, without benefiting from each other's learnings.

They lack time for experimentation. Delivery pressure leaves little room for exploring AI capabilities or developing new skills.

Leadership lacks visibility. Engineering leaders can't assess adoption maturity, identify gaps, or allocate resources effectively.

The CoE exists to address these challenges. Its purpose is simple: help engineering teams and leaders understand how to introduce and utilize AI effectively.

Structure and Roles
The CoE has two distinct functions that operate at different scales:

The Core Team (~5 People)
This is a lean, permanent team focused on research, experimentation, and publishing guidance. They are the organization's dedicated capacity for staying current with rapidly evolving AI capabilities.

Composition: Engineers who wear multiple hats—developing guidance, creating training content, running pilots, and coaching teams. This isn't a team of specialists; it's a team of versatile practitioners who can tackle whatever the organization needs.

Leadership: A director-level leader who remains a hands-on contributor, not just a manager. This person sets direction, coordinates with engineering leadership, and personally contributes to CoE outputs. You need someone who can speak credibly to both executives and engineers—strategic enough to align with organizational priorities, technical enough to earn respect from practitioners.

Key activities:

Evaluating new AI tools and capabilities as they emerge
Developing and maintaining adoption playbooks
Creating knowledge-sharing artifacts (how-to guides, reference implementations, video tutorials)
Establishing and documenting best practices
Running pilots to test new approaches before broader rollout

Who belongs here: Engineers who are enthusiastic about AI, ready to solve challenging problems, have a growth mindset, and can influence their peers. Seniority matters less than these qualities. Someone at mid-career with these traits will outperform a senior engineer who's skeptical or change-resistant.

These are permanent, full-time roles. As AI capabilities continue to evolve rapidly, you need dedicated capacity to stay current. This isn't a problem you solve once—it requires sustained attention.

The Coaching Function (Scales with Org Size)
The core team can't personally coach every engineering team in a large organization. You need a coaching layer that scales.

Key activities:

Regular coaching sessions with engineering teams
Office hours for questions and troubleshooting
Demos of new tools and practices
Hands-on support during initial adoption phases
Gathering feedback and field insights to inform CoE priorities

Scaling model: Coaches start as part of the CoE organization. As AI adoption matures and becomes embedded in standard practice, these coaching roles may migrate into functional organizations (engineering, QA, product). The CoE seeds expertise across the organization, not hoards it.

The CoE-Champions Ecosystem
A CoE alone isn't sufficient. You need a network that extends the CoE's reach into every corner of the organization.

This is where AI Champions come in. Champions are enthusiastic adopters who evangelize and support their peers—change agents embedded in delivery teams. The CoE and Champions network form a collaborative ecosystem:

The CoE develops best practices, publishes guidance, and provides structured training and coaching.

AI Champions apply these practices on real projects, adapt them to local contexts, and surface insights back to the CoE.

This creates a feedback loop: the CoE pushes knowledge out; Champions pull real-world learnings back in. The CoE ensures consistency and quality; Champions ensure relevance and adoption.

This collaboration must be formalized. Both Champions and CoE team members should have explicit expectations for interaction—regular syncs, shared communication channels, structured feedback mechanisms. Without formalization, the connection atrophies.

A key structural distinction: Champions are embedded in the engineering function, while the CoE operates as part of the AI strategy function. Champions live in delivery; the CoE bridges strategy and execution. This separation ensures both perspectives are represented.

I've written more extensively about building effective AI Champions networks and selecting Champions as change agents rather than just technical experts. The key insight is that Champions aren't your best coders—they're your best influencers who happen to be enthusiastic about AI.

Focus Areas: Enablement Over Governance
The CoE's focus should tilt heavily toward enablement and adoption, not governance and standards.

What the CoE should focus on:

Adoption playbooks: Step-by-step guidance for teams at different maturity levels
Best practices: Patterns that work, anti-patterns to avoid, lessons learned
Tool guidance: Evaluations and recommendations (advisory, not mandates)
Training and coaching: Building capability across the organization
Knowledge sharing: Making it easy for teams to learn from each other
Metrics and visibility: Helping leadership understand adoption maturity

What the CoE should avoid owning:

Tool procurement: The CoE should advise on tool selection but shouldn't own vendor relationships for the entire org
Compliance and governance: This belongs in security, legal, or a dedicated AI governance function
Mandatory standards: The CoE publishes best practices and recommendations, not requirements that teams must follow
Centralized AI development: The CoE enables teams; it doesn't do AI work for them

This distinction is crucial. The moment a CoE becomes associated with bureaucracy and approval gates, engineering teams start working around it rather than with it. Enablement builds trust and adoption. Governance builds resistance.

This philosophy aligns with what I've observed repeatedly: AI transformation is fundamentally about cultural change, with technology as an enabler. As I've written before, "culture eats AI strategy for breakfast." A CoE built around enablement reinforces a culture of learning and experimentation. A CoE built around governance reinforces a culture of compliance and fear.

Governance and Demonstrating Value
Reporting Structure
The CoE sits at the intersection of AI strategy and engineering execution. This requires close partnership between the Chief AI Officer (CAIO) and Chief Technology Officer (CTO)—or their equivalents in your organization.

The CAIO function owns AI strategy: where the organization is headed with AI, investment priorities, and transformation goals.
The CTO function owns engineering: how teams work, technical standards, and delivery execution.

The CoE needs both perspectives to succeed. It must align with strategic AI priorities and integrate with how engineering actually operates.

Where the CoE formally reports—to the CAIO, to the CTO, or through a joint arrangement—depends on your organization's structure and politics. What matters more than the org chart is that CAIO and CTO operate as genuine partners with shared accountability for AI engineering adoption. If these leaders aren't aligned, the CoE will be caught in the middle, and adoption will suffer regardless of reporting lines.

A Maturity Model for AI Engineering CoEs
Not every organization needs a fully developed CoE immediately. Here's how to think about maturity stages:

Stage 1: Foundation
Characteristics:

AI adoption is ad hoc and inconsistent (most teams at L0-L1)
No centralized expertise or guidance
Leadership has limited visibility into what's happening

CoE focus:

Establish the core team (even 2-3 people initially)
Assess current state of adoption across the organization
Identify early adopters who can become Champions
Create initial guidance artifacts for common use cases

Stage 2: Structured Enablement
Characteristics:

Adoption is growing but uneven
Some teams reaching L2, others still at L0-L1
Best practices exist in pockets but aren't shared systematically

CoE focus:

Formalize the Champions network
Develop comprehensive adoption playbooks
Establish regular coaching cadence
Implement adoption metrics and reporting
Create feedback loops between CoE and Champions

Stage 3: Scaled Adoption
Characteristics:

AI tools are in active use across most engineering teams
Majority of teams at L2, early adopters reaching L3
Leadership has visibility into maturity and gaps

CoE focus:

Scale coaching function to reach all teams
Develop advanced guidance for mature teams
Focus on optimization and efficiency gains
Begin migrating coaching capability into functional orgs
Shift research focus to emerging capabilities (agents, autonomous systems)

Stage 4: Embedded Excellence
Characteristics:

AI-assisted development is the default way of working
Most teams at L2-L3, AI-native practices becoming standard
Continuous improvement is self-sustaining

CoE focus:

Maintain research function to stay current with AI evolution
Focus on next-generation capabilities and practices
Provide specialized support for advanced use cases
Serve as innovation lab for new approaches
Champion network operates semi-autonomously

The journey through these stages typically takes 18-36 months, depending on organizational size, starting maturity, and investment level.

Getting Started
If you're an engineering leader considering a CoE, here's where to begin:

Assess your current state. Where is adoption happening? What's working? What questions are teams asking that aren't getting answered? Map your teams against the L0-L3 maturity framework.

Identify your first hires carefully. The initial CoE team sets the tone. Find engineers who are enthusiastic, influential, and oriented toward helping others succeed—not gatekeeping. Start with a strong director-level leader who can build the team and establish credibility.

Start with quick wins. Don't try to boil the ocean. Pick one or two high-value areas where guidance would help teams immediately, and deliver value fast.

Build the Champions network early. You can't scale without Champions. Identify early adopters who can extend the CoE's reach while you build the core team.

Establish the CAIO-CTO partnership. Misalignment between AI strategy and engineering execution will cripple the CoE. Get these leaders aligned before you start.

Measure from day one. You'll need to demonstrate value. Start tracking maturity levels immediately so you have a baseline to show progress against.

Don't neglect culture. A CoE can publish the best playbooks in the world, but if your organization's culture doesn't support experimentation, learning, and change, adoption will stall. Building psychological safety for AI experimentation is just as important as building technical guidance.

The Strategic Imperative
AI is transforming software engineering faster than most organizations can adapt. The teams that figure out how to drive adoption systematically will pull ahead. The teams that leave adoption to chance will fall behind.

A well-designed CoE is organizational infrastructure for AI transformation. It's how you ensure that AI adoption happens deliberately, consistently, and at scale—not randomly, in fragments, and only where you happen to have enthusiastic individuals.

The question isn't whether your organization will adopt AI in engineering. The question is whether you'll have the machinery to drive that adoption effectively.
