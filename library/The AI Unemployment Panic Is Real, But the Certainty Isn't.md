The AI Unemployment Panic Is Real, But the Certainty Isn't
Jeffrey Monnette

February 1, 2026
A recent opinion piece in The Hill declares that the US is "headed for mass unemployment, and no one is prepared." The author, John Mac Ghlionn, argues that Universal Basic Income (UBI) is now an inevitable "stability mechanism" to prevent social collapse as AI eliminates entry-level roles. He presents this as a fait accompli—a done deal. I’m not buying it. Not because the concerns are unfounded—the data is sobering—but because the certainty of this doomsday narrative exceeds what the evidence supports. Furthermore, the policy prescription of UBI as the primary solution is exactly backward.

What the Data Actually Shows

The disruption is real. Geoffrey Hinton, a pioneer of modern AI, has accurately warned that AI capability is doubling roughly every seven months in terms of the temporal horizon of tasks it can complete autonomously. He predicts AI will manage month-long software projects within a few years.

Labor market data supports this: Randstad’s analysis of 126 million job postings shows entry-level roles dropped 29% compared to January 2024. Entry-level computer science graduate unemployment has risen to 6.1-7.5%. This is not a cyclical downturn; it is a structural "missing middle" where the economy grows while entry-level white-collar hiring stalls.

Why Certainty Is Premature

We see displacement clearly because it is happening now, but we don't yet see the replacement because those jobs are only beginning to emerge. History shows that technological transitions take time. During the Industrial Revolution, the "Engels' Pause" saw productivity soar while real wages stagnated for nearly three generations before living standards rose. Similarly, it took 15 years for the PC to evolve from a niche tool into a driver of roles like "Web Designer"—a job that would have seemed absurd in 1982.

While the "seven-month doubling rate" suggests this transition is happening faster, that does not mean we know exactly how it ends. The World Economic Forum still projects that AI will create roughly 170 million new roles by 2030, compared to 92 million displaced—a net increase of 78 million jobs. We are already seeing the rise of AI Orchestrators and Ethics Auditors to manage these systems, alongside a surge in "New Collar" infrastructure roles and high-empathy healthcare professions that machines cannot replicate.

Moving Beyond the "Fait Accompli"

To navigate this, we must shift our policy focus from subsidizing idleness to incentivizing human contribution. This requires two critical shifts: Tax Reform and Educational Modernization.

Currently, our tax code perversely rewards automation while penalizing employment. Companies receive significant tax breaks and depreciation credits for investing in AI servers and hardware, yet they are hit with heavy payroll taxes for every human they hire. We must move toward "Human Capital Parity," reforming the code so that a dollar spent on training and retaining a human worker receives the same tax favor as a dollar spent on an AI algorithm. By leveling the financial playing field, we encourage businesses to reskill their junior staff rather than simply replacing them with an API.

Simultaneously, we must hasten the move away from the "one-size-fits-all" college paradigm. This starts with modernizing the Perkins Act to integrate Industry-Recognized Credentials into high school and promoting skills-based hiring in both the public and private sectors. By normalizing apprenticeships and "stackable" credentials, we create on-ramps for the 22-year-olds currently stranded by the "missing middle."

A Targeted Safety Net

This is not to say that Universal Basic Income is entirely without merit. As a "stability mechanism," it provides a floor that prevents total destitution and gives workers the "freedom to say no" to exploitative conditions. In a moment of hurricane-force technological change, the simplicity of a direct check can provide immediate relief that a slow-moving bureaucracy cannot.

However, if we need a universal safety net, a Job Guarantee remains the superior long-term option. By acting as the "employer of last resort" in non-automatable sectors like elder care or community infrastructure, the government can maintain social discipline and meaning while providing income support. While more administratively demanding than a simple cash transfer, this approach anchors financial support in human dignity and individual agency, ensuring every person maintains a vital social connection through a meaningful contribution.

If we accept the "fait accompli" framing and implement UBI as our only solution, we risk creating a society where a large portion of the population is effectively sidelined from the shared work of our community. That is not a floor; it is a ceiling. It tells young people the system has given up on finding productive roles for them. We have a choice: we can subsidize a retreat from the workforce, or we can build the tax, educational, and labor bridges that allow every citizen to participate in the future AI is building.
